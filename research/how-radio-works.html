How Radio Works

March 6, 2026

You speak into a box. Somewhere far away, someone hears you. No wires. No physical connection. Just invisible waves rippling through the air. It sounds like magic. It's not — but it's close.

The Wave That Started It All

In 1865, James Clerk Maxwell wrote down equations that changed everything. He proved mathematically that electric and magnetic fields could travel together as waves — moving at the speed of light. Light itself, he realized, was just such a wave.

Maxwell died in 1879, never knowing how right he was. In 1887, Heinrich Hertz proved these "electromagnetic waves" were real — using a spark gap transmitter and a receiver twenty meters away. The first radio transmission was a single spark. It carried no information beyond "I exist." But it proved the impossible.

What Is a Radio Wave?

A radio wave is an electromagnetic wave — a self-propagating disturbance in the fabric of spacetime. It has two parts: an electric field and a magnetic field, perpendicular to each other, both perpendicular to the direction of travel.

Unlike sound waves (which need air or water), radio waves travel through vacuum. They travel at light speed. They can pass through walls. They're everywhere — the universe is flooded with them.

Frequency and Wavelength

A radio wave is defined by two things:

  • Frequency (f) — how many cycles per second. Measured in Hertz (Hz). Your FM station at 101.1 MHz oscillates 101,100,000 times per second.
  • Wavelength (λ) — the distance between peaks. Related by the simple equation: λ = c / f (where c is the speed of light).

Higher frequency means shorter wavelength. AM radio (530–1700 kHz) has wavelengths of hundreds of meters. WiFi (2.4–5 GHz) has wavelengths of about 6 centimeters. The frequency band you use determines what the wave can do — penetrate walls, travel long distances, or carry lots of data.

The Three Big Problems

Maxwell gave us waves. But turning those waves into useful communication required solving three problems:

1. Generation — Making the Wave

You can't just shout into the electromagnetic field. You need to create an oscillating current — electrons moving back and forth in a conductor. The simplest way: a resonant circuit (inductor + capacitor) that stores and releases energy at a natural frequency. Tune it, and you get a clean sine wave at a specific frequency.

Early radios used spark gap transmitters — violent electrical sparks that radiated across a wide frequency band. It worked, but it was chaotic. Modern transmitters use oscillators — crystals or circuits that produce pure, stable frequencies. Precise. Controllable. Boring, in a beautiful way.

2. Modulation — Putting Information On It

A pure sine wave at one frequency carries no information. It's just... there. To send sound, we need to modulate the wave — vary some property in step with the signal.

There are two main ways:

  • Amplitude Modulation (AM) — vary the strength (amplitude) of the wave. Loud signal = strong wave. Quiet signal = weak wave. Simple, but susceptible to static.
  • Frequency Modulation (FM) — vary the frequency slightly. Loud signal = slightly higher frequency. It's immune to amplitude static (like thunder), which is why FM sounds cleaner.

For digital data, you get even more options: on-off keying (Morse code's ancestor), phase shifting, complex QAM constellations carrying multiple bits per symbol. The principle remains: you're carving information onto the carrier wave.

3. Reception — Hearing the Whisper

The hard part. Your antenna catches a tiny fraction of the transmitted power — often less than a billionth of a watt. The signal is buried in noise. The job of a receiver is to find the needle in the electromagnetic haystack.

Tuning uses a resonant circuit (like the transmitter) to select one frequency and reject others. Demodulation reverses the modulation process — extracting the audio from the carrier. Amplification boosts the weak signal to usable levels.

Early receivers used crystal sets — no power required, just a razor blade and a cat's whisker. Modern receivers use superhet architecture, mixing the incoming signal to a lower "intermediate frequency" for easier processing. The math gets hairy. The result is seamless.

The Radio Spectrum — A Finite Resource

Not all frequencies behave the same. The atmosphere filters, reflects, and absorbs different bands differently:

  • Long wave (LW) — follows the Earth's curvature, travels over mountains. Used for international AM broadcasting.
  • Medium wave (MW) — bounces off the ionosphere at night. The classic AM band.
  • Short wave (SW) — bounces between ionosphere and Earth, reaching around the world. Used by international broadcasters, amateur radio, and spies.
  • VHF/UHF — line-of-sight, goes through walls poorly. FM radio, TV, cell phones, WiFi, Bluetooth.

The spectrum is regulated. In most countries, you need a license to transmit on most frequencies. This prevents chaos — but also creates artificial scarcity. The fight over 5G spectrum is essentially a trillion-dollar argument about who gets to use which part of the air.

What Changed Everything

Radio's history is a parade of breakthroughs:

  • 1901 — Marconi sends the first transatlantic signal (the letter "S" from Newfoundland to England)
  • 1906 — Reginald Fessenden broadcasts voice and music for the first time
  • 1920 — KDKA in Pittsburgh broadcasts the first commercial radio station
  • 1947 — The transistor replaces vacuum tubes; radios get smaller, cheaper, more reliable
  • 1960s — FM stereo brings music to cars
  • 1980s — Cell phones begin the transition from circuit-switched voice to digital data
  • Today — Software-defined radio (SDR) can listen to everything, anywhere, with a USB dongle

Why It Still Matters

You carry more radios in your pocket than existed in the entire world in 1920. Your phone has cellular (multiple bands), WiFi, Bluetooth, NFC, and GPS. Each is radio. Each uses different frequencies, protocols, and modulations to solve different problems.

Radio is the invisible infrastructure of the modern world. Cell towers are just fancy radio stations. WiFi is unlicensed radio. Satellite communication is radio with extra steps. The internet — for all its fiber optics and undersea cables — ultimately relies on radio to reach your device.

The Magic Remains

When you press push-to-talk on a walkie-talkie, here's what happens:

  1. Your voice analog signal modulates a carrier wave (FM)
  2. The transmitter amplifies it and sends it out as an electromagnetic wave
  3. The wave travels at light speed in all directions
  4. The receiver's antenna catches a vanishing fraction of that power
  5. Filters isolate your frequency; demodulators extract your voice
  6. A tiny speaker turns the signal back into sound

All in milliseconds. All without wires. Maxell's equations, proven right 160 years ago, still govern every bit of it.

That's not magic. That's physics. But it still feels like magic.
