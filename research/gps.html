How GPS Works

March 4, 2026

Your phone knows where you are within a few meters. It does this by talking to satellites orbiting 20,000km above Earth. The math is elegant, the engineering is absurd, and without Einstein, it wouldn't work at all.

The Core Problem: Where Are You?

Imagine you're lost in a desert. You see a distant mountain and know it's 10km away — but that only puts you on a circle around the mountain. Add a second mountain 8km away, and now you have two circles. Where they intersect is your position. That's trilateration.

GPS does the same thing, except the "mountains" are satellites and the "distance" is travel time of a radio signal.

The Distance Trick

Radio signals travel at light speed (≈300,000 km/s). The satellite transmits a pseudorandom code. Your phone has the same code. When it receives the signal, it compares when it arrived vs when it was sent. That time difference × speed of light = distance.

Problem: satellite clocks use atomic clocks (accurate to nanoseconds). Your phone's clock is off by seconds. That tiny error becomes kilometers of position error.

Solution: you need a fourth satellite. The fourth measurement lets your phone solve for its clock offset along with X, Y, Z position. That's why GPS needs 4 satellites minimum, not 3.

The Orbits

GPS satellites aren't geostationary (they don't stay over one spot). There are about 31 active satellites in 6 orbital planes, each orbiting Earth twice a day at ~20,200km altitude.

The orbits are carefully designed so that at any point on Earth, you can always see at least 4 satellites. Usually you see 6–12.

The Relativistic Correction

This is the part that blows most people's minds: GPS doesn't work without Einstein.

Two effects:

  • Time dilation: satellites move at ~14,000 km/h. Time runs slower for them (relative to us) — about 7 microseconds per day slower.
  • Gravitational time dilation: satellites are further from Earth's mass, so time runs faster for them — about 45 microseconds per day faster.

Net effect: satellite clocks run fast by 38 microseconds per day.

That sounds tiny. But without correcting for it, GPS would drift 11 kilometers per day. After a week, your "accurate" GPS would think you're in the wrong city.

Every GPS satellite transmits its orbital parameters. Your phone's chip applies the Einstein corrections in real-time. The most practical proof of general relativity in everyday life.

Error Sources

Even with Einstein-corrected clocks, errors creep in:

  • Ionospheric delay: charged particles slow radio signals down (can be corrected using dual frequencies)
  • Multipath: signals bounce off buildings before reaching your phone
  • Satellite clock drift: tiny residual errors
  • Geometry: if all visible satellites are in one part of the sky, accuracy drops

Modern GPS: Beyond Location

GPS timestamps enable:

  • Network timing: cell towers sync to GPS for handoff
  • Financial timestamping: stock trades need precise timestamps
  • Power grid sync: grid operators use GPS for phase synchronization

The Takeaway

Every time you check a map, your phone does relativistic physics with orbiting atomic clocks to triangulate your position. The math was worked out by a patent clerk in Bern in 1905 and 1915. The satellites were launched starting in 1978. Today it's in your pocket.

We don't usually think of GPS as applied Einstein. But it's the most visible proof that the physics of the very fast and the very massive matters in daily life.
