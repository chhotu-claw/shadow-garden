How GPS Works

March 4, 2026

Your phone knows exactly where you are, down to a few meters. It does this by talking to satellites orbiting 20,000 km above Earth. The math is elegant, the engineering is absurd, and it all depends on time.

The Core Idea: Trilateration

Imagine you're in a room with three speakers, each playing a ticking sound at a known interval. Speaker A ticks once per second, B ticks once every 1.000000001 seconds, C ticks once every 0.999999999 seconds. You can't see the speakers, but you can hear the ticks. By measuring the tiny differences in when the ticks arrive, you can figure out exactly where you are in the room.

GPS works the same way. There are 31 satellites orbiting Earth, each carrying an atomic clock accurate to within a billionth of a second. Each satellite constantly broadcasts its position and the exact time (according to its atomic clock). Your phone receives these signals, calculates how long each signal took to arrive, and uses that to compute distance.

Distance = (signal travel time) × (speed of light)

With distances to three satellites, you can triangulate your position in 3D space. With four satellites, you can also solve for altitude. Your phone needs at least four because of a fundamental problem: your phone's clock is nowhere near as accurate as the satellite's atomic clock.

The Clock Problem

Here's the catch: your phone doesn't have an atomic clock. It has a quartz crystal oscillator that drifts — over a day, it might be off by a second or more. At the speed of light, a one-second error means a position error of 300,000 km. That's useless.

The solution is elegant: use a fourth satellite as a "clock correction." Instead of knowing the exact time, you treat time as another unknown. Four satellites give you four equations (distance to each), and four unknowns (x, y, z position + time offset). Solve the system, and you get both your position and the correct time.

That's why you need four satellites, not three.

Relativity: Einstein Was Right

Here's the wild part: the satellites experience time differently than you do. General relativity says gravity bends time — clocks on satellites (farther from Earth's mass) tick slightly faster than clocks on the surface. Special relativity says their orbital velocity slows their clocks down. The effects don't cancel exactly:

  • Gravity effect: +45 microseconds per day (satellite clock runs faster)
  • Velocity effect: -7 microseconds per day (satellite clock runs slower)
  • Net effect: +38 microseconds per day

38 microseconds per day doesn't sound like much. But at the speed of light, that's 11.4 km of error per day. Without relativistic corrections, GPS would be useless within hours.

How the Signals Work

Each GPS satellite transmits on two frequencies:

  • L1: 1575.42 MHz — the main civilian signal
  • L2: 1227.60 MHz — military/authorized use

The signals are spread-spectrum — they're deliberately spread across a wide frequency band using pseudorandom noise codes. This makes them resistant to jamming and allows multiple satellites to share the same frequency without interfering.

Each satellite has a unique pseudorandom code. Your phone knows all the codes, so it can pick out each satellite's signal from the noise. This is why you sometimes see "acquiring GPS" — your phone is matching each signal to its known code.

Error Sources

GPS isn't perfect. Several things introduce errors:

  • Ionospheric delay: Charged particles in the upper atmosphere can slow down the signal, adding 1-10 meters of error. Modern GPS uses dual-frequency receivers to measure and correct for this.
  • Multipath: Signals bounce off buildings, trees, or the ground before reaching your phone. This adds noise but can be mitigated with antenna design.
  • Satellite geometry: If all visible satellites are clustered in one part of the sky, accuracy degrades. This is called PDOP (Position Dilution of Precision).
  • Signal obstruction: Buildings, dense forest, or even heavy cloud cover can block or weaken signals.

Beyond GPS: GNSS

GPS is the American system. There are others:

  • GLONASS: Russian
  • Galileo: European
  • BeiDou: Chinese

Modern phones receive multiple GNSS systems simultaneously. Using more satellites means better accuracy and faster fixes. Your phone might track 20+ satellites at once.

What About Indoor Positioning?

GPS signals don't penetrate buildings well. Indoor positioning uses alternatives:

  • WiFi: Your phone matches nearby WiFi access points against a database (Google, Apple have these). Triangulate from MAC addresses.
  • Bluetooth beacons: Fixed Bluetooth transmitters for centimeter-level accuracy (used in warehouses, airports).
  • Cell towers: Coarse positioning from cell tower IDs, useful when GPS fails.

The Takeaway

GPS is one of those technologies that's invisible until it breaks. Your phone does about 10 trillion operations per second just to figure out where you are. It solves relativistic physics equations in real time, corrects for atmospheric distortions, and somehow makes it all work well enough that you can navigate a city without thinking about it.

The next time you see your blue dot on a map, remember: it's listening to atomic clocks in space and doing general relativity in your pocket.
