How GPS Works

March 4, 2026

Every time you check your phone's map, you're using one of the most precise engineered systems ever built — and it only works because Einstein was right.

The Basic Idea: Trilateration

GPS isn't triangulation — it's trilateration. The difference: triangulation uses angles, trilateration uses distances. You need to know how far you are from known points, then find where those distance circles intersect.

One satellite gives you a sphere of possible positions. Two gives you a circle. Three pins you down to two points. The fourth? It resolves the ambiguity and also solves your clock problem.

The Clock Problem

GPS satellites carry atomic clocks — devices accurate to about 1 nanosecond per day. But your phone doesn't. Your phone has a quartz crystal, accurate to about 1 millisecond per day. That's a million times less precise.

Here's the issue: light travels 300,000 kilometers per second. A 1-millisecond error means a 300-kilometer position error. So the fourth satellite isn't about location — it's about time.

The Math

Each satellite broadcasts:

  • Its position (ephemeris data)
  • The exact time (from its atomic clock)
  • Where all other satellites are (almanac data)

Your receiver measures how long each signal took to arrive. Distance = time × speed of light. Do this with 4 satellites, and you get 4 equations with 4 unknowns: your X, Y, Z position, plus your clock offset.

Relativity: The Part That Blows Minds

GPS satellites orbit at about 20,200 km altitude, moving at 14,000 km/h. Two relativistic effects kick in:

1. Time dilation (special relativity): Moving clocks run slower. The satellites' clocks tick about 7 microseconds slower per day due to their velocity.

2. Gravitational time dilation (general relativity): Clocks in weaker gravity run faster. The satellites are in weaker gravity, so their clocks tick about 45 microseconds faster per day.

Net effect: GPS clocks run about 38 microseconds faster per day than ground clocks. Without correcting for this, GPS would drift 11 kilometers per day. After just two weeks, the system would be useless.

Your phone's navigation works because we applied Einstein's equations to engineering.

Augmentation Systems

Base GPS gives you ~5-10 meter accuracy. But we can do better:

  • WAAS (Wide Area Augmentation System) — Ground stations measure GPS errors and broadcast corrections. Improves accuracy to ~1-2 meters.
  • GLONASS / Galileo / BeiDou — Other global navigation satellite systems. Using multiple constellations improves accuracy and availability.
  • RTK (Real-Time Kinematic) — Uses a known base station to achieve centimeter-level accuracy. Used in surveying and precision agriculture.

Why 4 Satellites?

You need at least 4 because:

  1. 3 satellites give you a position, but only if your clock is perfect
  2. Your phone's clock is never perfect
  3. The 4th satellite solves for time error

In practice, your phone tracks as many satellites as it can (often 10-20) and uses all of them for better accuracy.

The Takeaway

Every time you navigate somewhere, you're carrying a device that:

  • Receives signals from 20+ orbiting satellites
  • Uses atomic clocks to measure nanosecond-level time differences
  • Corrects for relativistic effects predicted by a century-old theory
  • Solves 4 simultaneous equations to find where you are

And it fits in your pocket.
