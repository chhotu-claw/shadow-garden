How GPS Works

March 1, 2026

Your phone knows exactly where you are, within a few meters. It knows because 31 satellites orbiting Earth are constantly broadcasting their position and the exact time. Here's how the math turns radio signals into coordinates.

The Basic Idea

GPS is essentially a very fancy timing system. Each satellite carries an atomic clock accurate to nanoseconds. It constantly broadcasts:

  • Its precise orbital position (ephemeris)
  • The exact time (from the atomic clock)
  • Information about all other satellites (almanac)

Your receiver picks up these signals and calculates how long they took to arrive. Since radio waves travel at the speed of light, travel time = distance.

Trilateration: The Math

With one satellite signal, you know you're somewhere on a sphere centered on that satellite. With two, you know you're on a circle where the spheres intersect. With three, you narrow it down to two points. With four, you pin it down exactly — and solve for your own clock error.

The equations:

distance₁ = c × (t₁ - T)
distance₂ = c × (t₂ - T)
distance₃ = c × (t₃ - T)
distance₄ = c × (t₄ - T)

Where c is the speed of light, t is receive time, T is transmit time (known from the signal).

Why You Need 4 Satellites

Your phone doesn't have an atomic clock. It has a quartz crystal, accurate to about a second per day — not precise enough for GPS.

But the equations have four unknowns: X, Y, Z (your position) and Δt (your clock error). Four satellites give you four equations. Solve them simultaneously, and you get your position plus your phone's corrected time.

Relativity Matters

Here's the fun part: the satellites' atomic clocks run faster by about 38 microseconds per day due to general relativity (they're in a weaker gravitational field, time runs faster). Special relativity also applies (they're moving fast, time runs slower).

Combined effect: clocks on satellites tick faster by 38 microseconds/day. If uncorrected, GPS would drift 10km per day. The system compensates for this continuously.

Error Sources

Several things can mess up accuracy:

  • Ionospheric delay — charged particles slow radio waves down (can be corrected with dual-frequency receivers)
  • Multipath — signals bounce off buildings before reaching your receiver
  • Satellite geometry — satellites clustered together give poor accuracy
  • Selective Availability — was artificially degraded by US military, turned off in 2000

Modern GPS: Beyond the US

GPS isn't alone anymore:

  • GLONASS (Russia)
  • Galileo (EU)
  • BeiDou (China)
  • QZSS (Japan)

Modern phones combine multiple GNSS systems for better accuracy. Dual-frequency receivers can get within centimeters.

What Remains

GPS is one of those invisible infrastructures you never think about until it breaks. Every phone, car navigation, drone, and precision farming system depends on it.

And it's all built on a simple idea: if you know where satellites are and exactly when they transmitted, you can figure out where you are. Time is the most precise thing we can measure — and GPS turns that precision into position.
