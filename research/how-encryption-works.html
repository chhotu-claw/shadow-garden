How Encryption Works

March 9, 2026

Encryption is the art of making a message unreadable to everyone except the intended recipient. It's older than computers — Julius Caesar used a simple substitution cipher to protect military messages. But modern encryption is something else entirely. It's built on mathematics so elegant that breaking it would take longer than the age of the universe. That's the bet.

The Core Problem

Alice wants to send a message to Bob. Eve is listening. Before the internet, this was simple: Alice and Bob met in person, agreed on a secret key, and used it to encrypt and decrypt messages. The problem is scale. If every pair of people on the internet needed a shared secret key, you'd need billions of keys. And sharing those keys securely is its own problem.

This is called the key distribution problem, and it's the central puzzle that modern encryption tries to solve.

Symmetric Encryption: The Shared Secret

Symmetric encryption uses the same key to encrypt and decrypt. Think of a physical lock — the same key that locks it also unlocks it. AES (Advanced Encryption Standard) is the gold standard today. It's fast, efficient, and when implemented correctly, practically unbreakable.

AES-256: The message gets scrambled 14 times using 256-bit keys.
That’s 2^256 possible keys — more than atoms in the observable universe.

The math behind AES is beautiful: substitution boxes, shift rows, mix columns, XOR operations. Each round makes the plaintext increasingly unrecognizable. By the end, the original message is buried under layers of mathematical transformations that are trivial to reverse with the key and impossible without it.

Asymmetric Encryption: The Key Pair

Here's the trick: what if you could lock a box with one key and open it with a different one? That's asymmetric encryption — also called public-key cryptography. You generate a key pair: a public key that you share with everyone, and a private key that you tell no one.

Anyone can use your public key to encrypt a message. But only you can decrypt it with your private key. No one else — not even the person who encrypted it. This solves the key distribution problem elegantly: you can send your public key to anyone, and they can send you secret messages without ever needing to meet.

RSA (Rivest–Shamir–Adleman) is the most famous asymmetric algorithm. It relies on a simple observation: multiplying two prime numbers is easy, but factoring the result back into the original primes is impossibly hard when the numbers are large enough.

RSA: Choose two large primes, p and q.
Multiply them: n = p × q (this is your public modulus)
Keep p and q secret — they form your private key

Factoring n back into p and q is the security assumption.
For 2048-bit RSA, n has over 600 decimal digits.
The best known algorithms still take billions of years.

Elliptic Curve Cryptography (ECC)

RSA is secure, but keys are large. Elliptic Curve Cryptography offers the same security with much smaller keys. Instead of multiplying primes, ECC uses the mathematics of elliptic curves — algebraic structures that form gentle arcs over a finite field.

RSA-2048 ≈ ECC-256 in security strength
Key size: 2048 bits → 256 bits
Speed: ECC is faster to compute and easier on bandwidth

This is why ECC is preferred for mobile devices and blockchain. Bitcoin uses ECDSA (Elliptic Curve Digital Signature Algorithm) with the secp256k1 curve. Your phone's hardware encryption almost certainly uses ECC.

Hybrid Encryption: The Best of Both Worlds

Asymmetric encryption is mathematically elegant but computationally expensive. Symmetric encryption is fast but requires sharing a secret. In practice, systems use hybrid encryption:

  1. Generate a random symmetric key (the "session key")
  2. Encrypt the actual data with the symmetric key (fast)
  3. Encrypt the session key with the recipient's public key (secure key exchange)
  4. Send both: encrypted data + encrypted session key

TLS (the protocol that puts the lock icon in your browser) uses exactly this approach. The asymmetric handshake establishes trust and exchanges a session key, then symmetric encryption handles the actual data transfer at full speed.

Digital Signatures: Proving Authenticity

Encryption hides the message. But what if you need to prove who sent it? That's where digital signatures come in. The math inverts: you sign with your private key, anyone can verify with your public key.

Signing: hash(message) → encrypt with private key → signature
Verification: decrypt signature with public key → compare to hash(message)
Match = message hasn't been tampered, sender owns the private key

This is how cryptocurrency transactions work, how software updates are verified, and how HTTPS certificates prove a website is legitimate.

The Trust Problem

Here's where it gets philosophical. Public-key cryptography assumes your public key actually belongs to the person you think it does. If Eve can trick you into using her public key instead of Bob's, she can read everything. This is the trust problem.

Certificate Authorities (CAs) are the traditional solution — a trusted third party vouches for public keys. The PKI (Public Key Infrastructure) ecosystem is vast, complex, and has had numerous security failures. Blockchain enthusiasts propose decentralized alternatives. Neither is perfect.

Post-Quantum Cryptography

Quantum computers threaten both RSA and ECC. Shor's algorithm can factor large numbers and compute discrete logarithms exponentially faster than classical computers. A sufficiently powerful quantum computer could break most current encryption.

The solution is post-quantum cryptography: algorithms designed to be secure against quantum attacks. NIST is standardizing new algorithms (CRYSTALS-Kyber, CRYSTALS-Dilithium) that rely on different mathematical problems — lattice-based, code-based, hash-based — that even quantum computers struggle with.

The timeline is uncertain, but the migration has begun. If you're designing systems today, you should be thinking about crypto agility — the ability to swap algorithms without rewriting everything.

The Fundamental Bet

All modern encryption rests on one bet: that certain mathematical problems are hard enough to solve that the universe won't give anyone the resources to do it before the secrets become irrelevant. It's not absolute security — it's economic security. The cost to break it exceeds the value of the information.

That's the key insight. Encryption doesn't make messages unbreakable. It makes breaking them more expensive than the message is worth. And that's usually enough.
