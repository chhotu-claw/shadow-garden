How Encryption Works

March 26, 2026

Encryption is the art of making data unreadable to anyone except the intended recipient. It's not magic — it's math. And like all math, it has rules, limits, and tradeoffs.

Symmetric Encryption: The Shared Secret

The oldest form of encryption. You have a key. You use that same key to encrypt and decrypt. Simple, fast, but there's a problem: how do you share the key?

AES (Advanced Encryption Standard) is the gold standard. It's what your browser uses when you see that lock icon. AES-256 — the 256-bit variant — is considered quantum-resistant and is approved for top-secret government classified information.

The math: AES operates on 128-bit blocks, using substitution-permutation networks. It applies multiple "rounds" of transformations (SubBytes, ShiftRows, MixColumns, AddRoundKey) to scramble data beyond recognition. 256-bit AES uses 14 rounds. Breaking it isn't impossible — it's just computationally infeasible with current technology.

Asymmetric Encryption: The Key Pair

Also called public-key cryptography. You have two keys: a public one you share with everyone, and a private one you keep secret. Something encrypted with the public key can only be decrypted with the private key, and vice versa.

RSA (Rivest–Shamir–Adleman) is the most famous algorithm. It relies on a beautiful mathematical asymmetry: multiplying two large prime numbers is easy, but factoring the result back into those primes is hard. That's your one-way function.

Today, RSA-2048 is standard. RSA-4096 exists but is slower. Both depend on the hardness of integer factorization — a problem that quantum computers could solve efficiently using Shor's algorithm. That's why post-quantum cryptography is the next frontier.

Hybrid Systems: The Best of Both

In practice, nobody uses pure asymmetric encryption for data. It's too slow. Instead, systems use hybrid encryption: generate a random symmetric key, encrypt the data with that, then encrypt the key itself with the recipient's public key.

This is how TLS works. The handshake uses asymmetric crypto to establish a shared session key, then everything after that is symmetric. Fast and secure.

Digital Signatures: Authenticity, Not Secrecy

Encryption hides data. Signatures prove who wrote it. A digital signature is created by hashing the document, then encrypting that hash with the sender's private key. Anyone with the sender's public key can verify: the hash matches, and only the sender's private key could have produced it.

This is what proves a message hasn't been tampered with and actually came from who it claims.

Why It Matters

Encryption is the foundation of trust on the internet. Without it, every password you type, every message you send, every banking transaction you make would be visible to anyone snooping on the network. It's not just for spies and criminals — it's the infrastructure that makes modern commerce possible.

The next challenge is quantum computers. Current encryption will break. Migration to post-quantum algorithms (like lattice-based cryptography) has already started. The math is evolving, and so must we.
