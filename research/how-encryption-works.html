How Encryption Works

2026-03-19

Encryption is the art of making information unreadable to anyone except those who possess the key. But behind that simple idea lies decades of mathematical research, evolving standards, and trade-offs that most people never think about. Let's break it down.

Symmetric Encryption: The Shared Secret

Symmetric encryption is the oldest form — both parties share the same key. You lock the box with a key, and the recipient uses the same key to unlock it. Simple, fast, but there's a problem: how do you get the key to the other person without someone else intercepting it?

The math behind symmetric encryption typically involves:

  • Substitution — replacing bytes with other bytes based on the key
  • Permutation — rearranging bytes in predictable ways
  • XOR operations — bitwise operations that are fast and reversible

AES (Advanced Encryption Standard) is the gold standard today. It supports 128, 192, and 256-bit keys, and it's designed to be efficient in both software and hardware. AES-256-GCM (Galois/Counter Mode) adds authentication — you know the message hasn't been tampered with, not just that it's encrypted.

The beauty of AES is its simplicity. The entire algorithm is just a few operations repeated rounds — substitution, shift rows, mix columns, add round key. But the key schedule and the number of rounds make it secure.

Asymmetric Encryption: The Key Pair

Asymmetric encryption solves the key distribution problem with a key pair: a public key that you share with everyone, and a private key that you keep secret. Anyone can use your public key to encrypt a message, but only you can decrypt it with your private key.

The math here is trapdoor functions — easy to compute in one direction, impossibly hard to reverse without special knowledge:

  • RSA — based on the difficulty of factoring large prime numbers
  • ECC (Elliptic Curve Cryptography) — based on the discrete logarithm problem on elliptic curves

ECC is the modern favorite. A 256-bit ECC key provides roughly the same security as a 3072-bit RSA key, but with much smaller key sizes and faster computations. This matters especially on mobile devices and in protocols like TLS.

The Hybrid Approach

Here's the thing: asymmetric encryption is slow. Really slow. Symmetric encryption is fast — AES can encrypt gigabytes per second. So in practice, systems use both:

  1. Generate a random symmetric key (the "session key")
  2. Encrypt your data with AES using that session key
  3. Encrypt the session key with the recipient's public key
  4. Send both the encrypted data and encrypted session key

TLS does exactly this. The client and server use asymmetric crypto to establish a shared session key, then use symmetric encryption for the actual data. Best of both worlds.

What Actually Protects You

A few things worth knowing:

  • Key length matters — longer keys are harder to brute-force, but the relationship isn't linear. Doubling key length doesn't double security.
  • Mode of operation matters — ECB (Electronic Codebook) is insecure because it reveals patterns. GCM, CBC, CTR are all different trade-offs.
  • IVs (Initialization Vectors) — random data added to ensure the same plaintext encrypts to different ciphertext each time
  • Forward secrecy — if a key is compromised, past sessions should still be secure. This requires ephemeral key exchanges (like in TLS 1.3).

The Takeaway

Encryption isn't magic. It's math + engineering + trust. The algorithms are well-understood and publicly vetted. The hard part is key management — how do you generate, store, rotate, and recover keys? That's where most breaches happen, not in the crypto itself.

Use AES-256-GCM for symmetric encryption. Use ECC (Curve25519) for asymmetric. Demand forward secrecy in your protocols. And remember: strong crypto is necessary but not sufficient. The infrastructure around it matters just as much.
