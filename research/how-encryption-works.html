How Encryption Works

March 11, 2026

Encryption is the art of making information unreadable to everyone except the person who should be able to read it. It's older than computers — Caesar used it to hide military messages — but the math behind modern encryption is both beautiful and terrifying.

The Problem

Alice wants to send a message to Bob. Eve intercepts every message. How does Alice hide the content from Eve while letting Bob read it?

The solution is encryption: transform the message using a key so that only someone with the matching decryption key can recover the original.

Symmetric Encryption: The Shared Secret

Symmetric encryption uses the same key to encrypt and decrypt. It's like a physical lock: anyone with the key can lock and unlock.

AES (Advanced Encryption Standard)

The gold standard. AES is what the US government uses to classify top secret information. It operates on 128-bit blocks with keys of 128, 192, or 256 bits.

AES isn't broken because there's no known way to crack it faster than brute force. And brute force on a 256-bit key would take longer than the age of the universe — even if you turned every atom in the universe into a computer.

The Key Exchange Problem

Here's the catch: Alice and Bob need to agree on the same key. But if they can securely share a key, why not just share the message the same way?

This is the fundamental paradox of symmetric encryption. It works great once keys are shared — but getting keys shared securely in the first place is hard.

Asymmetric Encryption: The Key Pair

Asymmetric encryption solves the key exchange problem with a pair of keys:

  • Public key: Can encrypt, cannot decrypt. Share freely.
  • Private key: Can decrypt what the public key encrypted. Never share.

Think of it like a mailbox. Anyone can drop letters through the slot (encrypt with public key), but only the mailbox owner has the key to open it and read them (decrypt with private key).

RSA

RSA is the most famous asymmetric algorithm. Its security rests on a beautiful mathematical fact: multiplying two large prime numbers is easy, but factoring the result back into the primes is impossibly hard.

Multiply 17 × 23 = 391. Easy. Now tell me the prime factors of 4,027,198,319. That's RSA.

RSA keys are typically 2048 or 4096 bits. The 4096-bit version would take a classical computer about 6 quadrillion years to factor.

ECC (Elliptic Curve Cryptography)

ECC provides the same security as RSA with much smaller keys. A 256-bit ECC key is roughly as secure as a 3072-bit RSA key.

Your phone uses ECC. Bitcoin uses ECC. It's the future because it needs less computation — important for devices with limited power.

Hashing: The One-Way Function

A hash function turns any input into a fixed-size "fingerprint." Good hashes are:

  • Deterministic: Same input always gives same output
  • Fast to compute
  • Irreversible: Can't recover input from output
  • Collision-resistant: Extremely hard to find two inputs with same output

SHA-256 produces a 256-bit hash. It's used in Bitcoin, TLS certificates, file integrity checking. Changing one bit in the input completely changes the output.

Hashing isn't encryption — you can't "decrypt" a hash. But it's essential for password storage. Services don't store your password; they store its hash. When you log in, they hash your input and compare.

Digital Signatures: Proving Identity

Encryption hides content. Signatures prove who created it.

Digital signatures work like this:

  1. Create a hash of the message
  2. Encrypt the hash with your private key
  3. Anyone can decrypt with your public key and compare to a fresh hash

If the hashes match, we know: (1) the message hasn't been tampered with, (2) the holder of the private key signed it.

Hybrid Encryption: The Best of Both Worlds

In practice, systems use both:

  • Asymmetric encryption to securely exchange a symmetric key
  • Symmetric encryption to encrypt the actual data (much faster)

This is exactly what TLS does. Your browser uses RSA or ECC to verify the server's identity and exchange a session key. Then AES encrypts all the data you send and receive.

The Quantum Threat

Shor's algorithm can factor large numbers and compute discrete logarithms exponentially faster on a quantum computer. A sufficiently powerful quantum computer would break RSA and ECC.

Post-quantum cryptography is developing new algorithms based on problems that even quantum computers struggle with. NIST recently standardized CRYSTALS-Kyber and CRYSTALS-Dilithium.

The math is beautiful: easy one way, impossibly hard the other. That's the bet all encryption makes.
