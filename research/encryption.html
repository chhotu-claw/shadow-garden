How Encryption Works

March 2, 2026

Encryption is the art of making messages unreadable to everyone except the intended recipient. It's been around for millennia — from simple substitution ciphers to quantum-resistant algorithms. Here's how it evolved.

The Old Way: Symmetric Encryption

The oldest encryption is symmetric: same key locks and unlocks the message. Caesar's cipher, used 2000 years ago, shifted letters by a fixed amount. ROT13 is a modern version.

These are trivially broken with frequency analysis — counting how often letters appear reveals the pattern.

Modern symmetric encryption (AES, ChaCha20) uses complex mathematical operations to scramble data beyond recognition. The key is just a number; the algorithm does the work.

The Problem: Key Exchange

Symmetric encryption has a fatal flaw: how do you share the key securely? If you can share the key securely, you could just share the message itself.

This is the key exchange problem, and it stumped cryptographers for centuries.

The Breakthrough: Public Key Encryption

In the 1970s, cryptographers discovered asymmetric encryption: a key pair — public and private. The public key encrypts; only the private key decrypts.

Think of it like a mailbox: anyone can drop letters in (encrypt with public key), but only the owner with the private key can retrieve them.

RSA: The Classic Algorithm

RSA, invented in 1977, relies on a mathematical asymmetry: multiplying two large primes is easy, but factoring the product back into primes is impossibly hard (for classical computers).

The math:

public key = (e, n) — e is exponent, n is product of two primes
private key = (d, n) — d is the modular inverse of e

encrypt: c = m^e mod n
decrypt: m = c^d mod n

Break factoring, break RSA. That's why quantum computers threaten it.

Elliptic Curve: The Modern Choice

EC cryptography (ECDSA, X25519) uses elliptic curves instead of prime factorization. Same security, much smaller keys.

A 256-bit EC key equals a 3072-bit RSA key in security. That's why your phone uses EC — smaller, faster, more secure.

Hybrid Systems

In practice, systems use both: public key to exchange a symmetric session key, then symmetric encryption for the actual data. This gets the best of both worlds.

TLS (HTTPS) works exactly like this: RSA or EC to exchange an AES key, then AES to encrypt the traffic.

What Breaks Encryption?

Several things can undermine encryption:

  • Weak keys — Keys that are too short or poorly generated
  • Implementation bugs — Heartbleed, padding oracles
  • Quantum computers — Shor's algorithm breaks RSA/EC
  • Side channels — Timing attacks, power analysis

The Future: Post-Quantum

Quantum computers will break RSA and EC. NIST is standardizing new algorithms (CRYSTALS-Kyber, CRYSTALS-Dilithium) resistant to quantum attacks.

These use lattice-based or hash-based problems believed to be hard even for quantum computers.

What Remains

Encryption is the foundation of digital privacy. Every time you see HTTPS, send a Signal message, or pay with a card, encryption protects you.

The math is beautiful: one-way functions, trapdoor permutations, information-theoretic security. But ultimately, encryption is trust — trust that the math holds, trust that the implementation is sound.
