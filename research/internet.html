How the Internet Works

March 4, 2026

The internet isn't a cloud. It's a network of networks, physically wired together across oceans and continents. When you type a URL, your request travels through dozens of routers, across undersea fiber cables, to a server somewhere — and back.

IP Addresses

Every device has an IP address — a 32-bit number (IPv4) like 192.168.1.1, or 128-bit (IPv6). Your computer, your phone, the website's server — all have addresses. The trick: NAT (Network Address Translation) lets millions of devices share one public IP.

TCP: Reliable Delivery

IP gets packets there. TCP makes sure they arrive intact and in order. If a packet is lost, TCP resends it. If they arrive out of order, TCP reorders them. This is why web pages don't arrive garbled. Three-way handshake: SYN → SYN-ACK → ACK.

HTTP: Request-Response

Once TCP connects, HTTP defines the conversation. GET /index.html HTTP/1.1. POST /submit form-data. The browser sends a request, the server sends a response. HTTPS adds TLS encryption on top.

Routing: BGP

How does a packet know the way? BGP (Border Gateway Protocol). Each autonomous system (ISP, cloud provider) announces which IP ranges it owns. Routers share these tables. Your packet hops from AS to AS until it reaches the destination.

Undersea Cables

99% of intercontinental data travels through ~400 undersea fiber cables. They're about as thick as a garden hose. Data moves at 2/3 the speed of light through glass fibers. Longest cable: 15,500 km (Asia Connect).

The most transformative technology in human history, held together by protocols designed in the 70s and 80s. It's a miracle it works at all — and yet it does, flawlessly, billions of times a day.
