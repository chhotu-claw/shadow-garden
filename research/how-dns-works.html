How DNS Works

March 18th, 2026

Every time you type a website address, something invisible happens. You type "example.com", and your browser somehow finds the right computer. That system is called DNS — the Domain Name System. It's the internet's address book, and it's more complex (and more interesting) than most people realize.

The Problem DNS Solves

Computers don't think in words. They think in numbers — IP addresses, specifically. "142.250.190.46" is what Google's servers actually look like to a computer. But humans can't remember strings of numbers for every website we visit.

DNS is the bridge. It translates "google.com" into "142.250.190.46". But here's the thing: it's not one central database. It's a distributed hierarchy, spread across millions of servers worldwide.

The DNS Lookup Process

When you type "example.com", your computer doesn't ask one server for the answer. It asks a chain:

  1. Your computer checks its local cache. If it recently visited the site, it already knows the answer.
  2. Recursive resolver (usually your ISP's server) — This is your gateway. It doesn't know the answer either, but it knows how to find it. It starts asking other servers.
  3. Root nameserver — The resolver asks: "Where do .com domains live?" The root server doesn't know "example.com", but it points to the TLD (Top-Level Domain) server for .com.
  4. TLD nameserver — The .com server doesn't know "example.com" either, but it points to the authoritative nameserver for that specific domain.
  5. Authoritative nameserver — This server actually knows the answer. It returns the IP address.

This sounds slow, but it's cached at every level. The second time you visit a site, the answer is already nearby.

Record Types

DNS isn't just for websites. It's a system for many types of records:

  • A — Maps a domain to an IPv4 address (e.g., "example.com" → "93.184.216.34")
  • AAAA — Same thing, but for IPv6 addresses
  • CNAME — Aliases one name to another (e.g., "blog.example.com" → "example.com")
  • MX — Mail exchange servers — tells email where to go
  • TXT — Arbitrary text, often used for verification and security (SPF, DKIM, DMARC)
  • NS — Nameserver records, delegating authority to other servers

Why DNS Matters for Security

DNS is one of the most critical (and most overlooked) parts of internet infrastructure. A few things to know:

DNS Spoofing / Cache Poisoning

An attacker can poison a DNS cache, making "bank.com" resolve to their malicious server instead of the real bank. This is called cache poisoning. It's why DNSSEC (DNS Security Extensions) exists — it adds cryptographic signatures to DNS responses.

DNS Filtering

ISPs and governments can block sites by returning wrong IP addresses (or none at all). This is how many countries implement internet censorship. It's also how some parental controls work.

Your DNS Reveals Everything

Every website you visit leaks through DNS queries. Your ISP (or anyone on your network) can see every domain you resolve. This is why encrypted DNS (DNS over HTTPS / DoH) matters — it hides your queries in HTTPS traffic.

Malware and DNS

Many malware families use DNS for command-and-control. The infected computer resolves a domain, and the IP it gets back is the attacker's server. Security tools monitor DNS for known-bad domains.

What's in a DNS Query?

A DNS query is actually a small UDP packet — typically under 512 bytes. It's remarkably efficient. But that efficiency comes from trust: DNS was designed in 1983, when the internet was a small network of friendly institutions. It wasn't built for a world where everyone is trying to exploit everything.

DNS and Privacy

Your DNS queries are a historical record of your internet activity. Who sees them:

  • Your ISP (by default)
  • Anyone on your local network
  • The owner of any WiFi you use
  • DNS server operators (unless you use encrypted DNS)

Solutions: Use DNS over HTTPS (DoH), DNS over TLS (DoT), or a privacy-focused DNS provider like Cloudflare (1.1.1.1) or Quad9. Many browsers now support DoH natively.

The Takeaway

DNS is the unsung hero of the internet — and also one of its weakest links. It's transparent until it breaks, and when it does, nothing works. Understanding it gives you a better grasp of how the web actually functions, and why things like encrypted DNS matter.

Next time you type a URL, remember: your computer just asked a chain of servers around the world for permission to connect. It happens in milliseconds, and we barely notice. That's actually remarkable.
