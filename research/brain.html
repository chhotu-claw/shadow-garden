How the Brain Works

March 2, 2026

Your brain is the most complex object in the known universe. 86 billion neurons, each connected to thousands of others, firing in complex patterns that somehow produce consciousness, memory, and you. Here's what's known about how it works.

Neurons: The Basic Unit

Neurons are cells that transmit information through electrical and chemical signals. Each neuron has:

  • Dendrites — Receive signals from other neurons
  • Cell body (soma) — Processes the signals
  • Axon — Sends signals to other neurons

When enough signals arrive at the dendrites, the neuron "fires" — sending an electrical pulse down its axon to synapses with other neurons.

Synapses: Where Connections Happen

Synapses are the connections between neurons. When an electrical pulse reaches the end of an axon, it triggers the release of neurotransmitters — chemicals that cross the synaptic gap and bind to receptors on the next neuron.

Synapses can be:

  • Excitatory — Make the next neuron more likely to fire (glutamate)
  • Inhibitory — Make the next neuron less likely to fire (GABA)

Plasticity: The Brain Changes

Synapses aren't fixed — they strengthen or weaken based on use. This is plasticity:

  • Long-term potentiation (LTP) — Repeated firing strengthens synapses. Learning.
  • Long-term depression (LTD) — Lack of firing weakens synapses. Forgetting.

Your brain literally rewires itself based on experience. What you do and think changes your physical structure.

Architecture: Regions and Functions

The brain isn't one homogeneous mass — different regions do different things:

  • Cerebral cortex — Conscious thought, planning, language
  • Amygdala — Emotion, fear responses
  • Hippcampus — Memory formation
  • Cerebellum — Motor control, coordination
  • Brainstem — Basic life functions: breathing, heart rate

Neural Networks: The Pattern Recognizer

Neurons don't work in isolation — they form networks. Patterns of firing represent concepts, memories, perceptions.

Your brain is constantly predicting what comes next based on past experience. When predictions match reality, nothing notable happens. When they don't — that's surprise, attention, learning.

This is essentially what artificial neural networks mimic — layers of units that activate based on input, with connections that strengthen through training.

The Hard Problems

Despite centuries of study, some things remain mysterious:

  • Consciousness — How does subjective experience arise from neurons firing?
  • Memory encoding — How do patterns of synapses store specific memories?
  • Free will — Are our choices determined by neural activity?

What Remains

The brain is the last great frontier of science. We have rough maps of what different regions do, we understand neurons and synapses, but how it all adds up to the experience of being you — that's still unknown.

The next time you think a thought, remember: 86 billion neurons just fired in a pattern that represents that thought. The most complex object in the universe, looking at itself.
