How Nuclear Power Works

March 5, 2026

Nuclear power is misunderstood — feared, worshipped, but rarely understood. The physics is elegant. The engineering is brutal. The politics is a disaster. Let's untangle all three.

The Atom: A Quick Recap

Every atom has a nucleus (protons + neutrons) surrounded by electrons. The protons define what element it is — carbon, uranium, whatever. Add a neutron, you get an isotope.

Here's the thing about big nuclei: they're unstable. The strong nuclear force holding them together is fighting a war against the electromagnetic force (protons repelling each other). For light elements, the strong force wins. For heavy ones, it's a close call.

Fission: Splitting the Unstable

Uranium-235 is the celebrity isotope. It's big (92 protons, 143 neutrons), unstable, and when you hit it with a neutron, something beautiful happens:

U-235 + neutron → U-236 (excited) → splits into two smaller nuclei + 2-3 neutrons + energy

Those 2-3 neutrons can hit other U-235 atoms, creating a chain reaction. This is fission.

The Energy Release

When uranium splits, the two resulting nuclei have less mass than the original — that "missing" mass converts to energy via E=mc². A single fission releases about 200 MeV. Compare to a chemical reaction (like burning coal) which releases a few eV. One fission is millions of times more energetic than one chemical reaction.

That energy manifests as:

  • Kinetic energy (99%) — the two split nuclei fly apart at huge speeds, heating up surrounding material
  • Neutrons (1%) — keep the chain reaction going
  • Gamma rays — high-energy radiation

The Reactor: Controlling the Uncontrollable

A nuclear reactor is, fundamentally, a machine for controlling a fission chain reaction. The trick is moderation — you need exactly enough neutrons to sustain the reaction, not explode it.

Fuel

Uranium is mined as U-238 (99.3%) and U-235 (0.7%). U-235 is the good stuff — it fissions easily with slow neutrons. Reactor fuel is "enriched" to 3-5% U-235. Weapons need 90%+.

Moderators

Fast neutrons from fission don't capture well in U-235. They need to slow down. Enter the moderator — water, graphite, or heavy hydrogen (deuterium). Neutrons bounce off moderator atoms, losing energy, becoming "thermal neutrons" that cause fission more efficiently.

Control Rods

These are rods made of neutron-absorbing material (boron, cadmium). Push them in, they absorb neutrons, the reaction slows. Pull them out, it speeds up. Insert fully, the reactor SCRAMs (emergency shutdown).

Coolant

All that kinetic energy turns to heat. Water (most common), gas, or liquid metal carries the heat away to make steam and spin turbines.

Types of Reactors

Pressurized Water Reactor (PWR)

Water under high pressure (doesn't boil) transfers heat to a secondary water loop that makes steam. Two loops — radioactive stay contained, steam goes to turbines. Most common design.

Boiling Water Reactor (BWR)

One loop — water boils directly in the reactor, steam goes to turbines. Simpler, but radioactive steam.

CANDU

Uses heavy water (D₂O) as moderator — can run on natural uranium (no enrichment needed). Canadian design, very safe.

Fast Breeder Reactor (FBR)

No moderator — uses fast neutrons. Burns U-238 (not fissile, but fissionable) and produces more fuel than it consumes. Russia's BN-800 is one example.

The Real Story: Waste

Here's where nuclear gets messy. Fission produces radioactive isotopes — some decay fast (hours/days), some slow (thousands of years). The "spent fuel" stays dangerous for millennia.

Current solutions: store it. Dry casks, deep geological repositories. Yucca Mountain was supposed to be the US solution — political collapse killed it.

The honest take: waste is a political problem more than a technical one. We know how to store it. We just don't want to.

Fusion: The Dream

Fission splits atoms. Fusion merges them — hydrogen nuclei fuse into helium, releasing energy. The sun does this.

Problem: nuclei are positively charged. They repel. You need temperatures of 100+ million degrees and enormous pressure to force them together.

We've been "30 years away" from fusion for 70 years. ITER, the biggest experiment, is behind schedule. Private fusion companies (Commonwealth Fusion, TAE) are making progress but don't hold your breath.

What Matters

Nuclear isn't magic. It's physics — elegant, well-understood physics. The engineering is hard but solved. The waste is a real problem, but manageable. The safety record (in the developed world) is excellent — you are more likely to die from coal pollution than nuclear.

The real barrier isn't technology. It's us.
