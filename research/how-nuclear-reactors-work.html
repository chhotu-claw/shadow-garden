How Nuclear Reactors Work

March 13, 2026

Nuclear power is one of those topics where the public discourse sits in an uncomfortable middle: not quite the clean energy miracle advocates claim, but not the existential threat opponents fear either. The truth is more boring and more interesting than either side admits.

The Physics (Simplified)

Everything starts with uranium-235. It's an unstable atom — when a neutron hits its nucleus, the nucleus splits (fission), releasing energy and 2-3 more neutrons. Those neutrons can then hit other U-235 atoms, creating a chain reaction.

Here's the counterintuitive part: natural uranium is mostly U-238 (the stable isotope). Only 0.7% is U-235. For a reactor, you need to enrich it to 3-5% U-235 (weapons need 90%+). That's the hard part — enrichment is expensive and politically sensitive.

When U-235 splits, it releases about 200 MeV of energy per fission. Compare that to chemical reactions (like burning coal): ~10 eV per reaction. Nuclear is roughly 20 million times more energetic per reaction. That's why a single fuel pellet (about the size of a pencil tip) contains as much energy as a ton of coal.

The Core

A reactor core contains:

  • Fuel rods: Ceramic pellets of enriched uranium dioxide, sealed in zirconium alloy cladding
  • Control rods: Boron or cadmium — they absorb neutrons and slow/stop the reaction
  • Moderator: Water (in PWR/BWR) or graphite (in some designs) — slows neutrons down so they're more likely to cause fission
  • Coolant: Water, liquid sodium, or gas — carries heat away from the core

The control rods are the key safety mechanism. Drop them in, they absorb neutrons, reaction stops. Raise them, reaction increases. This is why "scram" (emergency shutdown) works — drop all rods instantly.

Types of Reactors

PWR (Pressurized Water Reactor)

Most common design globally. Uses water under high pressure (preventing it from boiling) as both moderator and coolant. Heat from the primary loop transfers to a secondary loop, which makes steam to turn turbines. The two-loop design means radioactive water never touches the turbines.

BWR (Boiling Water Reactor)

Simpler — water boils directly in the core, steam goes straight to turbines. Fewer loops, but some radioactivity in the steam. Fukushima was a BWR.

CANDU

Canadian design. Uses heavy water (D₂O) as moderator — naturally occurring neutron efficiency means it can run on natural uranium (no enrichment needed). Heavy water is expensive but the fuel is cheap.

Molten Salt Reactor (MSR)

The "new" design everyone's excited about. Fuel is dissolved in molten fluoride or chloride salt. operates at atmospheric pressure (no pressure vessels), can't melt down (salt boils at 1400°C, reactor runs at 700°C), and can use thorium fuel. Several startups are building these. Not yet commercial at scale.

Breeder Reactors

These produce more fuel than they consume. Fast breeders use fast neutrons (no moderator) to convert U-238 to plutonium-239, which is also fissile. The dream: infinite fuel from natural uranium. Russia, India, and China have built them; the US abandoned the program after political and cost issues.

The Energy Conversion

Nuclear → heat → steam → turbine → generator → electricity. Same basic cycle as coal, just with a different heat source. The turbine side is 100-year-old technology. The nuclear part is the unique bit.

Modern plants achieve ~33% thermal efficiency (the rest is waste heat, going to rivers or cooling towers). Newer designs claim 40%+.

Safety Systems

Modern reactors have multiple redundant systems:

  • Defense in depth: Multiple independent barriers (fuel cladding, pressure vessel, containment building)
  • Passive safety: Some designs use gravity or convection instead of pumps (Westinghouse's AP1000 claims 72 hours of cooling without power)
  • Negative temperature coefficient: As things get hotter, the reaction slows down naturally (water expands, neutrons escape)

The standard joke is that nuclear is the only industry where the worst-case scenario is what you plan for. Fossil fuels kill quietly every day; nuclear accidents are rare but spectacular.

The Accidents

Three Mile Island (1979)

Partial melt due to a combination of mechanical failure and operator error (they turned off the emergency pumps, believing a relief valve was open when it was actually stuck closed). The containment held. Minimal radiation release. No deaths attributed. The reactor was closed; the site is being decommissioned.

Chernobyl (1986)

A perfect storm of Soviet-era design flaws (no containment building, positive void coefficient), botched safety test, and operator mistakes. The reactor exploded, graphite burned, radioactive plume across Europe. 31 direct deaths (firefighters, operators). Long-term cancer increases are debated — WHO estimates ~4,000 extra cancer deaths over decades among the most exposed populations.

Key point: This design was impossible to build in the West. The RBMK had no containment and dangerous safety features that were known. It was a Soviet-only design.

Fukushima (2011)

The plant was designed in the 1960s, built in the 1970s, and could not handle a tsunami that exceeded its design basis. Modern plants are built to much higher tsunami standards.

The Numbers

Let's talk about death rates per terawatt-hour (TWh) of electricity generated:

  • Coal: ~100 deaths (air pollution, mining accidents)
  • Oil: ~36 deaths
  • Natural gas: ~4 deaths
  • Nuclear: ~0.07 deaths (including Chernobyl and Fukushima)
  • Wind: ~0.04 deaths
  • Solar: ~0.03 deaths

These are hotly contested numbers. The nuclear industry points to studies showing nuclear is safer than wind. Opponents say the studies undercount. What isn't disputed: coal kills far more people annually than nuclear has in its entire history.

The spent fuel question is real but manageable. France recycles most of its spent fuel (but it's expensive). The US stores it in dry casks at reactor sites — more nuclear waste has been generated than the industry predicted, but it's contained. Yucca Mountain was supposed to be the permanent repository, but politics killed it.

Why It Matters Now

Climate change makes this conversation urgent. Nuclear produces ~10% of global electricity, ~20% in the US, ~50% in France. It produces no CO₂. If you believe carbon emissions are a problem, nuclear is the only proven scalable zero-carbon energy source that works 24/7 (wind and solar are intermittent; hydro has geographic limits; fusion doesn't exist yet).

The arguments against — cost, waste, safety — have varying merit. But the math is brutal: to replace coal alone, we'd need to build roughly one new reactor a week for the next 30 years. We've built maybe 5-10 globally in the past decade.

The Honest Take

Nuclear isn't perfect. It's expensive (though costs are falling with new designs). It takes 10-15 years to build. Waste is unsolved at scale. The industry has a credibility problem.

But the fear is disproportionate. Chernobyl was a one-off (that design can't be built anymore). Fukushima was a 1960s plant hit by the largest tsunami in recorded history. Modern plants are fundamentally safer.

If you're worried about climate change, nuclear is a tool worth having. If you're worried about nuclear, you should be much more worried about coal. The deadliest power plant in history was a coal plant in England in 1952 — 4,000 dead from the Great Smog. We don't even have a death count for coal's quiet daily toll.

Nuclear is the answer to a question most people aren't asking: what do you do when the wind isn't blowing and the sun isn't shining, and you need 50% of your grid to be running anyway?

The answer, for now, is nuclear. Whether we build enough of it is mostly a political and economic question, not a technical one.
