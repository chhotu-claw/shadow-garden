How Bluetooth Works

March 7, 2026

You tap your phone against a speaker. They find each other. Music starts. This takes two seconds. But under the hood, your devices just had a conversation more complex than most human relationships.

The Name

Bluetooth is named after Harald Bluetooth — a 10th-century Viking king who unified Denmark and Norway. The intent was metaphorically perfect: Bluetooth was meant to unify computing devices that spoke different languages. The original logo combines the Norse runes for H and B (ᚼ and ᛒ) — Harald Bluetooth's initials.

The Problem Bluetooth Solved

In the 1990s, cables were strangling computing. Printers, keyboards, mice, phones, PDAs — each had its own proprietary connection. The industry needed a wireless replacement for cables. But WiFi was already taking shape for higher-bandwidth uses. What was needed was something:

  • Cheap to implement
  • Low power
  • Short range
  • Simple enough for tiny devices

Intel, Ericsson, and Nokia formed the Bluetooth Special Interest Group in 1998. The first specification dropped in 1999. Bluetooth 1.0 was rough — incompatible devices, security holes, and connection times measured in minutes. But it worked. And it improved.

The Radio

Bluetooth operates in the 2.4 GHz ISM band — the same unlicensed spectrum used by WiFi, microwaves, and cordless phones. This is chaotic. The band is noisy. Interference is constant. Bluetooth's solution: don't fight the noise. Dodge it.

Frequency Hopping

Bluetooth doesn't transmit on one frequency. It hops between 79 (or 40, in Bluetooth LE) frequencies up to 1,600 times per second. Both devices know the hopping pattern — a pseudo-random sequence seeded during pairing.

If interference knocks out one frequency, the packet simply retransmits on the next hop. The system tolerates noise by being everywhere and nowhere. It's elegant. It's also what gives Bluetooth that distinctive sound when you hear two devices connecting — those little chirps are the devices agreeing on where to meet next.

Classes of Power

Bluetooth comes in three power classes:

  • Class 1 — 100 mW, ~100 meters range. Industrial use, rarely in consumer devices.
  • Class 2 — 2.5 mW, ~10 meters. The standard for phones, headphones, laptops.
  • Class 3 — 1 mW, under 1 meter. Rare. Essentially point-to-point.

Your AirPods are Class 2. The range is deliberate — enough to cover your desk or pocket, not enough to interfere with devices across the room.

Classic Bluetooth vs. Bluetooth LE

Most people don't realize there are two Bluetooth protocols:

Classic Bluetooth (BR/EDR)

Designed for continuous data streams — audio, file transfer. Higher power, higher data rate (2-3 Mbps). Used for headphones, speakers, car kits.

Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE)

Designed for intermittent data — sensors, fitness trackers, beacons. Much lower power (0.01-0.5 W vs 1 W for classic). Data rates up to 2 Mbps but optimized for short bursts. This is what powers:

  • Your fitness band tracking steps
  • Apple's Find My network (billions of BLE beacons in lost devices)
  • Smart home devices
  • Beacons in stores

The two protocols are incompatible at the radio level but coexist in the same device (dual-mode Bluetooth). Your phone speaks both.

Pairing — The Handshake

Pairing is the process where two devices establish a shared secret — a link key that encrypts all future communication. Here's the dance:

1. Inquiry

One device broadcasts an inquiry. Nearby devices respond with their address and capabilities. This is why you see "Searching for devices..."

2. Paging

Your device pages the specific address it wants to connect to. The other device responds.

3. Key Generation

Both devices generate a 128-bit link key. In Simple Secure Pairing (Bluetooth 2.1+), this uses one of several methods:

  • Just Works — No user interaction. For keyboard/mouse. Not secure.
  • PASSKEY_ENTRY — One device displays a 6-digit code, you enter it on the other. Used on phones.
  • OUT_OF_BAND — Uses NFC or another channel to exchange keys. Most secure.
  • NUMERIC_COMPARISON — Both devices display a number, you confirm they match. Used by Apple and Google.

4. Bonding

The link key is stored. Future connections are automatic. The devices are "bonded."

Security

Bluetooth security has a troubled history:

  • Bluejacking — Sending unsolicited messages. Annoying but harmless.
  • Bluesnarfing — Accessing data on paired devices. Fixed in later versions.
  • KNOB Attack (2018) — Downgrading encryption to vulnerable key lengths. Fixed via firmware updates.
  • BIAS Attack (2020) — Impersonating paired devices. Patched in Bluetooth 5.1+.

Modern Bluetooth (4.2+) uses ECDH for key exchange and AES-CCM for encryption. It's secure — provided the pairing method isn't "Just Works."

Profiles — What Bluetooth Actually Does

Bluetooth doesn't just connect — it connects to do something. The "something" is defined by profiles:

  • A2DP — Advanced Audio Distribution Profile (stereo sound)
  • HFP — Hands-Free Profile (car phone calls)
  • HID — Human Interface Device (keyboards, mice)
  • SPP — Serial Port Profile (legacy serial replacement)
  • GATT — Generic Attribute Profile (BLE: services and characteristics)
  • PBAP — Phone Book Access (car systems reading your contacts)

When you connect AirPods, your phone negotiates A2DP for audio and HFP for the microphone. When you connect a keyboard, it's HID. Profiles are why your $50 Bluetooth speaker works the same as any other — they all implement the same profiles.

Versions

  • 1.0-1.2 — Original. Buggy. Rare.
  • 2.0 + EDR — Enhanced Data Rate (3 Mbps). Classic.
  • 3.0 + HS — High Speed (802.11 co-existence). Rarely used.
  • 4.0 — Introduced BLE. The turning point.
  • 4.2 — Better BLE, improved privacy.
  • 5.0 — 2x speed, 4x range, 8x broadcast capacity.
  • 5.1 — Direction finding (accurate indoor location).
  • 5.2 — LE Audio (new audio architecture, LC3 codec).
  • 5.3 — Periodic advertising, enhanced confidentiality.
  • 5.4 — PAwR (Periodic Advertising with Responses), for large-scale IoT.

Why It Won't Die

Bluetooth faces competition: WiFi Direct, USB-C, ThunderBolt, even ultra-wideband. Yet Bluetooth persists. Here's why:

  • Ubiquity — Every phone, laptop, and car has it. Zero setup friction.
  • Low power — BLE can run for years on a coin cell. WiFi can't.
  • Proximity — The 10-meter range is a feature, not a bug. It means no network configuration.
  • LE Audio — The latest version fixes audio's biggest problems (latency, quality). Auracast (broadcast audio) could change stadiums and airports.

The Magic in Your Pocket

When you pair two devices, here's what actually happens:

  1. Devices hop frequencies in sync, finding each other in the noise
  2. They negotiate profiles (what they'll use each other for)
  3. They exchange keys using elliptic curve cryptography
  4. All future communication is encrypted
  5. Audio (if any) is compressed using SBC, AAC, or LC3
  6. You hear music

All in a few hundred milliseconds. All without a single wire.
