How the Stock Market Works

March 5, 2026

The stock market is one of those things everyone talks about but few understand. It's not a casino, not a voting machine for companies — it's a price discovery mechanism. That's the key.

What Is a Stock?

When you buy a stock, you're buying a tiny slice of ownership in a company. That slice is called a "share." As a shareholder, you own a proportion of the company's assets and profits.

But here's the thing: most people don't buy stocks for the ownership. They buy for the price movement. The ownership is theoretical; the price is real.

How Prices Move

Stock prices are supposedly set by supply and demand. But what determines demand? Not just value — sentiment, expectations, greed, fear, and increasingly, algorithms.

The bid-ask spread is the gap between what buyers want to pay and what sellers want to receive. When bid meets ask, a trade happens. The last trade price becomes "the price" — until the next trade changes it.

Exchanges and Market Makers

You can't just call up a company and buy stock. You go through a broker, who routes to an exchange (NYSE, NASDAQ). But exchanges are just matchmakers — they don't set prices, they record them.

Market makers are firms that actually provide liquidity. They commit to buying and selling at quoted prices. This sounds noble, but it's a business. They make money on the spread.

Bulls and Bears

Bulls think prices go up. Bears think prices go down. Neither is always right. The market doesn't care about your narrative.

Trading vs Investing

Trading is timing the market — buying and selling based on short-term movements. Investing is time in the market — holding for the long haul while the company grows.

The data suggests most traders underperform index funds. But that doesn't stop people from trying.

What Actually Moves Stocks

  • Earnings — Companies making money tend to be worth more
  • Interest rates — When rates rise, future profits are worth less today
  • Sentiment — Fear and greed are real drivers, not just fundamentals
  • Liquidity — Easy money = higher prices, tight money = lower prices
  • Algorithmic trading — Most volume is bots reacting to patterns humans can't see

The Efficient Market Hypothesis

EMH says prices reflect all available information. If that's true, you can't beat the market consistently — you're competing against everyone who already knows what you know.

But EMH has holes. Markets misprice things constantly. The question is whether you can exploit that faster than someone else can.

Why It Matters

The stock market isn't a zero-sum game in the way gambling is — companies create value over time. But the trading around those companies is closer to a game. The question is: are you playing to own a piece of something, or to guess what others will pay?

Most people would be better off not thinking about this at all and just buying index funds. But understanding how it works means you at least know what game you're not playing.
