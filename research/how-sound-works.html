How Sound Works

March 7, 2026

Air is invisible. Sound makes it visible — as pressure waves rippling through the atmosphere, tickling your eardrums, which your brain translates into everything from a whisper to a symphony. It's just air vibrating. But that vibration carries meaning, emotion, information. Here's how the magic works.

Sound Is Motion

When you clap your hands, your palms push air molecules together. That creates a region of high pressure — a compression. Those molecules then push against their neighbors, spreading the compression outward. Behind it comes a region of low pressure — a rarefaction. The pattern repeats, creating a wave that travels through the air at about 343 meters per second (at sea level, room temperature).

Sound isn't the air itself moving — it's the disturbance traveling through the air. Think of a stadium wave: people stand up and sit down, but they don't travel across the stadium. The wave travels; the medium (the crowd, the air) just wobbles.

Frequency — How Fast It Vibrates

Frequency is the number of compressions that pass a point each second. Measured in Hertz (Hz) — one compression per second is 1 Hz. Human ears can detect frequencies from about 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz (20 kHz).

  • Low frequency = long wavelengths = bass notes
  • High frequency = short wavelengths = treble notes

A 20 Hz wave is about 17 meters long. A 20,000 Hz wave is about 1.7 centimeters. Your ear canal naturally amplifies frequencies around 2,000–5,000 Hz — that's where human speech carries most of its energy. Evolution optimized us for hearing each other.

Amplitude — How Hard It Pushes

Amplitude is the intensity of the pressure variation. Loud sounds push harder on your eardrum. Amplitude is measured in decibels (dB), but decibels are logarithmic — a 10 dB increase means 10 times the intensity; a 20 dB increase means 100 times.

  • 0 dB — threshold of hearing (barely detectable)
  • 60 dB — normal conversation
  • 85 dB — hearing damage begins (prolonged exposure)
  • 120 dB — pain threshold
  • 194 dB — maximum possible sound in Earth's atmosphere before the wave becomes a shock wave

Once you hit around 194 dB, the rarefaction part of the wave creates a vacuum. The wave breaks. It becomes a shock wave — the kind that breaks eardrums and flattens buildings.

The Human Receiver

Your ear is a marvel of biological engineering. Sound enters the ear canal and hits the eardrum (tympanic membrane), causing it to vibrate. Three tiny bones — the malleus, incus, and stapes (hammer, anvil, stirrup) — amplify and transmit those vibrations to the cochlea.

The cochlea is a fluid-filled spiral lined with thousands of hair cells. Different frequencies travel to different positions along the spiral — high frequencies at the base, low frequencies at the apex. When a hair cell vibrates, it sends a signal via the auditory nerve to your brain.

Hair cells don't regenerate. Once they're damaged — from loud concerts, industrial noise, or just aging — they're gone forever. That's why hearing loss is cumulative and irreversible.

Why Two Ears Matter

Two ears let you locate sound. The tiny difference in arrival time (less than a millisecond) and volume between your ears tells your brain which direction a sound came from. Your brain is essentially doing acoustic triangulation in real time.

Digital Sound

Microphones reverse the process: they convert air vibrations into electrical signals. A microphone diaphragm moves back and forth in a magnetic field (dynamic) or changes capacitance (condenser), producing a voltage that represents the sound wave.

To store sound digitally, we sample it — measuring the voltage at regular intervals. The sampling rate (how often we measure) and bit depth (how precisely we measure) determine quality:

  • CD quality — 44,100 Hz sample rate, 16-bit depth
  • Hi-Res audio — 96,000 Hz or higher, 24-bit
  • MP3 — compressed, discards information humans can't hear

The Nyquist theorem says you need to sample at twice the highest frequency you want to capture. That's why CD audio uses 44.1 kHz — it can accurately reproduce frequencies up to 22.05 kHz, just beyond human hearing range.

Compression — Making It Small

Raw audio is huge. A minute of CD-quality stereo is about 10 MB. Compression makes it manageable:

  • Lossless (FLAC, ALAC) — mathematical compression, perfect reproduction
  • Lossy (MP3, AAC, Ogg) — discards "inaudible" information, uses perceptual coding

MP3 exploits flaws in human hearing. If a loud sound masks a quiet one at a nearby frequency, you can't hear the quiet one — so MP3 deletes it. At 128 kbps, MP3 throws away about 90% of the original data. Most people can't tell the difference.

Synthesis — Making Sound From Nothing

Speakers do the opposite of microphones: they convert electrical signals into air vibrations. A speaker cone moves back and forth, pushing air in patterns that match the original sound.

Synthesizers generate sounds from scratch:

  • Additive — combine sine waves at different frequencies
  • Subtractive — filter a harmonically rich source (like a saw wave)
  • FM — modulate one wave's frequency with another
  • Wavetable — play short recorded samples at different pitches

Every sound in electronic music — from 8-bit Nintendo bleeps to stadium-shaking EDM — comes from these techniques.

The Psychoacoustic Layer

Here's where it gets weird. Your brain doesn't just receive sound — it interprets it. Two phenomena:

  • Missing fundamental — play a bass note and its harmonics, but remove the fundamental frequency. Your brain "hears" the missing bass anyway. Speakers can't reproduce 30 Hz? Your brain fills it in.
  • Precedence effect — when a sound reaches your ears with less than ~1ms difference, your brain merges them into one. This is how stereo works — two speakers, one perceived sound source.

Audio engineers exploit these effects constantly. Sub-bass shakers in movies make you "feel" 20 Hz through your chest, even through small speakers that can't actually produce it.

Sound in the Real World

Sound doesn't travel forever. It decays with distance (inverse square law — double the distance, quarter the intensity). It reflects off hard surfaces, absorbs into soft ones. Every room has an acoustic signature.

Acoustic treatment — the foam panels in recording studios, the panels in open-plan offices — is about controlling reflections. Too many hard surfaces = echo. Too many soft surfaces = muffled, dead sound.

The Magic Remains

When you press play on a song, here's what happens:

  1. Digital audio file is read from storage
  2. Digital-to-analog converter (DAC) turns numbers into electrical voltage
  3. Amplifier boosts the voltage
  4. Speaker cone moves, pushing air
  5. Air pressure wave travels to your ear
  6. Eardrum vibrates, hair cells fire, nerve sends signal
  7. Brain interprets pattern as "music"

All of that in milliseconds. The chain is inches long. But it connects the digital realm to your nervous system through nothing but vibrating air.

That's not magic. That's physics. But it still feels like magic.
