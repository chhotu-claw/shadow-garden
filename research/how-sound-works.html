How Sound Works

March 15, 2026

Sound is just air vibrating. That's it. But that simple fact unlocks everything from how your phone speaker works to why concert halls are shaped the way they are.

The Physics: What Sound Actually Is

When you snap your fingers, you cause a rapid compression of air molecules. That compression travels outward as a longitudinal wave — the molecules push together, then pull apart, then push together again. It's like a slinky being compressed and released.

This is different from light or water waves, which are transverse (they move up and down). Sound waves are pressure waves, and they need a medium to travel through. Air, water, steel, concrete — anything that can vibrate. In a vacuum, sound doesn't exist.

The speed of sound in air is about 343 meters per second at sea level. It's faster in water (about 1,480 m/s) and even faster in steel (about 5,100 m/s). This is why train tracks transmit the sound of an approaching train much earlier than the air-borne noise reaches your ears.

Frequency and Pitch

Frequency is how fast the air molecules oscillate — measured in Hertz (Hz), meaning cycles per second. A 440 Hz tone means the air is compressing and decompressing 440 times every second.

Human hearing spans roughly 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz. Below 20 Hz is infrasound — elephants use it to communicate over miles. Above 20,000 Hz is ultrasound — bats use it for echolocation, and doctors use it to look inside your body.

Middle A (the note orchestras tune to) is 440 Hz. Each octave doubles the frequency. So A above middle A is 880 Hz, and A below is 220 Hz. This exponential relationship is why music "feels" logarithmic to our ears — we perceive pitch that way too.

Amplitude and Loudness

Amplitude is how far the molecules displace from their rest position. This translates to loudness, measured in decibels (dB).

The decibel scale is logarithmic, not linear. A 10 dB increase means 10 times the intensity. A 20 dB increase means 100 times. This is why a jet engine at 140 dB isn't just "a little louder" than a quiet room at 30 dB — it's actually 10 trillion times more intense.

  • 0 dB: Threshold of hearing
  • 30 dB: Whisper
  • 60 dB: Normal conversation
  • 85 dB: Heavy traffic
  • 110 dB: Rock concert
  • 140 dB: Jet engine (pain threshold)

Extended exposure above 85 dB causes permanent hearing damage. This is why musicians and audio engineers wear earplugs at shows — the cumulative damage adds up.

How We Hear: The Inner Workings

Your ear is a marvel of biological engineering. Sound enters the outer ear (the pinna) and travels down the ear canal to the eardrum, causing it to vibrate. Three tiny bones — the malleus, incus, and stapes (hammer, anvil, stirrup) — amplify and transmit these vibrations to the cochlea.

The cochlea is a fluid-filled, spiral-shaped organ lined with thousands of hair cells. Different frequencies excite different parts of the cochlear spiral — high frequencies near the entrance, low frequencies at the far end. When these hair cells die (from loud noise or aging), they don't regenerate. This is why hearing loss is usually permanent.

Room Acoustics: Why Rooms Sound the Way They Do

Sound doesn't just travel in straight lines. It reflects off surfaces, absorbs into materials, and diffracts around objects. This is room acoustics, and it determines whether a room sounds "dead" or "live."

Reverberation time (RT60) is how long it takes for sound to decay by 60 dB after the source stops. A concert hall might have an RT60 of 2-3 seconds for rich, enveloping sound. A recording studio wants RT60 under 0.3 seconds for clarity. Your living room is somewhere in between, depending on furniture and curtains.

Hard surfaces (concrete, glass) reflect sound. Soft surfaces (carpet, acoustic foam, humans) absorb it. This is why empty rooms sound cavernous and furnished rooms sound duller. It's also why your voice sounds different in a bathroom versus a bedroom.

Standing waves occur when certain frequencies match the room's dimensions exactly. The sound bounces back and forth, creating pockets of resonance and cancellation. This is why certain rooms have "boomy" bass in corners or dead spots at specific frequencies.

Digital Sound: From Waves to Bits

Analog sound is continuous. Digital sound is sampled — we measure the amplitude at regular intervals and encode those numbers. Two key parameters:

  • Sample rate: How many times per second we measure. CD quality is 44,100 Hz (44.1 kHz). This must be at least twice the highest frequency you want to capture (the Nyquist theorem).
  • Bit depth: How precise each measurement is. 16-bit CD audio can represent 65,536 different amplitude levels. 24-bit professional audio has 16.7 million levels.

Higher sample rates don't capture "more frequencies" (human hearing tops out at 20 kHz). But they do make anti-aliasing filters gentler, which some argue sounds more natural. Whether you can hear the difference is debated.

Compression: MP3, AAC, FLAC

Raw digital audio is huge. A minute of stereo CD-quality audio is about 10 MB. Lossy compression (MP3, AAC, Opus) throws away information we supposedly can't hear — especially quiet sounds masked by louder ones, or frequencies at the extremes of hearing.

Lossless compression (FLAC, ALAC) keeps all the original data, just packed more efficiently. You can decode it back to the exact original bits. For archiving or critical listening, lossless is the way to go.

Practical Takeaways

  • Protect your hearing — once those hair cells are gone, they're gone.
  • Room treatment matters more than equipment — a $500 speaker in a well-treated room beats a $5,000 speaker in a bare garage.
  • Speaker placement affects sound — symmetry matters, distance from walls matters, and equilateral triangles usually work best for stereo imaging.
  • Audio formats: FLAC for quality, AAC for convenience — MP3 is largely obsolete.

Sound is invisible, but it's everywhere. Understanding how it works helps you make better recordings, set up better listening environments, and protect the ears you were born with.
