How the Stock Market Actually Works

March 6, 2026

Most people think the stock market is a place where you buy and sell pieces of companies. That's technically true, but it's like saying "the ocean is where fish live." You're missing the depth.

The stock market is a complex ecosystem of exchanges, participants, and mechanisms that collectively determine what companies are worth — minute by minute, down to the millisecond. Understanding how it actually works reveals why "the market" is a fiction, why "price discovery" is harder than it sounds, and why your broker might not be who you think they are.

What Is a Stock, Really?

A stock represents ownership in a company. When you buy one share of Apple, you own a tiny slice of Apple — theoretically, you have voting rights, claim on assets, and a claim on future profits (dividends, if any).

But here's the wrinkle: the vast majority of trading isn't about ownership. It's about speculation. The average holding period for a stock on the NYSE is now less than six months. Most traders don't want to own companies — they want to own the price movement.

Exchanges: The Meeting Place

When people say "the stock market," they usually mean the exchanges: NYSE, NASDAQ, CBOE, and dozens of others worldwide. An exchange is simply a marketplace — a place where buyers and sellers come together.

But here's what surprises most people: there isn't one price for a stock. The price on NYSE might differ slightly from the price on NASDAQ. Prices on "dark pools" (more on those later) might differ even more. The "price" you see on your screen is just one snapshot from one venue — not the definitive value of the company.

How an Exchange Works: The Order Book

Every exchange maintains an order book — a live list of all buy and sell orders waiting to be filled. It looks something like this (simplified):

BID (Buy Orders)              ASK (Sell Orders)
Price     Size                   Price     Size
$100.00   500 shares             $100.05   200 shares
$99.95    1,200 shares           $100.10   800 shares
$99.90    2,000 shares           $100.15   1,500 shares

The bid is the highest price someone's willing to buy at. The ask (or "offer") is the lowest price someone's willing to sell at. The difference between them is the spread.

When a buyer is willing to pay the ask price, the trade executes instantly. The order is "marketable" — it can be filled immediately. Orders sitting in the book are "limit orders" — they'll only execute if the price comes to them.

Market Makers: The Always-There Counterparty

Here's a dirty secret: most trades don't match a human buyer with a human seller. They match with a market maker — a firm (often a hedge fund or investment bank) that stands ready to buy when you want to sell and sell when you want to buy.

Market makers provide liquidity — they ensure there's always someone on the other side of your trade. In exchange, they capture the spread. If the bid is $100 and the ask is $100.05, the market maker buys at $100, sells at $100.05, and keeps the $0.05 per share as profit.

This seems small, but it adds up. Market makers can trade millions of shares per day. The spread is their salary.

How Prices Move: Supply and Demand, But Complicated

You learned in economics class: prices go up when demand exceeds supply. In the stock market, this is technically true — but the mechanics are messier.

Price Impact

When you place a large order to buy, you push the price up. Why? Because your order "walks up" the order book — it eats through the available asks:

  1. You want to buy 10,000 shares of a stock
  2. The ask has 500 shares at $100.05 — you buy those
  3. Then 800 shares at $100.10 — you buy those
  4. Then 1,500 shares at $100.15 — you buy those
  5. By the time you're done, you've moved the price up

This is called price impact. Large institutional investors (pension funds, hedge funds) don't just "place an order." They slice their orders into small pieces, spread across hours or days, to minimize impact. This is called order slicing or algo execution.

High-Frequency Trading: Speed as a Strategy

Some traders try to profit from speed. High-frequency trading (HFT) firms use dedicated fiber lines, colocation (servers placed inches from exchange computers), and algorithmic strategies to capture tiny price discrepancies.

Common HFT strategies include:

  • Market making — providing liquidity and capturing the spread
  • Statistical arbitrage — finding tiny price correlations between related securities and betting they'll converge
  • Front-running — detecting a large order in the book and jumping ahead of it (controversial, sometimes illegal)

HFT firms argument that they provide liquidity and narrow spreads. Critics argue they create phantom volatility and give speed advantages to those with the most money. Both are true.

Dark Pools: Where the Big Money Hides

About 40% of US equity trading happens in dark pools — private exchanges (or "alternative trading systems") where orders are hidden. You can't see the order book. You can't see prices until a trade executes.

Why does this exist? Because large investors don't want to reveal their intentions. If a pension fund wants to buy $100 million of a stock, announcing that publicly would move the price against them. Dark pools let them trade in privacy.

The controversy: this creates a two-tiered market. Retail investors (like you and me) trade on public exchanges, where our orders are visible. Big institutions trade in dark pools, where we can't see what they're doing. The "price" on your screen might not reflect what the big players are actually doing.

Short Selling: Betting Against

You can make money when stocks go down. This is called short selling, and it works like this:

  1. You borrow 100 shares from your broker
  2. You sell them at $100 (you get $10,000 cash)
  3. The price falls to $80
  4. You buy 100 shares back at $80 ($8,000)
  5. You return the borrowed shares to your broker
  6. You keep the $2,000 difference

The catch: if the price goes up, your losses are unlimited. You borrowed shares — you have to return them eventually. If the stock goes to $200, you lose $10,000. There's no ceiling.

Short sellers were blamed (rightly or wrongly) for amplifying crashes, including the 2008 financial crisis. When a stock is heavily shorted and something goes wrong, short sellers can pile in, creating a feedback loop that drives the price to zero. This is called a short squeeze when it goes the other way — see GameStop in 2021.

Broker-Dealers: Who You Actually Trade With

When you click "buy" on Robinhood or Fidelity, you think you're buying from another person. Usually, you're not. Most retail brokers use payment for order flow — they send your order to a market maker, who pays them for the right to fill it.

This sounds corrupt, but it's legal and common. The market maker is willing to pay for your order because they can often fill it at a better price than the public exchange — they have better technology and access. The broker passes some of this savings to you (often) or keeps it (sometimes).

The point: your broker isn't your friend. They're a intermediary who makes money from your trades. Understand how they make money, and you'll be a better trader.

The Index Fund Problem

Here's a strange paradox: most money in the stock market isn't managed by humans. It's in index funds — passive funds that just hold everything in the S&P 500 or Total Market.

This creates a strange dynamic. When you buy "the market," you're not actually evaluating companies. You're just buying everything. The price of Apple isn't determined by people who think Apple is a good company — it's determined by the collective weight of millions of investors, many of whom never looked at Apple's earnings.

This is called index cannibalization. As more money flows into passive funds, less money is available for active analysis. Eventually, the price signals that drive capital allocation become detached from actual company performance. This is one reason behind the "market concentration" concerns — the biggest companies get the most index money, regardless of merit.

What Actually Moves Prices

If you think stock prices reflect company fundamentals, here's a reality check:

  • Earnings — yes, eventually
  • News — but often the wrong interpretation of news
  • Sentiment — fear and greed, in mass
  • Liquidity — when money floods in, prices rise; when it leaves, they fall
  • Technical factors — momentum, rebalancing, index inflows
  • Randomness — a non-trivial portion of daily moves is noise

The efficient market hypothesis says prices reflect all available information. In practice, prices reflect the interpretation of information by fallible humans (and algorithms) with different incentives, time horizons, and access to capital.

The Takeaway

The stock market isn't a machine. It's a battleground of incentives, speed advantages, hidden information, and human psychology. The "price" you see is just one number from one venue at one moment. The real market — where the big money moves — is largely invisible to retail investors.

This doesn't mean you shouldn't participate. But understanding what you're actually participating in changes how you think about "buying the dip," "following the trend," and "the wisdom of the crowd." The crowd, it turns out, is often wrong — and the people who profit most are those who understand the game's hidden rules.
