Neural networks are everywhere now. They're in your phone, your camera, your translate app, and probably your toaster (okay, maybe not the toaster yet). But how do they actually work?

The answer is both simpler and weirder than you'd expect. It's not magic. It's math вЂ” relentless, iterative, gradient-descent math. Let's trace it from the ground up.

The Neuron: A Fancy Weighted Sum

At the heart of every neural network is the neuron (also called a node or unit). It does one thing: takes some inputs, multiplies each by a weight, adds them up, and passes the result through a function.

  • w = weights (these are what gets "learned")
  • b = bias (a shift value, also learned)
  • f = activation function (the non-linearity)

The weights start random. The network is dumb. Through training, those weights adjust to make the neuron fire for the right inputs.

Activation Functions: The Gatekeepers

Why do we need an activation function? Let's trace what happens without one. If you just sum inputs, stacked layers collapse into a single linear transformation. You couldn't learn complex patterns вЂ” you'd just be doing glorified regression.

Activation functions introduce non-linearity. That's where the magic happens.

ReLU dominates today because it's computationally cheap and avoids the "vanishing gradient" problem that killed early neural network research in the 80s.

The Architecture: Layers

Neurons are organized into layers:

  • Input layer вЂ” your data goes in (e.g., 784 pixels for MNIST digits)
  • Hidden layers вЂ” the middle stuff. One hidden layer can approximate any function. Deep networks are more efficient.
  • Output layer вЂ” the prediction (e.g., 10 classes for digits 0-9)

Forward Propagation: Making a Guess

When you pass input through the network to get an output, that's forward propagation. Simple matrix multiplication, layer by layer:

Backpropagation: The Magic

Here's where it gets weird. Backpropagation (short for "backward propagation of errors") is the algorithm that tells each weight how to change to reduce the loss.

You compute the gradient of the loss with respect to each weight, starting from the output layer and working backward. That's why it's called backpropagation.

Think of it like a mountain climber in fog. You can't see the whole landscape. All you can do is feel the slope under your feet and take a small step downhill. Repeat a billion times, and you might reach the valley.

The "step size" is the learning rate. Too big, you overshoot. Too small, it takes forever.

Training Loop

Put it all together:

for epoch in range(num_epochs):
    for batch in data_loader:
        # 1. Forward pass

This runs on GPUs because the matrix multiplications are parallelizable to hell. A single forward pass on ImageNet (1.2M images, 60M parameters) takes about 100ms on a good GPU. Doing it on CPU would take hours.

Why It Works (Sort Of)

Theз™ѕдё‡-dollar question: why does this absurd process of random matrix multiplications and gradient descent actually produce intelligent behavior?

The honest answer: we're still figuring it out. But we know some things:

  • Universal approximation вЂ” a single hidden layer with enough neurons can approximate any continuous function. Mathematically proven.
  • Gradient descent finds local minima вЂ” in high-dimensional spaces, local minima are rare. Most critical points are saddle points.
  • Regularization вЂ” dropout, weight decay, batch norm help the network generalize beyond memorization.
  • Scale works вЂ” bigger networks + more data + more compute = better results. We don't fully know why.
The universe has 10вЃёвЃ° atoms. GPT-4 has ~1.7Г—10В№ВІ parameters. We're building systems with more adjustable parts than there are atoms in the observable universe, and we're training them by gradient descent on the internet. The fact that this works at all is either a miracle or a sign we should rethink what "intelligence" means.

What's Next

Neural networks are the substrate, but the field moves fast:

  • Transformers вЂ” the attention mechanism changed everything. GPT, BERT, etc.
  • Diffusion models вЂ” generating images from noise (Stable Diffusion, DALL-E)
  • Self-supervised learning вЂ” pretraining on unlabeled data, fine-tuning on small labeled sets
  • Scaling laws вЂ” more parameters + more data = predictable improvement (until it doesn't)

The fundamental algorithm hasn't changed since 1986 (backprop + gradient descent). Everything else is engineering.
