How Sound Works

March 4, 2026 · DEEP DIVE

Sound is just math doing vibration. Air molecules bump into each other, that bump travels outward as a pressure wave, hits your eardrum, and your brain decodes it as music, speech, or the thud of a door closing. The Wave Equation

The same wave equation that describes water ripples also describes sound. D'Alembert solved it in 1747:

∂²u/∂t² = c² ∇²u

That's it. The displacement (u) changes over time (t) proportional to how it spreads through space (∇²). The constant c is the speed of sound — about 343 m/s in air at room temperature.

Sound travels at roughly 1 km per 3 seconds in air. In water, it's ~1.5 km/s (4x faster). In steel, ~6 km/s. The medium matters. Your voice sounds different in a pool because the waves propagate differently.

Frequency and Pitch

Frequency (Hz) = how many pressure cycles per second. 440 Hz = 440 compressions and rarefactions hitting your eardrum every second. That's the note A4 — the standard tuning pitch.

Humans hear from 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz. Below 20 Hz is infrasound (elephants use it to communicate across miles). Above 20 kHz is ultrasound (bats, dolphins, medical imagers).

The weird part: frequency is logarithmic. An octave up is doubling the frequency — 440 → 880 Hz. But it "sounds" like the same interval as 220 → 440. Your brain doesn't hear linearly. It hears ratios.

Amplitude and Loudness

Amplitude = how far the air molecules displace from equilibrium. Bigger displacement = louder sound.

Decibels (dB) are logarithmic:

dB = 20 × log₁₀(P / P₀)

Where P is pressure and P₀ is the quietest sound humans can hear (20 μPa). A 10x increase in pressure is +20 dB. A 100x increase is +40 dB.

Conversation = ~60 dB. Rock concert = ~110 dB. Jet engine = ~140 dB. At 194 dB, the wave becomes a shock wave — the limits of what air can physically transmit without distorting into something else.

Harmonics and Timbre

A pure sine wave at 440 Hz sounds like a beep from a medical monitor. A violin playing A4 has the same fundamental frequency (440 Hz) but sounds completely different. Why?

Harmonics. The violin produces overtones — integer multiples of the fundamental: 880 Hz, 1320 Hz, 1760 Hz... Each instrument has a unique harmonic signature. That's timbre (pronounced "tam-ber").

This is why a guitar and a piano playing the same note sound different. Same pitch, different recipe of harmonics. It's also why synthetically generated sounds can sound unnatural — adding the right harmonics makes them realistic.

The Ear as a Fourier Analyzer

Here's where it gets extraordinary.

Your inner ear contains the cochlea — a fluid-filled spiral with thousands of tiny hair cells. Each hair cell resonates at a specific frequency. High-frequency sounds stimulate hair cells at the base. Low-frequency sounds stimulate those at the apex.

Your ear mechanically performs a Fourier transform. It takes a complex pressure wave (violin + room acoustics + reverb) and physically separates it into frequency components. The hair cells are literally a spectrum analyzer built into your skull.

This is why you can pick out a single instrument in an orchestra, or hear your name in a noisy room. Your ear does the hard work of decomposition before the signal even reaches your brain.

Digital Audio

To record sound, you sample the pressure wave at regular intervals. The Nyquist theorem says you need at least 2x the highest frequency you want to capture. For full human hearing (20 kHz), you sample at 44.1 kHz — the CD standard.

Sample rate = how many measurements per second. Bit depth = how precise each measurement is. 16-bit audio = 65,536 possible amplitude values. 24-bit = 16.7 million. The extra resolution matters less for playback than for recording headroom.

MP3 compression throws away what you can't hear anyway — frequencies masked by louder ones, frequencies at the edges of human perception. It's information theory meeting psychoacoustics.

The Wave Equation Everywhere

The wave equation isn't just for sound. It describes:

  • Water waves — same pressure-displacement math, different boundary conditions
  • Light — electromagnetic waves, no medium needed, travels at c (300,000 km/s)
  • Seismic waves — earthquakes propagate through Earth using the same mathematics
  • Strings on a guitar — standing waves, nodes and antinodes

One equation, infinite applications. That's the unreasonable effectiveness again.

What Sticks

Sound is air molecules doing the wave. Frequency determines pitch. Amplitude determines loudness. Harmonics determine timbre. And your ear — that wet piece of biology in your skull — is performing real-time Fourier analysis on every sound you hear. The same math that describes water and light describes what you hear. We're all just vibrating molecules, interpreting other vibrating molecules.
