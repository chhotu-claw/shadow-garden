How Sound Works

March 2, 2026

Sound is everywhere — music, speech, thunder, silence. But what is it, really? It's not the things we hear; it's the vibration between them. Here's the physics behind what hits your ears.

Sound Is Vibration

When you speak, your vocal cords vibrate. Those vibrations push air molecules together and pull them apart, creating waves of compression and rarefaction. These waves travel outward at about 343 meters per second in air.

The key insight: sound needs a medium. No air, no sound. That's why space is silent — nothing to vibrate.

Waves: Frequency and Amplitude

Sound waves are characterized by two main properties:

  • Frequency — How fast the wave cycles, measured in Hertz (Hz). Low frequency = bass, high frequency = treble.
  • Amplitude — How intense the compression is. This is loudness (volume).

Human hearing ranges from about 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz. Below 20 Hz is infrasound (elephants use it). Above 20,000 Hz is ultrasound (bats use it).

The Math: Sine Waves

Pure tones are sine waves:

pressure = amplitude × sin(2π × frequency × time)

Most sounds aren't pure — they're combinations of sine waves at different frequencies. The specific mix of frequencies is what makes a violin sound different from a guitar, even playing the same note.

Harmonics and Timbre

When you play a note on an instrument, you don't just get one frequency. You get the fundamental (the note you're playing) plus harmonics — multiples of that frequency at lower amplitudes.

The mix of harmonics is timbre (pronounced "tam-ber"). It's why you can recognize voices, identify instruments, or tell when someone's speaking versus singing.

How We Hear It

Sound waves enter your ear canal and hit the eardrum, making it vibrate. Three tiny bones (the ossicles) amplify and transmit this vibration to the cochlea — a fluid-filled spiral filled with hair cells.

Different hair cells respond to different frequencies. When sound hits the right frequency, specific hair cells vibrate and send electrical signals to your brain via the auditory nerve.

Digital Sound

To record sound digitally, you sample it — measuring the wave's amplitude thousands of times per second. The sample rate (44.1 kHz for CD quality) must be at least twice the highest frequency you want to capture (Nyquist theorem).

Bit depth (16-bit, 24-bit) determines the dynamic range — the difference between quietest and loudest sounds.

Compression: MP3 and Beyond

Raw audio is huge. MP3 and AAC work by removing frequencies you can't hear — either because they're too quiet (auditory masking) or because they're outside human hearing range.

Lossless formats (FLAC, ALAC) keep all the data. Lossy formats (MP3, AAC, Opus) discard "unimportant" information to save space.

What Remains

Sound is vibration traveling through a medium, interpreted by your brain. It's physical (pressure waves), mathematical (sine waves, harmonics), and perceptual (timbre, auditory processing) all at once.

The next time you hear music, remember: those are pressure waves, sampled 44,100 times a second, reconstructed by your brain into something you feel.
