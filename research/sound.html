How Sound Works

March 3, 2026

Sound is vibration traveling through a medium — air, water, steel, your skull. What you "hear" is your eardrum being pushed back and forth by pressure waves. The brain turns that mechanical motion into the experience of hearing. But the physics underneath is surprisingly simple math.

Wave Basics: Frequency and Amplitude

Every sound can be described by two numbers: frequency and amplitude.

Frequency — how fast the wave oscillates — determines pitch. Measured in Hertz (Hz), or cycles per second. A 440 Hz tone is the note A4 (the tuning standard). Human hearing spans roughly 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz. Below 20 Hz is infrasound (elephants use it to communicate across miles). Above 20 kHz is ultrasound (bats, dolphins, your dog's confused stare when you blow that dog whistle).

Amplitude — how tall the wave is — determines loudness. Decibels (dB) are logarithmic: a 10 dB increase is 10× the power, 20 dB is 100×, 30 dB is 1,000×. Your threshold of pain hovers around 120 dB. Silence is technically 0 dB (the quietest sound a healthy human ear can detect).

Harmonics: The Reason Instruments Sound Different

A pure sine wave — one frequency, one note — sounds like a beep from a medical monitor. Boring. Real instruments produce harmonics: the fundamental frequency plus integer multiples of it (2×, 3×, 4×...).

A violin playing A4 produces 440 Hz (the fundamental), plus 880 Hz, 1320 Hz, 1760 Hz... each at different volumes. A piano playing the same note has a different harmonic mix. That's why they sound different, even though they're playing the same pitch. Your ear is a biological Fourier analyzer — it automatically breaks complex sounds into frequency components without you needing to think about it.

This is also why adding "warmth" or "brightness" to audio is really just boosting certain harmonics. Audio engineering is applied math disguised as art.

How We Hear: The Biological Fourier Transform

Your ear doesn't just catch vibrations — it performs real-time signal processing. Sound enters through the outer ear, vibrates the eardrum, which moves three tiny bones (the ossicles: malleus, incus, stapes — Latin for hammer, anvil, stirrup because that's what they look like). Those bones amplify and transmit the vibration to the cochlea, a fluid-filled spiral lined with thousands of hair cells.

Each hair cell responds to a specific frequency. High frequencies stimulate hair cells at the base of the cochlea; low frequencies reach the apex. Damage to hair cells — from loud concerts, aging, or just bad luck — causes hearing loss at specific frequencies. This is why audiograms look the way they do: notches where certain hair cells died.

The fact that your ear does Fourier analysis mechanically, without any conscious thought, is remarkable. You are a signal processing machine running on biology.

Digital Audio: Sampling and Quantization

To record sound digitally, you sample — measure the amplitude at regular intervals. The Nyquist theorem says you need at least 2× the highest frequency you want to capture. To capture full human hearing (20 Hz to 20 kHz), you sample at at least 40 kHz. CD audio uses 44.1 kHz — that's why. (Audiophiles argue about whether 96 kHz or 192 kHz matters. Mathematically: no. Practically: maybe, for engineering reasons, but not because you can hear above 20 kHz.)

Bit depth — 16-bit audio gives 65,536 possible amplitude values, 24-bit gives 16.7 million. The difference is in dynamic range: the gap between the quietest and loudest sound. 16-bit is fine for most purposes. 24-bit is better for recording studios where you're capturing quiet details alongside loud peaks.

Compression: MP3 and Its Cousins

Raw audio is huge. A minute of CD-quality stereo is about 10 MB. MP3 compression exploits two things: psychoacoustics (what you can't hear) and redundancy (what's predictable).

MP3 throws away frequencies you can't hear due to masking: a loud sound makes nearby frequencies inaudible. It also uses temporal masking — a loud sound briefly drowns out quieter sounds that come right after. Your ear/brain fills in gaps that aren't really there. MP3 exploits this systematically.

Lossless formats (FLAC, ALAC) keep all the data. Lossy formats (MP3, AAC, OGG) are approximations your brain approves of.

The Interesting Part

Your ear is a biological Fourier analyzer. You perform real-time signal processing without thinking about it.

Sound is just air pressure oscillating. But your experience of music, speech, a loved one's voice, a warning alarm — all of that emerges from biology doing math. The same wave equation that describes sound describes light, water, and seismic activity. Math doesn't care what medium it travels through. That's the beauty and strangeness of physics: the same equations describe everything.
