How WiFi Works

2026-03-14

WiFi is one of those things we use every day without thinking about it. But underneath that little icon in your corner lies a surprisingly complex dance of radio waves, timing slots, and negotiation between devices. Let's take it apart.

The Foundation: Radio Waves

WiFi is radio. That's it. Your router shoots electromagnetic waves into the air, your device catches them, and they trade data back and forth. But not just any radio — WiFi operates on specific frequencies that are unlicensed (free to use, no license required) and relatively high frequency.

The two main bands you'll encounter:

  • 2.4 GHz — Lower frequency, longer range, better at penetrating walls. Slower max speeds but reaches farther. Older devices, smart home gadgets, and legacy everything use this.
  • 5 GHz — Higher frequency, shorter range, faster speeds. Less congestion, more bandwidth, but walls kill it. This is where modern WiFi lives.

There's also 6 GHz (WiFi 6E and WiFi 7), which is even faster but has even shorter range — basically line-of-sight territory.

The 802.11 Family

WiFi isn't a single standard. It's a family of evolving protocols, each with a number:

  • 802.11b/g — The old stuff. 2.4 GHz only, max speeds around 11-54 Mbps. If you have a device from 2005, it might still use this.
  • 802.11n (WiFi 4) — Introduced MIMO (multiple inputs, multiple outputs). Could use both 2.4 and 5 GHz. Up to 600 Mbps.
  • 802.11ac (WiFi 5) — 5 GHz only (mostly), faster speeds, beamforming. Up to 3.5 Gbps in theory.
  • 802.11ax (WiFi 6) — The current standard. Better handling of many devices (OFDMA), target wake time for battery savings, 9.6 Gbps max.
  • 802.11be (WiFi 7) — The bleeding edge. Multi-link operation (uses 2.4, 5, and 6 GHz simultaneously), 30 Gbps speeds. Expensive, rare hardware.

How Data Moves Through the Air

Modulation: Turning Data Into Waves

Your router doesn't just blast raw 1s and 0s. It modulates the radio wave to encode information. The current standard is OFDM (Orthogonal Frequency-Division Multiplexing) and its successor OFDMA (Orthogonal Frequency-Division Multiple Access).

Think of it like this: instead of one lane of traffic, you split the road into hundreds of smaller sub-channels. Each one carries a tiny piece of the data, and they're packed tightly together (that's the "orthogonal" part — they're mathematically arranged so they don't interfere).

WiFi 6 improves on this with OFDMA, which lets multiple devices share a channel simultaneously. Previously, each device had to wait its turn. Now, your phone, laptop, and smart fridge can all talk at the same time on the same channel.

CSMA/CA: The "Don't Talk Over Each Other" Protocol

Since everyone shares the same airwaves, there's a problem: what happens when two devices try to talk at once? WiFi solves this with CSMA/CA — Carrier Sense Multiple Access with Collision Avoidance.

Before transmitting, your device listens. If it hears anything, it waits. If the channel is clear, it sends — but also includes a tiny waiting period in case someone else decides to transmit at the same time. This is why WiFi slows down with more devices: everyone is constantly negotiating who goes next.

MIMO: Multiple Antennas, More Data

Modern routers have multiple antennas. This isn't just for show — it's MIMO (Multiple Input, Multiple Output). By using multiple antennas, the router can send different data streams simultaneously. More antennas = more parallel data = faster speeds.

MU-MIMO (Multi-User MIMO) takes this further: instead of serving one device at a time, the router can serve multiple devices at once. WiFi 6 added this, and WiFi 7 makes it even better.

Beamforming: Focusing the Signal

Old WiFi broadcast in all directions equally. Modern routers use beamforming — they detect where your device is and focus the signal in that direction. It's like a spotlight instead of a lightbulb. The signal is stronger where you need it.

Why Your WiFi Sucks

Now that you understand how it works, you can diagnose why your connection is terrible:

  • Distance — WiFi follows the inverse-square law. Double the distance, quarter the signal.
  • Interference — 2.4 GHz is crowded. Microwaves, cordless phones, Bluetooth, and your neighbor's WiFi all compete.
  • Channel congestion — In apartment buildings, everyone is on the same channels. Use a scanner to find an empty one.
  • Obstructions — Walls, metal, water (your body!), and even furniture absorb or reflect signals.
  • Too many devices — Each device takes a turn. Twenty devices = twenty turns = slower for everyone.
  • Old hardware — If your router is from 2015, it's probably 802.11n or older. Newer standards make a huge difference.

Security: WPA3 and Before

WiFi security has evolved:

  • WEP — Broken in minutes. Don't use.
  • WPA — Temporary fix, also broken.
  • WPA2 — The standard for years. Still mostly fine, but vulnerable to brute force on weak passwords.
  • WPA3 — Current standard. Better encryption, protects against offline attacks, easier setup for IoT devices.

If you're setting up WiFi today, use WPA3 (or WPA2 with a strong password). Avoid open networks unless you use a VPN.

The Future: WiFi 7 and Beyond

WiFi 7 (802.11be) is starting to roll out. The big changes:

  • Multi-Link Operation (MLO) — Uses 2.4, 5, and 6 GHz simultaneously. If one band is congested, it uses the others. Massive latency reduction.
  • 320 MHz channels — Double the bandwidth of WiFi 6. More data, faster.
  • 4K QAM — Denser modulation. More data per wave. Requires high-quality hardware.

But honestly, for most people, WiFi 6 is already overkill. The bottleneck is usually the internet connection, not the local network.

What Actually Matters

Here's the practical takeaway:

  • Use 5 GHz (or 6 GHz) when you can. It's faster and less congested.
  • Place your router centrally. WiFi is omnidirectional — it goes everywhere equally.
  • Don't hide your router in a cabinet. Antennas need line of sight.
  • Restart your router occasionally. Memory leaks happen.
  • If your house is big, consider mesh WiFi or wired access points. One router can only do so much.
  • Check what channels are congested. Apps like WiFi Analyzer show you.

WiFi is magical when it works, infuriating when it doesn't. Now you know why.
