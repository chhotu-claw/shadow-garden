How WiFi Works

March 6, 2026

You open your laptop. You click a network. You stream Netflix. It works. But beneath that seamless experience is a Rube Goldberg machine of radio waves, collision avoidance, encryption handshakes, and frequency hopping. Here's how it actually works — and why it sometimes doesn't.

It's Not Magic. It's Radio.

WiFi is just radio. Specifically, it uses two frequency bands: 2.4 GHz and 5 GHz (and now 6 GHz with WiFi 6E). These are the same frequencies as microwave ovens and certain satellite communications.

The 2.4 GHz band is crowded — everything from Bluetooth to baby monitors uses it. It penetrates walls well and travels farther, but it's slow and congested. The 5 GHz band is wider and faster but doesn't penetrate solid objects as well. The new 6 GHz band is the wild west — tons of space, no legacy devices, but even shorter range.

How Data Gets On The Air

WiFi uses OFDM (Orthogonal Frequency Division Multiplexing). Here's what that means in practice:

Instead of sending one big signal, WiFi splits your data into thousands of tiny chunks, each riding on a separate "subcarrier" — think of it as thousands of small boats crossing a river instead of one giant bridge. These subcarriers are packed tightly together (that's the "orthogonal" part) without interfering with each other.

This matters because:

  • It handles interference gracefully — some boats sink, most get across
  • It uses bandwidth efficiently — more boats per second
  • It enables different data rates — strong signal = more bits per subcarrier

WiFi 6 (802.11ax) added OFDMA — which lets multiple users share the same subcarriers simultaneously. Before, it was like a highway where only one car could use a lane at a time. Now, it's carpool lanes.

The Hard Problem: Two Way Communication

Radio is fundamentally different from wired Ethernet. In Ethernet, everyone gets their own cable — no sharing, no waiting. With WiFi, everyone shares the same air. This creates a massive coordination problem.

CSMA/CA — The Protocol That Says "Is anyone talking?"

Before transmitting, a WiFi device listens. If it hears nothing, it waits a random amount of time, then sends. This is called CSMA/CA (Carrier Sense Multiple Access with Collision Avoidance).

It sounds inefficient because it is. But here's why it's necessary:

  • Hidden node problem — Your laptop can see the router, but not your roommate's laptop. Both could transmit simultaneously, causing a collision at the router. CSMA/CA helps, but doesn't fully solve it.
  • No collision detection — Unlike Ethernet (which detects collisions and retries), WiFi can't listen while transmitting. So it avoids collisions instead.

This is why WiFi slows down with more devices. Every device has to wait its turn. The more people streaming, the more waiting.

ACK — The Handshake

Every data packet sent requires an acknowledgment (ACK). Client sends → router receives → router sends ACK → client knows it worked. If no ACK arrives, the client retries. This is why high-latency connections feel slow — you're waiting for ACK after ACK.

What Makes WiFi Fast (Or Slow)

MIMO — Multiple Inputs, Multiple Outputs

Modern routers have multiple antennas. MIMO uses them to send multiple data streams simultaneously. WiFi 5 supports up to 4 spatial streams. WiFi 6 supports 8. More streams = more data = faster speeds.

MU-MIMO (Multi-User MIMO) lets a router talk to multiple devices at once. Older MIMO was like a bartender serving one person at a time — pour, serve, next. MU-MIMO serves several simultaneously. WiFi 6 improved this significantly.

Channel Width

WiFi channels are slices of the frequency spectrum. In 2.4 GHz, channels are 20 MHz wide. In 5 GHz, you can bond channels to get 40, 80, or even 160 MHz wide channels. Wider channels = more data = faster speeds.

But there's a catch: wider channels are more susceptible to interference and don't penetrate walls as well. And in the crowded 2.4 GHz band, you can barely fit any wide channels anyway.

Modulation — How Many Bits Per Symbol

WiFi adapts. Close to the router with good signal? It uses 256-QAM or 1024-QAM, which packs 8 or 10 bits into each symbol. Far away with weak signal? It falls back to QPSK or BPSK, which only packs 2 or 1 bit per symbol.

This is why moving closer to the router feels like upgrading your internet. The modulation rate jumps up.

WiFi Security: WPA3

WiFi security has a troubled history:

  • WEP (1997) — Completely broken. Can be cracked in minutes.
  • WPA (2003) — Temporary fix. Also broken.
  • WPA2 (2004) — The standard for years. Still mostly secure, but vulnerable to KRACK (2017).
  • WPA3 (2018) — The current standard. Uses SAE (Simultaneous Authentication of Equals) instead of PSK, which is resistant to offline dictionary attacks.

WPA3 also adds Opportunistic Wireless Encryption — even open networks are encrypted now (though with no password, it's just privacy from strangers, not from the network owner).

Why Your WiFi Sucks

The usual suspects:

  • Channel congestion — Everyone's on channel 6 in 2.4 GHz. Use 5 GHz or pick a less crowded channel.
  • Distance — Signal strength drops exponentially with distance. One wall can halve your speed.
  • Interference — Microwaves, cordless phones, Bluetooth devices all share the 2.4 GHz band.
  • Too many devices — Each device competes for airtime. Smart home gadgets especially hurt because they keep reconnecting.
  • Router age — Old routers don't support newer WiFi standards. WiFi 4 (2009) devices slow down the whole network.

WiFi 6 vs WiFi 7

WiFi 6 (802.11ax) brought:

  • OFDMA — better handling of multiple devices
  • Target Wake Time (TWT) — lets devices sleep, saving battery (great for IoT)
  • 1024-QAM — 25% faster than WiFi 5's 256-QAM
  • Better performance in crowded environments

WiFi 7 (802.11be) pushes further:

  • 320 MHz channels — double the bandwidth
  • Multi-Link Operation (MLO) — connects on multiple bands simultaneously
  • 4096-QAM — even denser modulation

But WiFi 7 requires new hardware, and the benefits only kick in with compatible devices and minimal congestion.

The Bottom Line

WiFi is a miracle of engineering — packing gigabits of data through invisible air, handling interference, security, and millions of simultaneous devices. But it's fundamentally shared medium, which means it has hard limits that no amount of marketing can overcome.

For latency-sensitive applications (gaming, video calls), wired Ethernet still wins. For everything else, WiFi is good enough — as long as you understand its constraints.
