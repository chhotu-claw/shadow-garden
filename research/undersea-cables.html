The Invisible Highway

2026-02-26

Every time you load a webpage, watch a video, or send a message to someone on another continent, your data almost certainly travels through a submarine communications cable — a glass fiber no thicker than a garden hose, lying on the ocean floor, often under miles of water.

The scale is absurd

There are over 400 active submarine cables spanning roughly 1.4 million kilometers (870,000 miles). Together, they carry 95%+ of all intercontinental internet traffic. Satellites handle the crumbs — maybe 5% at most.

The first submarine cable was a telegraph line laid between England and France in 1850. It carried a few words per minute. Today's cables carry terabits per second — enough to download the entire Library of Congress in seconds.

What they actually look like

A modern submarine cable is about 17-25mm in diameter. That's roughly the width of a hose you might use in a garden. Inside that jacket:

Cross-section of a typical fiber optic cable:
├── Outer polyethylene jacket (protection)
├── Metallic armor (rodents, anchors)
├── Copper power conductor
├── Water-blocking gel
└── Optical fibers (typically 4-16 pairs)

The optical fibers themselves are hair-thin — a single strand can carry 400+ Gbps using wavelength-division multiplexing. Light pulses travel through the glass at nearly c, boosted by repeaters every ~80km.

Who owns them

It's not just telecom giants. Google, Meta, Microsoft, and Amazon now own or lease a significant portion of transoceanic capacity. MAREA (built by Microsoft and Facebook) runs from Virginia to Bilbao, Spain — one of the highest-capacity cables ever. FASTER connects the US to Japan with six fiber pairs. Google's Dunant crosses the Atlantic with 12 fiber pairs.

These aren't public utilities. They're corporate assets, and they're strategically critical.

Choke points

The network isn't evenly distributed. Several narrow passages concentrate traffic:

  • Suez Canal / Red Sea — the bottleneck for Europe-Asia traffic
  • Strait of Malacca — near Singapore, the main artery for Asia-Pacific
  • Hawaii — a major Pacific junction
  • Egypt — almost all Europe-Asia traffic passes through landing stations near Alexandria

Cut any of these, and millions of people lose connectivity. In 2023, a fault near Egypt disrupted 25% of Europe's internet capacity for days.

What kills them

Sharks are famous but overhyped — they account for a tiny fraction. The real threats:

  • Fishing trawlers and anchors — the #1 cause of damage
  • Natural degradation — cable degradation over 25+ years
  • Earthquakes — undersea landslides can sever cables
  • Sabotage — rare, but not impossible (Baltic Sea incidents in 2023-2024)

Why this matters

Most people never think about undersea cables. They think about "the cloud" as something fluffy, abstract, floating somewhere. It's not. It's physical. It's buried in mud at the bottom of the ocean, maintained by ships that spend months at a time splicing fiber in hostile environments.

The internet isn't virtual. It's a million-kilometer-long wire laid by ships, owned by corporations, protected by treaties, and vulnerable to everything from Anchor #7 to a fault in the Red Sea.

The next time you load a page from a server on another continent, remember: your bits probably just crossed an ocean in the dark.
