How Search Engines Work

March 8, 2026 · DEEP DIVE

Every day, billions of searches happen. You type words, you get pages. It feels magical. It's not. It's one of the hardest engineering problems humans have solved, and understanding it reveals a lot about how information is organized — and manipulated.

The Three Pillars

Any search engine has three jobs:

  1. Crawl — Find pages on the web
  2. Index — Store and organize that content
  3. Rank — Decide which results to show

Simple concept. Insane execution.

Crawling: The Spider's Web

Search engines send out bots (called spiders, crawlers, or robots) to discover pages. It starts with a seed list — a handful of known URLs. From each page, the crawler extracts every link and adds those to the queue.

This is called graph traversal. Think of the entire web as a giant node graph, and the crawler is exploring it node by node.

Key challenges:

  • Scale — The web has trillions of pages. You can't crawl it all.
  • Freshness — Pages change, die, or move. Keeping the index current is a moving target.
  • Politeness — Slamming requests is rude a server with (and illegal). Crawlers obey robots.txt and rate-limit themselves.
  • Hidden content — JavaScript-rendered pages, login-protected content, and infinite-scroll pages are harder to crawl.

Indexing: The Library Card Catalog

Once a page is crawled, it gets processed and added to the index — a massive database that maps words to pages. This isn't a simple list. It's an inverted index.

Normal index: Page → Words
Inverted index: Word → Pages

This reversal is crucial. When you search "pizza delivery", the engine doesn't scan every page. It looks up "pizza" in the inverted index, looks up "delivery", then finds the intersection.

What gets stored:

  • Text content — The actual words on the page
  • Metadata — Title, description, keywords, publication date
  • Links — Which pages link to which (crucial for ranking)
  • Signals — Page speed, mobile-friendliness, HTTPS, core web vitals

Analysis happens at crawl time:

  • Tokenization — Breaking text into searchable terms
  • Stemming — Reducing words to root form ( "running" → "run")
  • Stop word removal — Filtering common words ("the", "and", "is")
  • Language detection — Determining what language the page is in

Ranking: The Algorithm's Black Box

This is where it gets interesting — and controversial. The ranking algorithm decides what you see. Google uses over 200 ranking factors. Let me break down the big ones:

On-Page Factors (Content)

  • Keyword relevance — Does the page contain the search terms?
  • Content quality — E-E-A-T: Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trustworthiness
  • Freshness — Is this page current? Some queries want old info, some want new.
  • Content depth — Comprehensive pages often beat thin ones
  • Multimedia — Images, videos can boost engagement

Off-Page Factors (Links)

  • PageRank — The original Google innovation. A page's importance based on how many other quality pages link to it
  • Anchor text — The clickable text in a link. "Best pizza" linking to your site helps for that query
  • Link diversity — Links from many different domains matter more than many from one
  • Link quality — A link from Wikipedia beats a link from a spam blog

Technical Factors

  • Page speed — Core Web Vitals: LCP, FID, CLS
  • Mobile-friendly — Mobile-first indexing
  • HTTPS — Security is a ranking signal
  • Schema markup — Structured data helps engines understand content
  • SSL certificates — Already covered by HTTPS, but worth repeating

User Signals

  • Click-through rate (CTR) — Do people click your result?
  • Dwell time — How long do people stay on your page?
  • Bounce rate — Do people immediately leave?
  • Query refinement — What do people click next?

PageRank: The Innovation That Started It All

Larry Page and Sergey Brin's 1998 breakthrough wasn't better crawling or indexing. It was a new way to measure importance. PageRank treats links as votes. A page gets PageRank from pages that link to it. But not all votes are equal — a vote from a high- PageRank page counts more.

The math is elegant:

PR(A) = (1-d) + d * (PR(T1)/C(T1) + PR(T2)/C(T2) + ... + PR(Tn)/C(Tn))

Where d is a damping factor (usually 0.85), T are pages linking to A, and C is the number of links on page T.

This recursive definition means a page's rank depends on who links to it, whose who links to them, and so on. It propagates importance through the entire web graph.

Modern Search: Beyond Keywords

Today's engines are far beyond keyword matching. They understand:

  • Semantic meaning — "Java" can mean coffee or programming language. Context resolves ambiguity.
  • Entity recognition — Understanding people, places, organizations as distinct entities
  • Natural language — You can ask questions like "How do I..." and get direct answers
  • Personalization — Your search history, location, and preferences shape results
  • Multimodal search — Images, voice, and soon video as first-class inputs

The Search Engine Optimization Industry

When you have a $200B+ industry built around manipulating search results, you get an arms race. SEO exists to game these 200+ factors. And search engines constantly evolve to combat manipulation.

This creates a strange dynamic: the algorithm shapes content. What ranks well gets created. What gets created shapes what we see. The line between information and optimization blurs.

What You Actually See

Here's the uncomfortable truth: what you see in search results is negotiated. Advertisers pay for placement. Companies optimize their pages. Search engines balance relevance, quality, freshness, and business objectives.

The "best" result isn't always the most relevant. It's the most optimized. Understanding this is the first step to being a better searcher — and a more critical consumer of information.

Key Takeaways

  • Search engines crawl, index, and rank — three massive engineering problems
  • The inverted index makes search fast at scale
  • PageRank revolutionized importance measurement through link analysis
  • 200+ factors determine ranking; SEO exists to game them
  • Modern search understands semantics, entities, and context

The next time you search, remember: that instant result is the output of one of the most complex information systems ever built. And what you see is carefully constructed — by algorithms, by competitors, by advertisers, and by the limits of what machines can understand.
