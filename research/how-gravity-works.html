How Gravity Works

March 6, 2026

It's the most familiar force in the universe. It keeps your feet on the ground, the Moon in orbit, and galaxies spinning. Yet gravity is also the least understood. While other forces fit neatly into quantum mechanics, gravity refuses to play along. It's the orphan of physics.

Newton's Apple (and the Moon)

The story is probably apocryphal — Newton may have invented the apple story years later to make his point more memorable — but the idea is real. In 1687, Isaac Newton published Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica and changed everything.

Before Newton, people assumed heavenly motions and earthly motions followed different rules. Newton said no: the same law governs a falling apple and an orbiting Moon. His law of universal gravitation stated that every mass attracts every other mass, with a force proportional to their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them.

F = G(m₁m₂)/r²

Simple. Elegant. Unreasonably powerful. Using this single equation, you could calculate the trajectory of a cannonball, predict the return of comets, and weigh the Sun. It worked spectacularly well for over 200 years.

But Newton never explained how gravity worked. How does the Sun reach across 93 million miles of empty space to pull on Earth? He famously refused to hypothesize: "I frame no hypotheses."

Einstein's Revolution

In 1915, Albert Einstein solved what Newton couldn't explain. His answer was that gravity isn't a force at all.

Einstein's general relativity says that mass and energy tell spacetime how to curve, and curved spacetime tells matter how to move. The Earth isn't being pulled by the Sun via some invisible rope. It's simply following the straightest possible path through spacetime that the Sun's mass has curved.

This is hard to visualize because we're trying to imagine a four-dimensional rubber sheet. But the math is clear, and the predictions were spectacular:

  • Mercury's orbit — Newton's equations slightly mispredicted Mercury's path. Einstein's matched perfectly.
  • Light bending — Mass curves spacetime, so light should bend around massive objects. Confirmed in 1919 during a solar eclipse.
  • Gravitational time dilation — Time runs slower near massive objects. This is measurable; GPS satellites have to account for it.
  • Gravitational waves — Ripples in spacetime from accelerating masses. Detected directly in 2015, 100 years after Einstein predicted them.

What Gravity Is Not

General relativity is our best theory of gravity, but it's not the whole story. Here's what's strange:

Gravity is weak

The electromagnetic force between two electrons is about 10⁴² times stronger than the gravitational force. A tiny magnet holds up a paper clip against the entire Earth's gravity. We have no good explanation for this.

Gravity is non-quantum

Every other force — electromagnetism, the strong nuclear force, the weak nuclear force — has a quantum description. We know about photons, gluons, W and Z bosons. Gravity should have a quantum carrier too: the hypothetical graviton. But no one has ever detected one, and incorporating gravity into quantum mechanics produces mathematical nonsense (infinities that can't be canceled).

Gravity is accelerating

The universe's expansion is accelerating. Something is causing this — something we call "dark energy" but understand even less than dark matter. It acts like a repulsive gravity, pushing everything apart. About 68% of the universe is this stuff, and it's completely mysterious.

The Three Big Problems

Gravity holds three of the biggest unsolved problems in physics:

1. Quantum gravity

How do we unify general relativity with quantum mechanics? String theory and loop quantum gravity are the leading candidates, but neither has experimental support. The energy scales involved are unreachable — the Planck scale is 10¹⁹ times more energetic than what the Large Hadron Collider can probe.

2. Dark matter

Galaxies spin faster than their visible matter should allow. Either our understanding of gravity is wrong at galactic scales, or there's invisible "dark matter" providing extra gravity. We've never directly detected dark matter despite decades of searching.

3. Dark energy

The universe's accelerated expansion implies some form of energy in empty space. The simplest explanation — Einstein's "cosmological constant" — requires an incredible fine-tuning problem: why is it exactly the value it is, rather than infinity or zero?

What We Actually Know

For all our ignorance, gravity is remarkably well-understood in practical terms:

  • Objects fall at 9.8 m/s² (ignoring air resistance)
  • Orbits follow predictable elliptical paths
  • Light bends around massive objects exactly as predicted
  • Gravitational waves exist and we've detected them
  • GPS requires relativistic corrections to work

We can use gravity. We just can't explain it.

The Philosophical Problem

Here's what's weird: gravity is the only fundamental force that doesn't fit into the Standard Model of particle physics. The other three forces are carried by particles. Gravity, if it follows the pattern, should be carried by gravitons — but we've never found them.

Some physicists think this means gravity "leaks" into extra dimensions, which is why it's so weak. Others think we need a completely new framework. A few think we're missing something obvious.

The honest answer: we don't know how gravity works at the deepest level. We have excellent approximations (Newton) and better approximations (Einstein), but the final theory remains elusive.

Why It Matters

Gravity seems solved — we can predict orbits, land rovers on Mars, detect gravitational waves. But the gap between "good enough for engineering" and "fundamentally understood" is vast.

Understanding gravity might be the key to understanding everything: the origin of the universe, the nature of space and time, whether we live in a simulation. Or it might be a dead end. That's science for you — the most important questions are the ones you can't answer.

For now, gravity remains what it's always been: invisible, omnipresent, and mysteriously pulling us toward the ground.
