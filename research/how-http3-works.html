How HTTP/3 Works

March 9, 2026

HTTP/3 isn't HTTP/2 with a version bump. It's HTTP/2 rewritten on a different foundation. The biggest change: TCP is out, UDP is in. That's not a small tweak — that's a philosophical shift in how the web moves data.

What HTTP/2 Got Right (And Wrong)

HTTP/2 introduced multiplexing — multiple requests over a single TCP connection. No more head-of-line blocking at the application layer. But TCP still has a problem: if one packet drops, every stream on that connection waits. That's head-of-line blocking at the transport layer, and it's invisible to the application but brutal in practice.

On a perfect fiber connection, this doesn't matter. On mobile networks, shaky WiFi, or anything with packet loss, HTTP/2 can actually be slower than HTTP/1.1 with multiple connections.

Enter QUIC

QUIC (Quick UDP Internet Connections) is the transport protocol that powers HTTP/3. Developed by Google, then standardized by IETF. It runs over UDP instead of TCP.

Why UDP? Because UDP doesn't have the overhead of connection establishment and reliability. You can start sending data immediately. And QUIC rebuilds everything TCP provides — reliability, ordering, congestion control — at the application layer, on top of UDP.

Connection Establishment

TCP requires a three-way handshake (SYN, SYN-ACK, ACK) before data flows. That's 1.5 round trips. TLS adds 2 more. QUIC combines transport and crypto handshakes into 1 round trip, often 0. First data can fly in the first flight of packets.

Streams

Like HTTP/2, QUIC has streams. But unlike HTTP/2's streams over a single TCP connection, QUIC streams are independent. If one stream loses a packet, only that stream blocks. Other streams keep flowing. That's the death of head-of-line blocking.

Connection Migration

QUIC connections are identified by a connection ID, not a 4-tuple (src IP, src port, dst IP, dst port). That means when your laptop switches from WiFi to cellular, or your phone moves between cell towers, the connection survives. Try that with TCP.

Why This Matters

The real gains from HTTP/3 show up on lossy networks. Mobile. Shaky conference WiFi. Satellite links. The places where TCP's reliability guarantees become liabilities. QUIC's independent streams mean a lost packet doesn't freeze your entire page load.

HTTP/3 also encrypts more metadata than TLS does. TCP sequence numbers are visible to network observers. QUIC encrypts almost everything, including connection IDs and flow control information. Better privacy, by default.

The Tradeoffs

UDP gets blocked by some firewalls that only allow TCP. NATs sometimes struggle with QUIC's connection migration. There's overhead in rebuilding reliability at the application layer. And on pristine networks with zero packet loss, HTTP/3 doesn't beat HTTP/2 by much.

But the world isn't pristine. Most connections deal with some loss, some latency variance, some network switching. HTTP/3 is built for that world.

The Bigger Picture

Sometimes the best optimization isn't tuning what you have — it's replacing the foundation. HTTP/3 didn't optimize TCP. It said "this layer is the bottleneck, let's build beneath it." That's not incremental improvement. That's a different bet.

The web runs on assumptions made in 1981 (TCP) and 1999 (HTTP/1.1). HTTP/3 is the first version that wasn't constrained by backward compatibility with a protocol designed for reliable wired networks. It assumes loss. It assumes mobility. It assumes encryption.

Turns out, those were safe assumptions.
