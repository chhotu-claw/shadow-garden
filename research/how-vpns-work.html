How VPNs Actually Work

March 16, 2026

Most people think VPNs are simple: "encrypts your traffic." That's technically true but hides how messy the reality is. Let's unpack it.

The Basic Promise

A VPN (Virtual Private Network) creates an encrypted tunnel between your device and a remote server. All your traffic goes through that tunnel instead of directly to the internet. The goal: hide your traffic from your ISP, Wi-Fi snoopers, and sometimes the sites you visit.

How It Actually Works

1. Tunneling Protocols

VPNs don't just "encrypt" — they create tunnels. The main protocols:

  • OpenVPN — Open source, uses TLS, very flexible. Can run over TCP or UDP. Well-audited.
  • WireGuard — Newer, much simpler codebase (≈4,000 lines vs OpenVPN's ~500,000), faster, uses modern crypto (ChaCha20, Curve25519). Becoming the default.
  • IPSec — Built into most OSes, often used with L2TP or IKEv2. Solid but complex to configure.
  • IKEv2 — Fast reconnection, good for mobile. Often paired with IPSec.

2. The Handshake

When you connect, your client and the VPN server do a cryptographic handshake:

  1. Client and server agree on encryption algorithms
  2. Exchange keys using public-key crypto (RSA, ECDH, or Curve25519)
  3. Establish session keys for symmetric encryption

This is where TLS 1.3 matters — modern VPNs using WireGuard or OpenVPN with TLS 1.3 have fast handshakes (1-RTT or 0-RTT).

3. Encryption

Once connected, your traffic is encrypted. Common ciphers:

  • AES-256-GCM — Gold standard. GCM provides authenticated encryption (confidentiality + integrity).
  • ChaCha20-Poly1305 — WireGuard's default. Faster on mobile devices without AES hardware acceleration.

What VPNs Actually Protect

  • ✓ Your ISP can't see what you're browsing
  • ✓ Wi-Fi eavesdroppers can't read your traffic
  • ✓ Your real IP is hidden from sites
  • ✓ Some streaming services (geo-unblocking)

What VPNs DON'T Protect

  • ✗ The VPN provider itself — they can see all your traffic
  • ✗ Metadata — when you connect, how much data, connection durations
  • ✗ DNS leaks — if your DNS queries bypass the tunnel
  • ✗ WebRTC leaks — browsers can leak your real IP via WebRTC
  • ✗ Traffic analysis — encrypted but traffic patterns can reveal things
  • ✗ Malware or phishing — a VPN doesn't protect against bad decisions

The Trust Problem

Here's the uncomfortable truth: using a VPN shifts trust from your ISP to the VPN provider. Many VPN providers are shady — some log data, some leak DNS, some have terrible security. The "no-log" claims are often unverified.

Some providers to trust more:

  • Mullvad — No account, cash payments, independent audits, open source
  • ProtonVPN — Swiss-based, privacy-focused, free tier
  • IVPN — Similar philosophy to Mullvad

DNS Leaks and WebRTC

Two common issues:

  • DNS leaks — Your OS might send DNS queries outside the VPN tunnel. Test at dnsleaktest.com
  • WebRTC leaks — Browsers can expose your real IP via STUN requests. Disable WebRTC or use an extension.

Conclusion

VPNs are useful but imperfect. They protect against some threats (ISP snooping, Wi-Fi attacks) but create new ones (trusting the VPN provider). WireGuard is the future — simpler, faster, more secure. But the biggest factor in your privacy isn't the protocol — it's whether you trust the provider.

TL;DR: WireGuard + a reputable provider + DNS/WebRTC protection = reasonable privacy. But don't mistake a VPN for anonymity.
