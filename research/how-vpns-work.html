How VPNs Work

March 13, 2026

VPNs promise privacy. They promise security. They promise to hide you from your ISP, bypass geo-restrictions, and make you anonymous online. The marketing is confident. The reality is more nuanced.

The Problem VPNs Solve

Every device on the internet has an IP address — a unique identifier that reveals your location, your ISP, and (with enough effort) your identity. When you visit a website, your traffic flows through your ISP's servers, which can see everything: what sites you visit, how long you stay, what you download.

A VPN creates a tunnel between your device and a VPN server. Your ISP sees encrypted gibberish going to one IP address. The VPN server decrypts it and forwards your request to the destination. From the website's perspective, the request comes from the VPN server — not you.

The Tunnel

The "virtual private network" is a tunnel through the public internet. It's "virtual" because it doesn't use dedicated physical infrastructure — it runs over the same pipes as everything else. It's "private" because the traffic inside is encrypted.

The tunnel has two ends: your device and the VPN server. Everything that leaves your device gets wrapped in an encrypted packet, sent through the tunnel, unwrapped at the server, and forwarded to the real destination.

Encryption

VPN encryption typically uses AES-256 — the same standard used by governments and banks. The key exchange happens during connection setup, using protocols like RSA or elliptic curve cryptography. Once established, all traffic is encrypted with a session key that exists only for that connection.

The encryption happens at the transport layer (usually). Your VPN client encrypts data before it leaves your machine. Your ISP sees encrypted packets. The VPN server decrypts them. The destination server sees the VPN server's IP.

VPN Protocols

The protocol determines how the tunnel is built and encrypted:

  • OpenVPN — Open source, highly configurable, uses OpenSSL. The gold standard for transparency and security.
  • WireGuard — Newer, simpler code base (under 4,000 lines), faster than OpenVPN, gaining rapid adoption.
  • IPSec — Built into most operating systems, often used with L2TP. Solid security, sometimes complex to configure.
  • IKEv2 — Fast reconnect on network changes (like switching WiFi to mobile), good for mobile devices.

What VPNs Actually Hide

  • Your ISP — Yes. They see encrypted traffic to one IP, not what you're doing.
  • Your location — Partially. Websites see the VPN server's location, not yours.
  • Your browsing history from your ISP — Yes, after the VPN is established.

What VPNs Don't Hide

  • From the VPN provider itself — They can see everything. "No-log" policies are claims, not guarantees.
  • From websites you log into — If you log into Google, Google knows who you are regardless of your IP.
  • From DNS leaks — If your DNS queries bypass the VPN tunnel, your ISP can still see what domains you visit.
  • From browser fingerprinting — Your browser reveals plenty beyond IP address.

DNS Leaks

Even with a VPN, your device might send DNS queries to your ISP's DNS servers instead of through the encrypted tunnel. This "DNS leak" reveals your browsing to your ISP. Good VPN clients route all traffic — including DNS — through the tunnel.

WebRTC Leaks

WebRTC is a browser feature that enables real-time communication. It can sometimes reveal your real IP address even behind a VPN. Disabling WebRTC or using browser extensions can prevent this.

The Trust Problem

Here's the uncomfortable truth: when you use a VPN, you're trusting the VPN provider instead of your ISP. Your ISP can't see your traffic, but the VPN provider can. "No-log" policies have been violated before. Some VPN providers are owned by shady companies. Some inject ads. Some sell data.

Choosing a VPN is a trust decision. Open-source audits, independent security reviews, jurisdiction (14 Eyes countries matter), and transparency reports are the best signals.

Splittunneling

Most VPNs can route only some traffic through the tunnel. You might want your browser to use the VPN but your local network devices to bypass it. Splittunneling gives you that control.

Kill Switch

If the VPN connection drops, your traffic reverts to going directly through your ISP. A kill switch blocks all internet traffic until the VPN reconnects, preventing accidental exposure.

Multi-hop

Some VPNs route your traffic through multiple servers (e.g., your device → server A → server B → internet). This makes traffic analysis harder but adds latency.

VPNs vs Tor

VPNs offer one hop (you → VPN server). Tor offers three hops through volunteer relays. Tor is slower but more private — no single entity sees both you and your destination. VPNs are faster but put all trust in one provider.

What Actually Matters

A VPN is a useful tool for:

  • Hiding browsing from your ISP
  • Accessing geo-restricted content
  • Securing traffic on public WiFi
  • Masking your IP from websites

A VPN is not:

  • Anonymity (see: Tor)
  • Protection from tracking (see: browser fingerprinting, cookies)
  • A guarantee of privacy (see: the trust problem)

The Bottom Line

VPNs work. They encrypt your traffic and hide your IP from your ISP and most websites. But they're not magic. They shift your trust from your ISP to the VPN provider. The best VPN is a trustworthy one with a clean record, transparent operations, and open-source clients. The worst VPN is a false sense of security.
