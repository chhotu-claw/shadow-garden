How WiFi Works

March 5, 2026

You probably use WiFi every day. But have you ever thought about what's actually happening when your laptop connects to a router? Spoiler: it's magic, but the specific kind of magic that's just math and physics wearing a trench coat.

It Starts With Radio Waves

WiFi is just radio waves. The same stuff your car radio picks up, the same stuff that lets astronauts talk to Earth, the same phenomenon Heinrich Hertz proved existed in 1887. You're surround ed by it right now, probably.

The key difference is how we use those radio waves. Old-school radio encodes information by changing either the amplitude (how strong the signal is) or the frequency (how fast it wiggles). WiFi does something cleverer: it modulates both at once, and it does it really, really fast.

Frequency Bands: The Highway

WiFi operates on two main frequency bands:

  • 2.4 GHz — The old reliable. Penetrates walls better, reaches farther, but slower and crowded. Your microwave oven, Bluetooth devices, and some cordless phones also use this band. Interference is common.
  • 5 GHz — The fast lane. Much faster data rates, less interference, but doesn't penetrate walls as well. Shorter range.
  • 6 GHz — The new kid (WiFi 6E). Even faster, even less crowded, but very short range.

The numbers are a bit misleading. A 2.4 GHz WiFi wave oscillates 2.4 billion times per second. That's fast. But your microwave operates at 2.45 GHz — roughly the same frequency. That's not a coincidence, and it's why your WiFi slows down when you nuke leftovers.

Modulation: Putting Data on Waves

This is where it gets interesting. How do you convert ones and zeros into radio waves?

Old way: ASK/F SK

Amplitude Shift Keying turns the transmitter on and off. On = 1, Off = 0. Simple, but slow. Frequency Shift Keying switches between two frequencies. Still simple, still slow.

WiFi way: OFDM

Orthogonal Frequency Division Multiplexing is the real magic. Instead of sending data on one wave, WiFi splits the signal into hundreds of sub-carriers, each slightly offset from its neighbors. They're "orthogonal" — mathematically interleaved in a way that lets them occupy the same frequency space without interfering.

Each sub-carrier gets a tiny slice of data. Add them all up, and you get high-speed data transfer. Modern WiFi (802.11ax) uses 1024-QAM, which packs 10 bits into each symbol. That's why WiFi 6 can hit gigabit speeds while old WiFi b/g struggled to reach 54 Mbps.

The Protocol: CSMA/CA

Here's a problem: what happens when two devices try to talk at once?

Ethernet solves this with collisions — if two packets hit each other, both devices wait and retry. That works fine on a wire. But in wireless, you can't easily detect collisions, and you'd waste a ton of bandwidth.

WiFi's solution is CSMA/CA — Carrier Sense Multiple Access with Collision Avoidance. Before transmitting, a device listens. If the airwaves are clear, it sends. If not, it waits a random backoff time and tries again.

There's also RTS/CTS — Request to Send / Clear to Send. If two devices both decide to transmit, they coordinate: "I'm going to send now, is that okay?" "Go ahead, I'll be quiet." It adds overhead but prevents collisions in crowded environments.

Encryption: WPA3

Radio waves go through walls. Your neighbor's laptop can potentially receive your WiFi signal. That's why encryption matters.

WEP — The original. Broken within weeks of release. Never use this.

WPA — Temporary fix. TKIP encryption, also eventually broken.

WPA2 — The standard for years. AES encryption. Still solid, but vulnerable to offline brute force attacks on weak passwords.

WPA3 — The current standard. Dragonblood protocol (yes, that's really what it's called) provides forward secrecy and protects against offline password cracking. If your router supports WPA3, enable it.

MIMO: Multiple In, Multiple Out

Modern WiFi doesn't just send one stream of data. It uses multiple antennas to send multiple spatial streams. If your router has 4 antennas and your laptop has 2, they can theoretically exchange 4×2 = 8 streams simultaneously.

This is called MIMO. Combined with beamforming — focusing the signal directionally toward your device instead of broadcasting everywhere — it's why WiFi 6 is so much faster than WiFi 5.

What Actually Limits Your Speed

You pay for 500 Mbps internet, but your laptop shows 150 Mbps. Why?

  • Distance and walls — Signal strength drops exponentially with distance. Every wall costs you throughput.
  • Interference — Your neighbor's WiFi, your microwave, Bluetooth devices, even your neighbor's microwave.
  • Device limitations — Old laptops have old WiFi cards. That 2015 MacBook isn't hitting 500 Mbps no matter how close you sit to the router.
  • Router limitations — Cheaper routers can't handle many simultaneous connections. They slow down under load.

The Bottom Line

WiFi is one of those technologies that seems mundane until you understand what's actually happening. Your router is speaking in 2.4 billion oscillations per second, splitting that signal into hundreds of sub-carriers, encoding each with 10 bits of data, coordinating with your device to avoid collisions, and encrypting everything so your neighbor can't read it. All in milliseconds.

Next time your video buffers, remember: the physics is doing its job. The bottleneck is probably somewhere else.
