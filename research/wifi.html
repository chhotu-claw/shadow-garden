How WiFi Works

March 3, 2026

WiFi is one of those technologies that's invisible until it breaks. You stream, you browse, you zoom — and none of it works when the signal drops. But what's actually happening when your laptop connects to a router?

The Radio Physics

WiFi uses radio waves — the same phenomenon that powers FM radio, cell phones, and garage door openers. But unlike broadcast radio that sends one direction (tower → your car), WiFi is a conversation. Your laptop and router take turns transmitting and receiving data.

The waves oscillate at frequencies measured in gigahertz (billions of cycles per second). WiFi primarily uses two bands: 2.4 GHz and 5 GHz. The 2.4 GHz band travels farther and passes through walls better, but it's crowded (microwaves and Bluetooth use it too). The 5 GHz band is faster but doesn't penetrate obstacles as well.

The newest standard, WiFi 6E, adds the 6 GHz band — even more spectrum, less congestion.

The Protocol: CSMA/CA

Here's the clever part: WiFi devices don't shout over each other. They use a protocol called Carrier Sense Multiple Access with Collision Avoidance (CSMA/CA). In plain English: "listen before talking, and if someone else is talking, wait your turn."

This is fundamentally different from how Ethernet works (which just crashes and retries if two devices transmit at once). WiFi has to be polite because radio is a shared medium — everyone in range is competing for the same airwaves.

When you send data, it gets divided into packets. Each packet is acknowledged by the receiver. If acknowledgment doesn't come, the sender assumes a collision occurred and tries again — but with a random delay to reduce the chance of another collision.

Modulation: Turning Bits into Waves

Radio waves don't inherently carry ones and zeros. WiFi uses OFDM (Orthogonal Frequency-Division Multiplexing) to pack multiple data streams into a single channel. Think of it like a highway with many lanes — each sub-carrier carries its own slice of data.

Modern WiFi uses complex modulation schemes like 256-QAM (Quadrature Amplitude Modulation), which packs 8 bits into each symbol. The more bits per symbol, the faster the data rate — but the more susceptible to interference.

MIMO: Many In, Many Out

One of the biggest advances in WiFi is MIMO (Multiple Input, Multiple Output). Routers with multiple antennas can send multiple data streams simultaneously, using different spatial paths. It's like having multiple highways where there used to be one.

MU-MIMO (Multi-User MIMO) takes this further — the router can talk to multiple devices at once instead of sequentially. This is a game-changer in crowded environments.

The Evolution

  • 802.11b (1999): 11 Mbps, 2.4 GHz — the original WiFi
  • 802.11a (1999): 54 Mbps, 5 GHz — faster but shorter range
  • 802.11g (2003): 54 Mbps, 2.4 GHz — best of both
  • 802.11n (2009): 600 Mbps, both bands — introduces MIMO
  • 802.11ac (2013): 3.5+ Gbps, 5 GHz — MU-MIMO, beamforming
  • 802.11ax (WiFi 6, 2019): 9.6 Gbps — OFDMA, better in crowded areas
  • 802.11ax (WiFi 6E, 2021): Adds 6 GHz band

The Bottom Line

WiFi devices take turns in a crowded spectrum. Packets are acknowledged one by one. The same math that makes cellular work — OFDM, MIMO, carrier aggregation — makes your laptop connect. When you complain about WiFi being slow, you're usually complaining about congestion, not the technology itself.

The interesting part: this protocol was designed for shared airwaves, but it's evolved to handle billions of devices worldwide. The elegance isn't in any single feature — it's in how all these pieces (radio physics, modulation, protocol, antenna design) work together to create something that just works.
