How WiFi Works

March 1, 2026

You probably use WiFi every day. But what's actually happening when your phone connects to a router? It's a fascinating chain of radio waves, protocols, and clever engineering. Here's how it works.

Radio Waves: The Physical Layer

WiFi uses radio waves in the 2.4 GHz and 5 GHz bands. These are electromagnetic waves, like light, but at much lower frequencies.

The 2.4 GHz band goes further but is slower and crowded (microwaves, Bluetooth, cordless phones all use it). The 5 GHz band is faster but doesn't penetrate walls as well.

Newer WiFi 6E and WiFi 7 use the 6 GHz band — even more spectrum, less congestion.

Modulation: Encoding Data

Radio waves carry data through modulation — varying the wave's amplitude, frequency, or phase.

Modern WiFi uses OFDM (Orthogonal Frequency Division Multiplexing): the spectrum is split into many sub-carriers, each carrying data in parallel. This is more efficient and resistant to interference.

QAM (Quadrature Amplitude Modulation) packs even more data by varying both amplitude and phase. 256-QAM, 1024-QAM — the higher the number, the more bits per symbol, but the more susceptible to noise.

The 802.11 Protocol

WiFi is technically IEEE 802.11. The main versions:

  • 802.11b/g — 2.4 GHz, up to 54 Mbps
  • 802.11a — 5 GHz, up to 54 Mbps
  • 802.11n (WiFi 4) — 2.4/5 GHz, up to 600 Mbps, MIMO
  • 802.11ac (WiFi 5) — 5 GHz, up to 3.5 Gbps, MU-MIMO
  • 802.11ax (WiFi 6) — 2.4/5/6 GHz, up to 9.6 Gbps, OFDMA
  • 802.11be (WiFi 7) — All bands, up to 46 Gbps, MLO

CSMA/CA: Avoiding Collisions

Here's a hard problem: how do multiple devices share the same radio spectrum without talking over each other?

Ethernet uses CSMA/CD (Collision Detection) — if two signals collide, both devices notice and retry. But radio can't detect collisions while transmitting (you're transmitting, so you can't hear).

WiFi uses CSMA/CA (Collision Avoidance): before transmitting, the device listens. If the channel is busy, it waits. If quiet, it waits a random backoff period, then transmits. If no acknowledgment comes, it assumes collision and retries.

This is why WiFi gets slower with more devices — they're all taking turns.

MIMO and MU-MIMO

MIMO (Multiple Input, Multiple Output) uses multiple antennas to send/receive multiple data streams simultaneously. More antennas = more throughput.

MU-MIMO (Multi-User MIMO) lets an access point talk to multiple devices at once, instead of sequentially. WiFi 5 has downlink MU-MIMO; WiFi 6 adds uplink.

OFDMA: The WiFi 6 Revolution

OFDMA (Orthogonal Frequency Division Multiple Access) splits each channel into smaller "resource units." Multiple devices can transmit simultaneously on the same channel.

This is huge for congestion. Instead of waiting for the whole channel, devices share. It makes WiFi 6 much better in crowded environments.

What Remains

WiFi keeps evolving — more spectrum, better modulation, smarter protocols. But the fundamentals haven't changed much: radio waves, modulation, medium access, acknowledgment.

The next time you stream a video over WiFi, remember: you're riding radio waves, taking turns in a crowded spectrum, with your packets acknowledged one by one.
