How Vector Databases Work

March 24, 2026

If you've used ChatGPT and asked it about your own documents, you've interacted with a vector database. They're the memory layer of AI systems — the technology that lets machines find "similar" things without exact matches. Here's how they actually work.

From Words to Numbers

The first step is turning text into numbers. This is what embeddings do. An embedding model (like BERT, OpenAI's text-embedding-ada-002, or hundreds of others) takes a piece of text and converts it into a vector — a list of numbers, typically 256 to 4096 dimensions long.

The magic is that semantically similar texts end up close to each other in this high-dimensional space. "King" and "Queen" are neighbors. "Apple the company" and "Apple the fruit" are far apart — the context pushes them in different directions.

These vectors are just points in space. And once you have points, you can ask: which points are closest to each other?

Nearest Neighbor Search

Finding the nearest neighbor seems simple: just measure the distance from your query to every other point, sort by distance, and take the top results. This is called brute-force search, and it works — until you have millions of vectors.

The problem: measuring distance in 768-dimensional space isn't cheap, and doing it millions of times adds up. A 1 million vector database doing brute-force search? That's hundreds of millions of distance calculations per query. At scale, this kills latency.

So vector databases use approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) algorithms. They sacrifice a little accuracy for massive speed gains.

HNSW: The Popular Kid

HNSW (Hierarchical Navigable Small World) is the most widely-used ANN algorithm. Here's the intuition:

Imagine you're trying to find the closest coffee shop to a specific building in a city. You could check every building — brute force. Or you could use a mental map: "I'm in downtown, near the financial district, around 5th and Main." You navigate through landmarks.

HNSW does something similar. It builds a multi-layer graph. The bottom layer has every point. The layers above are "summaries" — entry points that let you jump long distances quickly. To search, you start at the top, make big jumps until you're close, then descend to find the exact nearest neighbors.

This gives you sub-millisecond queries on millions of vectors with 95-99% accuracy compared to brute force. That's the trade-off: you get mostly-right answers, incredibly fast.

IVF: The Index Approach

IVF (Inverted File Index) is another common approach. It clusters vectors into groups — think of it like organizing books by genre. When you search, it looks at the most relevant clusters first.

It's simpler than HNSW and works well when your data has natural groupings. Many production systems combine IVF with other techniques (like product quantization) for maximum efficiency.

Product Quantization: Shrinking the Math

Here's a trick: those 768-dimensional vectors take a lot of memory. What if you could compress them?

Product quantization breaks each vector into smaller chunks, compresses each chunk separately, and stores them more compactly. You lose some precision, but the vectors still work for similarity search — the distances are "close enough."

This is how systems handle billions of vectors on a single server. The math is approximate, but the results are usable.

The RAG Revolution

Vector databases exploded because of retrieval-augmented generation (RAG). The idea: your LLM knows everything from its training data, but not your data. So you embed your documents, store them in a vector DB, and when a user asks something relevant, you retrieve the relevant chunks and feed them to the LLM as context.

This is the architecture behind every "AI that knows your docs" product. The vector DB is the bridge between static training knowledge and dynamic, user-specific information.

What's Next

The field is evolving fast. Hybrid search (combining keyword and vector), multi-modal embeddings (images, audio, video), and disk-based indexes are all active areas of development. The core insight remains: embeddings map meaning to numbers, and ANN algorithms find approximate matches without checking everything.

Understanding this layer is essential for building anything AI-related. It's the new database layer for the intelligence age.
