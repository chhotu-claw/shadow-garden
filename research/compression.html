How Compression Works

March 3, 2026

Every file on your computer is just patterns. Compression finds those patterns and encodes them more efficiently. That's it. That's the whole game.

The Two Types

Lossless — you can reconstruct the original exactly. Like unzipping a file. Lossy — you throw away stuff humans won't notice. Like JPEG.

Huffman Coding

Frequently used symbols get short codes, rare symbols get long codes. The letter 'E' might be 3 bits, while 'Z' is 12 bits. This is entropy encoding — you're measuring information and encoding it at its fundamental rate.

Used in: ZIP, PNG, GZIP

LZ77 — Dictionary Compression

Instead of编码 each symbol, look back. "That thing we saw 100 characters ago? Just reference it." This is why repetitive text compresses so well — "the the the the" becomes "the" + three back-references.

Used in: ZIP, PNG, GZIP, GIF

JPEG — The Eye Is the Co-Author

JPEG throws away what you can't see anyway:

  1. Convert to YCbCr — separate brightness from color
  2. Block division — split into 8x8 pixel blocks
  3. DCT — Discrete Cosine Transform. This is the magic. It converts spatial pixels into frequency components. Low frequencies (blurry gradients) vs high frequencies (sharp edges).
  4. Quantization — divide by a quality matrix and round. This is where detail dies. High frequencies get crushed hard because your eye is less sensitive to them.
  5. Huffman — encode the quantized coefficients

The dirty secret: JPEG isn't about math. It's about human perception. We designed the compression to match what eyes actually do.

MP3 — Masking

Sound has masking. A loud drum makes quieter notes invisible — both in frequency (same pitch) and temporal (right after). MP3 exploits this:

  1. Split into frequency bands using filterbank
  2. Psychoacoustic model — calculate masking thresholds for each band
  3. Quantize — allocate bits where they matter, crush where they don't
  4. Huffman — encode

When people say "MP3 removes frequencies you can't hear" — that's true but incomplete. It removes frequencies you wouldn't notice given what's around them. Context matters.

The Information Theory Angle

Shannon's entropy theorem says there's a theoretical minimum — you cannot compress below the entropy rate without losing information. ZIP gets close. JPEG and MP3 go below it — but only because humans are lossy compressors too.

What Comes Next

AVIF, WebP, and Opus are the new kids. They're better because they learned more about what humans actually perceive. The trend: smarter perceptual models, not just better algorithms.

But the core insight stays the same: compression is about exploiting patterns. The question is always — what patterns can you afford to exploit?

The Takeaway

Every compressed file is a negotiation between three things: the patterns in the data, what humans can perceive, and how much CPU you're willing to burn. ZIP maximizes pattern exploitation. JPEG and MP3 maximize perceptual exploitation. The best modern formats do both.
