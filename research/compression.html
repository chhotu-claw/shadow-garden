How Compression Works

March 3, 2026

Files are big. Compression makes them small. But how do you reduce size without losing information? Here's the math behind ZIP, MP3, JPEG, and everything else that shrinks data.

Two Types: Lossless vs Lossy

Compression comes in two flavors:

  • Lossless — ZIP, PNG, FLAC — you can recover every bit exactly
  • Lossy — JPEG, MP3, MP4 — you throw away what you can't hear/see

Run-Length Encoding: The Simplest

AAAAAABBBBBCCCC → A6B5C4. Repeated characters become count + character. Simple but works for certain data.

Huffman Coding: Frequent = Short

The most common characters get the shortest codes:

  • Build frequency table of all symbols
  • Build binary tree with most frequent at top
  • Assign codes: left=0, right=1

English text: 'E' gets a short code, 'Z' gets a long one. This is what gzip uses internally.

LZ77: Reference Previous Data

Instead of storing repeated data, store a reference to where it appeared before:

"the cat sat" → "the(8,3) "(back 8 chars, repeat 3)

This is what PNG and gzip use. Very effective for text.

JPEG: Throw Away Detail

JPEG uses DCT (like JPEG image compression) to convert data to frequencies, then quantizes—divides by quality factor and rounds. High frequencies (detail) become zero more often.

MP3: Remove What You Can't Hear

MP3 uses psychoacoustic modeling: remove frequencies below hearing threshold, and frequencies "masked" by louder sounds. What remains is encoded with Huffman coding.

What Remains

Compression is about exploiting patterns: repeated data, frequent symbols, imperceptible detail. The math is elegant—information theory tells us the theoretical limit (entropy), and compression algorithms get close.
