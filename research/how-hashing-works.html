How Hashing Works

March 28, 2026

You've seen them everywhere — SHA-256, MD5, bcrypt. But what's actually happening when you "hash" something? And why does it matter?

The Basic Idea

A hash function takes any amount of input (a single character, a 10GB file, the entire internet) and produces a fixed-size output called a digest. That's the first strange part: infinitely many inputs map to a finite number of outputs.

SHA-256 always produces exactly 256 bits — 64 hexadecimal characters. That's it. Doesn't matter if your input is "hi" or the complete works of Shakespeare.

The Properties

A good cryptographic hash function needs three key properties:

1. Deterministic — The same input always produces the same output. No randomness.

2. One-way — You can't reverse it. Given a hash, you can't figure out the original input. This isn't encryption (there's no key). It's a mathematical one-way function.

3. Collision-resistant — It's practically impossible to find two different inputs that produce the same hash. This is the "birthday paradox" at work — with SHA-256's 2²⁵⁶ possible outputs, you'd need to try roughly 2¹²⁸ inputs before finding a collision by chance.

What Actually Happens Inside

Here's where it gets interesting. Hash functions are essentially scramblers. They take the input, break it into chunks, mix those chunks together through a series of bitwise operations (XOR, AND, OR, rotations), and produce a final digest.

The mixing is designed so that:

  • Changing one bit of input produces a completely different output (the avalanche effect)
  • The output appears random but is perfectly reproducible
  • You can't find patterns in the output to reverse-engineer the input

Why This Matters

Hashing is the backbone of modern security:

Password storage — Sites don't store your password, they store its hash. When you log in, they hash what you typed and compare. Even if they get breached, they only have hashes, not actual passwords.

Integrity verification — Download a file, check its hash. If it's different, the file was tampered with. This is how software updates verify nothing was modified.

Digital signatures — Hash your message, encrypt that hash with your private key. Anyone can verify it came from you without reading the message.

Proof of work — Bitcoin miners find inputs whose hash starts with a bunch of zeros. It's literally finding a needle in a haystack by checking every piece of hay.

The Caveats

Not all hashes are created equal. MD5 and SHA-1 are broken — collisions have been demonstrated. They're still used in non-security contexts (file verification) but never for passwords or signatures.

Password hashing specifically needs more than a fast hash. That's why we have bcrypt, scrypt, Argon2 — they add salting (random data added to each password so identical passwords produce different hashes) and work factors (deliberately slow to prevent brute force).

The Bottom Line

Hashing is one of those technologies that's invisible but everywhere. It proves integrity, secures passwords, and enables cryptocurrencies. And the magic is this: a simple mathematical function turns arbitrary data into a fixed-size fingerprint that can't be forged, predicted, or reversed.
