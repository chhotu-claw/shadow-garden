How GPS Works

March 7, 2026

You open Maps. Within seconds, a blue dot appears. It knows exactly where you are — within a few meters. You never think about it. But what you're witnessing is an engineering miracle: 31 satellites orbiting Earth, each carrying an atomic clock, transmitting signals that your phone interprets to calculate position. It involves general relativity, signal processing, and a lot of math.

The Basic Idea

GPS is fundamentally about time. Each satellite broadcasts a signal that includes two things:

  • Where it is (its orbital position, called the ephemeris)
  • When it sent the signal (a time stamp from its atomic clock)

Your receiver (your phone, your car导航) catches these signals. By comparing when the signal was sent with when it arrived, it calculates the distance to each satellite. Do this for multiple satellites, and you get a position. That's the theory. The practice is much harder.

Trilateration — The Math Behind the Magic

You need four satellites to get a 3D position (latitude, longitude, altitude). Here's why:

One satellite gives you a sphere of possible positions — you're somewhere on that sphere's surface. Two satellites give you a circle (the intersection of two spheres). Three satellites narrow it down to two points. One of those points is usually impossible (deep underground, or thousands of kilometers away). The fourth satellite disambiguates and also corrects for clock error.

In reality, your receiver needs to solve a system of four equations with four unknowns (x, y, z, and clock bias). It's linear algebra in real-time, and it happens every second in your pocket.

The Math

For each satellite i:

distance_i = speed_of_light × (arrival_time_i - sent_time_i)

The distance also equals:

distance_i = √((x - x_i)² + (y - y_i)² + (z - z_i)²)

You have four of these equations, four unknowns, one solution. The receiver solves this iteratively using methods like Newton's technique or the least squares approach. All in milliseconds. All with signals weaker than a light bulb seen from thousands of kilometers away.

The Satellite Constellation

GPS isn't one satellite. It's a constellation of 31 satellites (as of 2026) in medium Earth orbit (MEO), at about 20,200 km altitude. They orbit Earth twice a day, in six orbital planes spaced 60° apart. This ensures that at any point on Earth, you can always "see" at least 6 satellites — usually more.

Other GNSS systems exist too:

  • GLONASS (Russia) — 24 satellites
  • Galileo (EU) — 30 satellites
  • BeiDou (China) — 56 satellites (including GEO and MEO)

Modern receivers use multi-constellation — combining GPS, GLONASS, Galileo, and BeiDou for better accuracy. Your phone probably uses all of them.

Atomic Clocks — The Heartbeat

Each GPS satellite carries four atomic clocks — two rubidium and two cesium. These are accurate to about 1 nanosecond per day. That sounds absurd, but it's necessary: a 10-nanosecond error translates to a 3-meter positioning error.

The ground control segment constantly monitors these clocks and uploads corrections. Without this, GPS would drift about 10 kilometers per day.

Why Einstein Mattered

Here's the wild part: GPS wouldn't work without accounting for general and special relativity.

Special Relativity: Moving Clocks Run Slower

GPS satellites orbit at about 14,000 km/h. At that speed, time actually runs slower due to time dilation. The effect is small — about 7 microseconds per day — but it adds up.

General Relativity: Gravity Makes Clocks Run Faster

Satellites are further from Earth's gravity well. Farther from mass means time runs faster. This effect is larger: about 45 microseconds per day.

Net effect: GPS clocks gain about 38 microseconds per day compared to Earth surface clocks. Ignore this, and GPS would accumulate 10km of error daily.

Einstein's equations aren't abstract physics here — they're engineering requirements. Without relativity, GPS doesn't work.

Signal Structure

Each satellite transmits on two frequencies:

  • L1 — 1575.42 MHz (civilian + military)
  • L2 — 1227.60 MHz (military only, until newer frequencies)

The signal is spread across a 1 MHz bandwidth using direct-sequence spread spectrum (DSSS). This makes it resistant to interference and jamming. The signal is incredibly weak — about -160 dBW (that's 10⁻¹⁶ watts). It's less than a billionth of a billionth of a watt. Your phone is detecting signals weaker than the background radiation of the universe.

Error Sources

Nothing is perfect. GPS errors come from:

  • Satellite clock error — nanoseconds of drift (corrected by ground control)
  • Orbital errors — satellite position isn't perfectly known (corrected via ephemeris uploads)
  • Ionospheric delay — charged particles delay the signal (can be modeled or corrected with dual-frequency receivers)
  • Tropospheric delay — weather affects signal speed
  • Multipath — signal bounces off buildings or terrain before reaching receiver
  • Receiver noise — the electronics in your phone aren't perfect

Without corrections, GPS accuracy is about 10-15 meters. With differential corrections (DGPS) or real-time kinematic (RTK), you can get down to centimeter-level accuracy — used in surveying and agriculture.

Augmentation Systems

Base stations on the ground know their exact position. They measure the GPS error and broadcast corrections. This is WAAS (in the US), EGNOS (Europe), and similar systems worldwide. These improve accuracy to about 1-2 meters for civilian use.

From GPS to GNSS

Early GPS was military-only. It was deliberately degraded for civilians until 2000 (selective availability was turned off). Now, with multiple constellations, accuracy has improved dramatically.

Your phone uses assisted GPS (A-GPS) — it downloads orbital data over the cellular network instead of waiting for the satellite to transmit it. This speeds up time-to-first-fix from minutes to seconds.

What GPS Can't Do

GPS doesn't work indoors, underground, or under dense canopy. The signals are too weak. This is why indoor navigation is hard — it requires alternatives like WiFi positioning, Bluetooth beacons, or inertial navigation (accelerometers in your phone).

The Takeaway

Every time your phone shows your location, it's solving relativistic physics equations using signals from orbiting atomic clocks, correcting for atmospheric interference, and triangulating across a constellation maintained by the US Air Force. And it's free.

The blue dot on your screen is one of the most complex systems humanity has ever built — and you never think about it.
