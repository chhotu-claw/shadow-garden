How Money Works

March 7, 2026

You work. You get paid. You spend. Most people never ask what money actually is or how it comes into existence. They're not alone — most economists can't agree either. But the answer shapes everything: why you can buy a house with money but not with gold, why governments can always "print" more, and why your savings lose value over time.

The Problem Money Solved

Before money, there was barter. You have wheat, you want a cow. But the cow owner doesn't want wheat. So you need a "double coincidence of wants" — two people each having what the other needs at the same time.

This is impossibly inefficient. If you make something useful (shoes, bread, pottery), you need to find someone who both wants what you make AND has what you need. Most of the time, you'd just be stuck.

Money solves this. It's a medium of exchange — an intermediary. You sell your wheat for money, then use money to buy a cow. The cow seller accepts money because they know someone else will accept it later. Trust, encoded in a shared fiction.

But money is also a store of value — it holds purchasing power over time. And a unit of account — a common way to measure value. Everything has a price in money.

What Makes Something "Money"?

History experimented with many things:

  • Commodity money — gold, silver, shells, salt, cigarettes in prisons. Valuable because it's scarce and has uses.
  • Representative money — paper notes that can be redeemed for gold. You hand in the note, you get the metal.
  • Fiat money — money because the government says so. No intrinsic value. Backed by nothing but trust.

We're on fiat money now. The dollar, euro, yen — they're all fiat. You can't exchange them for gold. Their value comes from:

  • Legal tender laws — you must accept them for debt payment
  • Scarcity — the government controls the supply
  • Trust — people believe others will accept it

When trust collapses, so does the money. Zimbabwe's trillion-dollar bills. Weimar Germany's wheelbarrows of cash. The money didn't change — the trust did.

Who Creates Money?

Most people think: governments print it. That's true for cash. But cash is a tiny fraction of the money supply. Most money — about 97% in modern economies — is created by banks.

The Magic of Fractional Reserve Banking

Here's how it works. You deposit $1,000 in a bank. The bank keeps some as reserves (say, 10% = $100) and lends out the rest ($900). That $900 goes to someone who spends it. The recipient deposits it in another bank. That bank keeps 10% ($90) and lends out $810. Repeat.

Your $1,000 became $1,000 + $900 + $810 + $729... The money multiplies. This is the money multiplier.

In theory, with 10% reserves, $1,000 becomes $10,000 of deposits. Banks create money out of nothing — they're literally typing numbers into accounts. The catch: they have to maintain enough reserves to cover withdrawals. When everyone tries to withdraw at once, the system breaks. That's a bank run.

Central Banks

The Federal Reserve (in the US), European Central Bank, Bank of Japan — they control the money supply. They do three main things:

  • Open market operations — buy/sell government bonds to inject or remove money
  • Reserve requirements — set how much banks must hold (mostly zero now)
  • Interest rates — raise rates to slow the economy, cut rates to speed it up

When the Fed "prints money" (technically: expands its balance sheet), it's usually buying bonds from banks. Banks get cash, they lend more, the money supply expands.

Why Does Money Lose Value?

Inflation. Prices rise over time because money becomes more abundant relative to goods. If the money supply grows faster than production, you have inflation.

Governments like mild inflation (around 2%). It encourages spending — money sitting around loses value. Deflation (falling prices) is terrifying because people stop spending, waiting for prices to fall further. That kills economic activity.

But inflation is a hidden tax. If you save $10,000 and inflation is 3% per year, in 10 years that $10,000 buys only ~$7,400 worth of goods. Your savings lost value whether you spent them or not.

Interest — The Price of Time

Why do you pay interest on loans? Because:

  • Inflation — the lender needs to be compensated for losing purchasing power
  • Risk — the borrower might not pay back
  • Time preference — people value present goods more than future goods

Interest rates are the anchor of the financial system. When they're low, borrowing is cheap, assets inflate, people take risks. When they're high, borrowing is expensive, assets deflate, risk gets punished.

The entire bond market, mortgage rates, and much of corporate finance revolves around the central bank's benchmark rate. A 0.5% change ripples through trillions of dollars.

Gold — The Oldest Money

Gold has been valued for 5,000 years. Why?

  • It's scarce (hard to find more)
  • It's divisible (can be split into tiny pieces)
  • It's durable (doesn't corrode)
  • It's portable (high value per weight)
  • It's uniform (one ounce = another ounce)

But gold has a problem: its supply grows slowly (about 1-2% per year). An economy needs money supply to grow with production. If you tied money to gold, you couldn't expand the money supply easily. That's why we left the gold standard in 1971 — it was too restrictive.

Crypto tries to solve this differently: mathematically capped supply (Bitcoin = 21 million max). No inflation by design. But no government backing either. Its value is purely speculative — people buy it hoping others will accept it later.

What Is Wealth?

Money is not wealth. Wealth is real stuff — houses, factories, knowledge, skills, land. Money is just a claim on wealth.

You can have lots of money and be poor (million-dollar net worth in Zimbabwe). You can have no money and be rich (own a valuable farm you haven't sold). The confusion between money and wealth is the source of endless economic misunderstandings.

When a country "prints money," it doesn't create wealth. It just dilutes the value of existing money. The real wealth — the productive capacity — hasn't changed.

The Game

Money is a game. The rules are:

  1. Governments control cash. Banks create most of the rest through lending.
  2. Inflation is permanent. Your money is always losing value.
  3. Interest is the price of time. Low rates = risk-on. High rates = risk-off.
  4. Money is a shared fiction. It works because everyone agrees it works.

Understanding the game doesn't make you rich. But it helps you see why "saving" isn't enough, why debt can be rational, and why the money supply matters even if you never touch it.

The rich don't save. They borrow. Because when money is always losing value, being in debt is a hedge against inflation — you owe "expensive" dollars with "cheap" dollars later.

That's the game.
