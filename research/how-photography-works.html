How Photography Works

March 10, 2026

Everyone has a camera now. It's in your phone, your laptop, your smart fridge probably. But most people don't understand how photographs actually get made. The magic remains hidden behind a screen that shows you the result instantly.

Photography is older than you think — the first permanent photograph was captured in 1826. But the fundamental principles haven't changed. Light hits a surface. That surface records the light. The art is controlling how much light, when it hits, and what happens to it along the way.

The Exposure Triangle

Every photograph is a balance of three things: aperture, shutter speed, and ISO. Together, they control how much light reaches your sensor (or film) and how that light looks in the final image.

The balance: These three work together. If you let in more light one way, you can afford less another way. It's a zero-sum game — every stop of light you gain somewhere, you lose somewhere else.

Aperture — The Pupil of Your Lens

Aperture is literally a hole that opens and closes. It's like the pupil of your eye — wide open in dark rooms, tiny in bright sunlight. The notation is counterintuitive: f/1.4 is actually wide open (lots of light), while f/16 is nearly closed (very little light).

But aperture does something else: it controls depth of field. A wide aperture (f/1.4) throws the background out of focus. A narrow aperture (f/16) keeps everything sharp. That's why portraits use wide apertures — the blurry background makes the subject pop.

  • f/1.4 - f/2.8: Background blur, low light, dramatic
  • f/4 - f/8: Sweet spot for sharpness, usable depth
  • f/11 - f/16: Landscape mode, everything in focus

Shutter Speed — Time Itself

Shutter speed is exactly what it sounds like: how fast the shutter opens and closes. Measured in fractions of a second:

1/1000 — extremely fast. Freezes motion. Good for sports.

1/60 — slow. You'll need steady hands or a tripod.

1" (one second) — very slow. Creates motion blur. Good for silky waterfalls.

The rule of thumb: Your shutter speed should be at least 1/(focal length). If you're shooting at 50mm, don't go below 1/50s handheld. Otherwise, you'll get camera shake blur.

But wait — there's another use: slow shutter speeds create that dreamy, flowing water effect. Fast shutter speeds freeze a hummingbird's wings mid-flight. The same scene, totally different feels.

ISO — Signal vs. Noise

ISO is your sensor's sensitivity to light. Higher ISO lets you shoot in darker conditions. But there's a cost: noise.

Think of ISO like the volume on a microphone. Turn it up too high and you get static. Same thing happens in your camera — at ISO 100, the signal is clean. At ISO 6400, you get grain.

  • ISO 100-400: Clean, professional quality
  • ISO 800-1600: Acceptable, slight noise
  • ISO 3200-6400: Use only when you have to
The ideal workflow: Set your ISO as low as possible, then adjust aperture and shutter speed. Only bump ISO when you literally can't get enough light any other way.

So What Actually Happens?

Here's the sequence when you press the shutter:

  1. Mirror flips up (in DSLRs)
  2. Shutter opens
  3. Light hits the sensor
  4. Sensor converts photons to electrical signals
  5. Analog signal → digital conversion
  6. Image processor applies settings (white balance, contrast, etc.)
  7. JPEG or RAW file written to card

In a smartphone, all of this happens in milliseconds, automatically. The phone's processor is making dozens of decisions per frame — what to expose, where to focus, how to denoise. You're not taking a photo. You're approving the AI's interpretation of one.

RAW vs. JPEG

This is the fork in the road:

JPEG is processed in-camera. Contrast, saturation, white balance — all baked in. Smaller file, ready to share. You've committed to the look.

RAW is the raw data. Like a digital negative. You get everything the sensor captured. But you have to process it yourself. It's like having the original film negative — more work, more control.

The Takeaway

Photography isn't magic. It's physics and tradeoffs. More light = more options. Every setting you adjust has a cost somewhere else in the triangle. The professionals aren't better because they have better gear. They're better because they understand the exchanges.

Your phone hides all of this from you — and that's fine, for most photos. But when you want to take control, when you want the background blurred or the motion frozen or the stars trails across the sky, you need to understand what's actually happening inside the box.

Start with one setting. Go outside. Shoot the same thing at f/1.4 and f/16. Compare. That's how you learn. Not by reading — by doing.
