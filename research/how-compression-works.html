How Compression Works

March 8, 2026

You download a file. It's 10 megabytes. You extract it. Now it's 100 megabytes. What just happened? You got robbed — but legally. Compression is the art of storing more information in less space, and it touches everything from zipping files to streaming video to the words you're reading right now.

The Fundamental Limit

There's a law that governs all compression: the Shannon entropy limit. Developed by Claude Shannon in 1948, it states that any file has a minimum possible size — its entropy. You cannot compress below this limit without losing information.

Random data has high entropy. It looks like white noise. There's no pattern to exploit, no shortcut. That's why compressing already-compressed files (like .zip inside .zip) often makes them larger — you're adding compression overhead with no actual redundancy to remove.

Predictable data has low entropy. A text file in English only uses a fraction of the 256 possible byte values. Repeated patterns, common letters, structured formats — all of this is redundancy waiting to be squeezed out.

Two Worlds: Lossless vs Lossy

Compression splits into two families:

Lossless Compression

You can reconstruct the original exactly. No information lost. This is what you want for text, code, databases, and anything where a single bit matters. ZIP, gzip, PNG, and FLAC all use lossless compression.

Lossy Compression

You throw away stuff humans won't notice. A blurry sky pixel doesn't hurt. The 13th harmonic of a violin's E string at low volume doesn't matter. JPEG, MP3, and MPEG discard the imperceptible to achieve ratios lossless algorithms can only dream of. Once discarded, it's gone forever — you can't decompress back to the original.

The choice is simple: exactness or efficiency? For data that matters, lossless. For media you're willing to sacrifice, lossy.

Dictionary Coding: Finding Repeats

The oldest trick in the book: replace repeated sequences with shorter references.

Consider this text: the quick brown fox jumps over the lazy dog the end

"the" appears three times. Instead of storing it each time, we build a dictionary:

  • Position 0: "the"
  • Position 5: "quick"
  • ...

Now we encode: [0] quick brown fox jumps over [0] lazy dog [0] end — each reference takes less space than the word itself.

LZ77: The Classic Algorithm

Abraham Lempel and Jacob Ziv published LZ77 in 1977. It works by looking backwards in the already-encoded data for matches:

  • When you see a repeated sequence, output a (distance, length) pair — "go back X characters and copy Y bytes"
  • When there's no match, output a literal character

That's it. That's the core of gzip, ZIP, and most modern compressors. DEFLATE — the algorithm inside gzip and ZIP — combines LZ77 with Huffman coding (more on that in a moment).

LZW: The GIF Disaster

Lempel-Ziv-Welch (LZW) builds its dictionary as it goes, starting with single characters and adding longer sequences. It was the algorithm behind the original GIF format — and the reason Unisys held the world hostage with patent lawsuits for decades.

The patent expired in 2003. GIF died. PNG won. But LZW lives on in other formats.

Huffman Coding: Playing the Odds

If "E" appears 10,000 times in a file and "Z" appears 10 times, why give them the same space? Huffman coding assigns shorter codes to common symbols and longer codes to rare ones.

Building the Tree

  1. Count how often each symbol appears
  2. Put each symbol in its own leaf node with its frequency
  3. Repeatedly combine the two least-frequent nodes into a new parent node
  4. Assign 0 to left branches, 1 to right branches
  5. The path from root to leaf is your code

Common letters get short codes: "E" might become "1". Rare letters get long codes: "Z" might become "1101011". Average code length approaches the theoretical minimum.

Why It Works

In standard ASCII, every character is 8 bits. In Huffman-coded English, "E" is 1 bit, "A" is 2 bits, "Z" might be 9 bits. The math works out: most files shrink significantly.

Huffman is optimal for symbol-by-symbol encoding — but it's blind to patterns. That's why DEFLATE combines it with LZ77: LZ77 finds repeated sequences, Huffman encodes the results optimally.

DEFLATE: The Workhorse

Every zip file, every gzipped web response, every PNG image uses DEFLATE. It's the most successful compression algorithm in history because it's fast, simple, and effective.

DEFLATE is a pipeline:

  1. LZ77 encoding — find repeated sequences, replace with back-references
  2. Literal/length distance encoding — represent symbols as integers ready for Huffman
  3. Huffman coding — assign short codes to common symbols, long codes to rare ones

The magic is that both stages are tunable. You can use more aggressive LZ matching (longer back-references) or more aggressive Huffman coding (finer symbol grouping). Different compressors optimize differently, which is why gzip and zip files of the same data often have slightly different sizes.

Burrows-Wheeler: The Rearrangement Trick

bzip2 uses a different approach: rearrange the data to make it easier to compress, not compress the data itself.

The Burrows-Wheeler transform (BWT) takes all possible rotations of the input, sorts them, and outputs the last column. This clusters similar characters together — runs of repeated letters become much longer. Then a simple move-to-front transform and Huffman coding finish the job.

It's slower than DEFLATE but often achieves better compression, especially for text. That's why .bz2 files are sometimes smaller than .gz files.

What About Images and Video?

PNG: Lossless for Images

PNG uses DEFLATE but applies a preprocessing step: filter each row with predictors (like "the pixel to my left") before compressing. This reduces entropy dramatically.

JPEG: Lossy for Photos

JPEG does something completely different. It:

  • Converts RGB to YCbCr (luminance + color)
  • Chunks the image into 8×8 blocks
  • Applies Discrete Cosine Transform (DCT) — turns pixel values into frequency components
  • Quantizes the frequencies — divides by a quality factor, discarding high frequencies
  • Runs the result through Huffman coding

The human eye is less sensitive to color than luminance, and less sensitive to high-frequency detail than low. JPEG exploits both. Crank up the quality slider and it discards more. Crank it down and you get blocky artifacts.

MP3 and AAC: Lossy for Audio

Audio compression exploits psychoacoustics — the science of what humans hear. Key tricks:

  • Masking — a loud sound makes nearby quiet sounds inaudible
  • Frequency cutoff — humans hear 20Hz-20kHz, but most audio content doesn't need the extremes
  • Stereo redundancy — left and right channels are often similar

MP4/H.264: Lossy for Video

Video compression is where things get wild. You don't just compress each frame — you compress the differences between frames. Most of a video frame is the same as the previous one. Why store it twice?

Techniques include:

  • Motion compensation — describe how blocks of pixels moved
  • Keyframes — store full frames periodically
  • Bidirectional prediction — use both past and future frames to predict the current one

H.264 can compress video 100x or more while remaining watchable. That's why streaming Netflix works at all.

The Practical Limits

What can you actually compress? It depends on the data:

  • Plain text — 60-70% reduction typical
  • Source code — 60-70% (similar to text, repetitive keywords)
  • JSON/data — 50-80% (depends on redundancy)
  • Images — PNG: 30-50%, JPEG: 90%+ (lossy)
  • Audio — MP3: 90%+, FLAC: 40-50%
  • Video — 95-99% for streaming quality

Once you've removed obvious redundancy, you hit diminishing returns. The last few percentage points cost enormous complexity.

What This Means

Every time you load a web page, your browser decompresses multiple files — gzipped HTML, compressed CSS, optimized images. Every time you stream a video, your device is running complex decode pipelines at 60 frames per second. Every time you create a ZIP file, you're using algorithms developed over decades.

Compression isn't a feature. It's invisible infrastructure. And understanding it means understanding one of the fundamental limits of information: some things can be squeezed, but nothing can be squeezed below its entropy.
