How Compression Works

March 9, 2026

Compression is the art of making things smaller without losing what matters. Every compressed file on your computer is a small miracle of mathematics — patterns found, redundancy eliminated, bits reclaimed. But how does a machine know what to keep and what to throw away?

The Fundamental Insight

All compression rests on one idea: redundancy. Data almost always contains patterns. The letter "e" appears more often than "z". Adjacent pixels in an image are often the same color. A video frame usually looks similar to the one before it.

Compression algorithms find these patterns and encode them more efficiently. Instead of storing "00000000" (eight zeros), you store "0 × 8". The information is the same. The representation is shorter.

Lossless vs Lossy

Everything compresses, but not everything survives intact.

Lossless compression guarantees perfect reconstruction. The decompressed data is bit-for-bit identical to the original. PNG, ZIP, gzip, FLAC — all lossless. You need this for text, code, executables, and any data where a single wrong bit breaks everything.

Lossy compression sacrifices detail for size. JPEG throws away colors your eye can't distinguish. MP3 removes frequencies you probably can't hear. The decompressed data is similar but not identical. Acceptable for photos, audio, video — where human perception fills in the gaps.

Entropy and Coding

Claude Shannon defined entropy as the minimum number of bits needed to represent information. If a symbol appears 50% of the time, you can encode it in 1 bit. If it appears 1% of the time, you need about 7 bits.

Huffman coding builds optimal prefix codes. It assigns shorter codes to frequent symbols and longer codes to rare ones. The tree structure ensures no code is a prefix of another — decoding is unambiguous.

Optimal coding matches symbol frequency to code length. That's it. That's the whole trick.

Dictionary Methods: LZ77 and Friends

Huffman treats each symbol independently. But real data has repeated sequences. LZ77 (Lempel-Ziv 1977) finds repeated patterns and replaces them with references to earlier occurrences.

Imagine a file containing "the quick brown fox jumps over the lazy dog". Instead of storing "the" twice, the second occurrence becomes a pointer: "back 40 bytes, length 3". The dictionary is the data itself.

DEFLATE — used in ZIP, gzip, PNG — combines LZ77 with Huffman coding. First, LZ77 finds repeated sequences. Then, Huffman compresses the resulting symbols and lengths. Two layers of compression, one file.

How JPEG Works

JPEG is lossy and complicated, but the core ideas are intuitive:

  1. Convert to color space — Separate brightness (Y) from color (Cb, Cr). We perceive brightness sharply, color roughly.
  2. Divide into blocks — 8×8 pixel blocks. Each block is transformed mathematically.
  3. DCT (Discrete Cosine Transform) — Converts spatial pixels into frequency components. Low frequencies (gradual changes) vs high frequencies (edges, noise).
  4. Quantize — Divide each frequency by a quality-dependent number and round. This throws away high frequencies — details your eye misses.
  5. Huffman code — Compress the quantized result.

More aggressive quantization = smaller file = more artifacts. The art is finding the cutoff where artifacts become visible.

The Tradeoffs

Compression is always a tradeoff:

  • Compression ratio vs speed — Better compression usually means more CPU time. Brotli beats gzip but takes longer.
  • Lossy vs lossless — You can't recover what you threw away. But sometimes "good enough" beats "perfect but too large".
  • General vs specialized — gzip works on anything. Special-purpose codecs (WebP, AVIF, Opus) beat it on their target media but fail elsewhere.

What Matters in Practice

For text and code: gzip or Brotli. Fast, effective, universal. Enable them on your web server — they're free performance.

For images: WebP or AVIF. Both support lossy and lossless. AVIF has better compression; WebP has better compatibility.

For video: H.264 for compatibility, H.265/AV1 for efficiency. The codec wars aren't over, but AV1 is the current best bet for minimum size.

For archives: 7z with LZMA2. Slow to compress, excellent ratio. ZIP for speed and compatibility.

The Bigger Picture

Compression isn't just about saving disk space. It's about moving bits faster, fitting more in bandwidth-constrained channels, reducing storage costs at scale. Every megabyte not sent over the network is a millisecond saved.

But there's a floor. Information has entropy. You can't compress random data at all — there's no pattern to find. The ceiling is set by how well your algorithm matches the data's actual structure.

The art is choosing the right tool for the job — and understanding what you're trading away.
