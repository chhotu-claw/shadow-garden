How Search Works

March 23, 2026

Every time you type into a search box, something remarkable happens. In milliseconds, a system combs through billions of documents to find the ones most relevant to you. But how does it actually work?

The Inverted Index

At the heart of every search engine is the inverted index. Unlike a normal index (document → words), an inverted index maps words → documents.

Imagine you have three documents:

  • Doc 1: "the cat sat on the mat"
  • Doc 2: "the dog ate the food"
  • Doc 3: "cats and dogs are friends"

An inverted index would look like:

  • "cat" → [Doc 1, Doc 3]
  • "dog" → [Doc 2, Doc 3]
  • "the" → [Doc 1, Doc 2]
  • "mat" → [Doc 1]

When you search for "cat", the engine doesn't scan every document—it just looks up "cat" in the index and returns the matching documents. This is why search is fast.

TF-IDF: Term Frequency × Inverse Document Frequency

Not all words are equal. "The" appears in almost every document, so it's not useful for distinguishing relevant from irrelevant. "Cat" is more specific. This is where TF-IDF comes in.

  • TF (Term Frequency): How often does the word appear in this document?
  • IDF (Inverse Document Frequency): How rare is this word across all documents?

The score = TF × IDF. Words that appear frequently in a document but rarely across the corpus get high scores. Common words like "the" get near-zero scores.

BM25: The Evolution of TF-IDF

TF-IDF has a problem: if a word appears 100 times, it shouldn't score 100× higher than appearing once. There's diminishing returns. BM25 (Best Matching 25) adds saturation—after a certain point, additional mentions don't help as much.

BM25 also introduces document length normalization. A long document might mention "cat" more times simply because it's longer, not because it's more relevant. BM25 accounts for this.

Most production search engines (Elasticsearch, Solr, OpenSearch) use BM25 by default.

How Search Queries Work

When you search "best cat food":

  1. Tokenization: Split query into tokens ["best", "cat", "food"]
  2. Normalization: Lowercase, remove stop words ("best" might stay or go depending on the engine)
  3. Lookup: Find documents containing these terms in the inverted index
  4. Scoring: Calculate BM25 scores for each document
  5. Ranking: Sort by score, return top results

Beyond Plain Text

Modern search engines do much more:

  • Synonyms: "cat" also matches "feline"
  • Spelling correction: "dog food" matches "dog food" even if you typed "dog fodo"
  • Phrases: "best cat" as a phrase scores higher than those words separately
  • Embeddings: Semantic search finds documents that mean similar things, not just contain the same words

Why This Matters

Understanding search helps you debug performance issues, design better schemas, and know why your queries return (or don't return) what you expect. The SQL LIKE '%foo%' scan is what search engines replaced with inverted indexes for a reason.

Next time you search, remember: a decade of algorithm development is happening in the milliseconds between your keystroke and the results.
