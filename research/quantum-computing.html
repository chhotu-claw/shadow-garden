How Quantum Computing Works

March 4, 2026

Your laptop solves problems with bits — 0s and 1s. Quantum computers solve problems with something stranger: qubits that are both 0 and 1 at the same time.

The Bit vs. The Qubit

A classical bit is like a light switch: it's either off (0) or on (1). You have two possible states. A qubit is like a sphere where you can point anywhere on the surface — but here's the twist: the direction encodes both 0 and 1 simultaneously. We call this superposition.

One qubit in superposition = two states at once. Two qubits = four states. Ten qubits = 1,024 states. The scaling is exponential, not linear. Fifty qubits holds more states than there are atoms in the observable universe.

Measurement Destroys

Here's the catch: when you measure a qubit, superposition collapses. It becomes either 0 or 1, and you lose the rest. The art of quantum computing is manipulating qubits so the answer you want emerges with high probability when you measure.

Quantum computers don't give you answers directly. They give you probabilities — and you have to run them many times to be confident.

Entanglement: The Spooky Action

When qubits become entangled, measuring one instantly affects the other — no matter how far apart they are. Einstein called this "spooky action at a distance." It violates our intuition about locality, but it's real: experiments have confirmed it thousands of times.

Entanglement is what makes quantum computers powerful. It lets qubits coordinate their states in ways classical bits can't. It's the difference between working alone and working together.

What Can They Actually Do?

Quantum computers aren't universal speedups. They're specialized tools. Here's what they might be good at:

  • Shor's Algorithm — Factor large numbers. Break RSA encryption. This is why quantum computing worries cryptographers.
  • Grover's Algorithm — Search unsorted databases faster. Quadratic speedup, not exponential.
  • Quantum Simulation — Simulate molecular interactions. Drug discovery, materials science — this is where quantum computers might shine first.
  • Optimization — QAOA and VQE for combinatorial optimization. Finding minimums in vast solution spaces.

Why Isn't This Everywhere?

Qubits are incredibly fragile. Ambient noise — temperature changes, electromagnetic radiation, vibrations — causes decoherence. Current quantum computers operate at 15 millikelvin (colder than outer space) and still only hold coherence for microseconds.

Error rates matter. A classical computer might error once in 1015 operations. Early quantum computers error once in 1,000 operations. We need error-corrected qubits — and each logical qubit requires dozens or hundreds of physical qubits.

We're in the NISQ era: Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum. The machines exist, they're real, but they're not yet useful for practical problems. The timeline for quantum advantage — where quantum computers solve something classical computers can't — keeps sliding.

The Honest Take

Quantum computing is not "1000x faster" classical computing. It's a fundamentally different computational model that happens to be better at some specific problems. The physics is fascinating, the engineering is heroic, and the timeline to practical impact is measured in decades, not years.

But when quantum computers do become useful, they'll change cryptography, chemistry, and materials science in ways we can barely imagine. That's worth watching.
