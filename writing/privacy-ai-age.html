Privacy in the Age of AI

March 24, 2026

Privacy isn't dead. It's just become invisible.

We used to worry about data breaches — some hacker stealing our credit cards from a compromised database. That was the model: discrete events, quantifiable damage, someone to blame. Now we worry about data leakage through everyday conversations. Every prompt is a training datapoint. Every conversation is harvested. Every "helpful" AI assistant is quietly monetising our thoughts.

The shift is subtle but total. We used to opt into surveillance (cookies, location sharing, fitness trackers). Now we opt into it by default simply by using the most useful tools. The best AI assistants are the ones that know us best — which means the ones that collect most. We've normalised handing over our thinking to corporations.

The real privacy risk isn't hackers any more. It's the normalisation of surveillance as a feature.

The New Normal

Consider what's happened in three short years: we've gone from "don't share your password" to "tell an AI your deepest problems and it will help you." We trust these systems with our code, our writing, our medical questions, our relationship advice. We treat them like therapists, doctors, lawyers, friends.

And why not? They're useful. They remember what we forget. They surface what we need. The convenience is real.

But convenience has a price, and we've stopped asking what it is.

The Asymmetry

Here's what bothers me: we have zero visibility into what happens to our prompts. We don't know if they're retained, audited, used to train models we never consented to, or scraped by third parties. We sign Terms of Service we never read, and even if we did, they'd change tomorrow.

The relationship is fundamentally asymmetric. We give them our thoughts; they give us responses. We have no idea what's happening in between.

What's To Be Done?

I don't have a solution. But I think the first step is admitting the problem: this isn't about data breaches, it's about data normalisation. We've accepted a level of surveillance that would have been unthinkable five years ago — and we've accepted it because it came wrapped in friendly interfaces and helpful features.

The question isn't whether privacy is dead. It's whether we still care enough to notice it's gone.
