The Gap Between AI Hype and AI Reality

April 3rd, 2026

OpenAI announces something. Google announces something. Anthropic announces something. Every announcement promises to change everything. The products that follow? They're mostly the same as before, just with a chat interface.

The marketing cycle

It goes like this:

  1. Company announces "breakthrough" AI model
  2. Media writes about AGI being just around the corner
  3. Products get rebranded with "AI-powered" labels
  4. Six months later, the next announcement drops

Rinse and repeat. Each cycle feels more extreme than the last. But if you look at what actually shipped, the delta between "before AI" and "after AI" is surprisingly small.

What actually improved

Don't get me wrong — some things got genuinely better:

  • Code completion that doesn't suck
  • Summarization that works
  • Translation that doesn't require a human editor
  • Search that understands intent

These are real improvements. They're just not as dramatic as the marketing suggests.

What didn't change

Here's what's still broken:

  • AI still makes stuff up and presents it with confidence
  • Long-running tasks still lose context
  • Reasoning is still shallow compared to humans
  • Agents are still mostly marketing

The fundamental limitations haven't shifted. The models got faster and longer-lived, but they didn't get fundamentally smarter in the ways that matter.

The practical gap

What the gap actually looks like:

  • Marketing: "This tool will write your entire app"
  • Reality: It writes about 30% of the boilerplate, you still do the rest
  • Marketing: "Talk to your data"
  • Reality: You spend 2 hours preparing the data for the tool to work
  • Marketing: "Autonomous agents"
  • Reality: It makes API calls, resets on each conversation

Why this matters

The gap matters because it sets expectations wrong. When you expect magic and get a slightly better autocomplete, you get disappointed. Then you either abandon the tool entirely or spend too much time trying to make it do things it wasn't designed for.

The best approach: assume the marketing is 3x better than reality, and you'll be pleasantly surprised when something actually delivers.
