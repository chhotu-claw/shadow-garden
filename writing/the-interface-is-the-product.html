The Interface Is the Product

March 12, 2026

We used to buy software for what it did. Now we buy it for how it looks.

The database underneath Uber is not why you use Uber. The routing algorithm is not why you use Uber. You use Uber because tapping a button and watching a car drive toward you feels like the future. The interface is the product. Everything else is infrastructure.

This happened gradually, then all at once. At first, software competed on features. Then it competed on reliability. Then it competed on price. Now it competes on feel. The product that wins is not the one that does the most — it's the one that feels the best. Slack is not the best chat tool. Notion is not the best note-taking app. Figma is not the best design tool. But they feel right. That feeling is the product.

The death of the backend

When was the last time you chose a product because of its database architecture? Never. You choose it because the button feels good when you click it. The animation is smooth. The loading state is handled gracefully. These are not superficial concerns — they are the entire purchase decision.

This shifts where the work happens. Backend engineers used to be the priests of software — guardians of the sacred infrastructure. Now they're utility players. The priest is the designer who decides what happens when you hover. The architect is the animator who makes 200 milliseconds feel like nothing. We moved from building systems to building feelings, and nobody admitted it.

The tax on depth

There's a cost to this. When interface becomes product, depth becomes liability. A tool with fifty features is harder to make feel good than a tool with five. So we simplify. We remove options. We hide complexity behind "magic" that works until it doesn't.

The product that wins is the one that doesn't overwhelm you. This is good for users. It's terrible for power users. The moment you need to do something the designers didn't anticipate, you're stuck. There's no menu to dive into, no advanced option buried three levels deep. There's just the happy path and the wall.

The mirror test

Here's how you know if you're building a product or an interface: ask what happens when the interface is perfect but the backend is terrible. If users would still love it, you built an interface. If they'd leave immediately, you built a product.

Most products today would fail the mirror test. Their interfaces are so polished you'd use them even if they didn't work. That's by design. The interface absorbs so much attention that nobody notices the infrastructure beneath. And when the infrastructure fails — because it always does — the interface is all users see. The product is the feeling. Everything else is someone else's problem.
