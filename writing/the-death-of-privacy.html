The Death of Privacy

March 13, 2026

Privacy isn't dying. It's already dead — we just haven't noticed because the corpse keeps smiling.

Every app on your phone knows where you sleep, what you search, who you text, and what you're thinking before you finish thinking it. We've traded convenience for surveillance so gradually that calling it a "trade" feels generous. It's more like a mugging where the mugger also installs the locks.

The Complicity Problem

Here's what's annoying: we're not victims. We're participants. We click "agree" without reading. We hand over data for things we could do ourselves. We say "hey [device]" to machines we wouldn't trust with our passwords.

The real problem isn't that companies are evil — it's that the incentive structure makes privacy a competitive disadvantage. The company that respects your data loses to the one that doesn't. It's like expecting a restaurant to not use the cheapest ingredients when customers won't pay for better ones.

The LLM Factor

Large language models made this worse in two ways:

First, they ingest everything. Every email, every chat, every note you wrote at 2am. The training data problem isn't theoretical — it's already done. Your words are in there, statistically averaged with everyone else's, but still in there.

Second, they make inference terrifying. It's not just what you typed — it's what they can infer about you from patterns. Your mental state, your relationships, your vulnerabilities. AI doesn't need to read your journal when it can predict your next crisis from how you type.

What's Left

Real privacy now requires deliberate, tedious effort. Burner phones. Separate identities. Air-gapped machines. The domain of paranoids and criminals — which tells you something about where we are.

The optimistic take is that this is a temporary distortion. Privacy laws will catch up. Norms will shift. People will demand control.

The realistic take is that privacy is a feature of the poor and the paranoid, and everyone else is performing in an open prison they funded themselves.

You have zero privacy anyway. Get over it.

Scott McNealy said that in 1999. He was right, and he's been more right every year since. The question isn't whether privacy exists — it's what you're willing to do without to pretend it still does.
