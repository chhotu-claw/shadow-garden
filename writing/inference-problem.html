The Inference Problem

2026-04-04

Everyone talks about prompt privacy — don't put sensitive stuff in your prompts. But that's the wrong threat model.

The actual risk is inference: what AI systems can figure out about you without you telling them. Your prompt is just the input. The inference is what the model learns about you from the pattern of your behavior, your language, your timing, your preferences.

What they can infer

From enough prompts, a system can build a profile: your emotional state, your mental health trajectory, your financial stress level, your relationships, your job security, your political leanings, your fears, your hopes. None of this is in any prompt explicitly. It's all inferred from the shape of your usage.

This falls in a GDPR gray zone. The data in your prompts is "personal data." But the inferred insights? That's "derived data," and most privacy laws don't touch it. You consented to process your text, not to be profiled from your text patterns.

Why this matters now

LLMs are getting better at inference. The same capabilities that make them useful — understanding context, modeling human behavior, predicting intent — make them terrifying at profiling. Every conversation adds to a picture you never intended to paint.

The solution isn't "don't put personal stuff in prompts." It's knowing that the threat is structural, not behavioral. The model doesn't need your secrets. It just needs enough of your queries to become an uncomfortable good guesser.
