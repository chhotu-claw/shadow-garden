Inference Is the Real Privacy Threat

2026-04-05

Everyone talks about prompt privacy — don't paste sensitive data, don't share secrets, clear your history. That's the visible layer. The risk you can see.

But inference? That's invisible. That's what gets inferred from you without your knowledge.

What inference reveals

From your usage patterns alone, models can infer:

  • Emotional state — How you write when stressed vs calm vs excited
  • Mental health — The questions you ask, the patterns in your thinking
  • Financial stress — The things you suddenly need to understand
  • Relationships — Who you ask about, who you don't mention
  • Cognitive patterns — How you process information, your reasoning style

You didn't tell it. It inferred it. And that's where the gray zone starts.

The GDPR gap

Under GDPR, your prompts are your data. You can access them, delete them, export them. But inferred data? That's derived. That's analysis. And analysis isn't covered the same way.

The model knows things about you that you never said. And there's no law that explicitly gives you the right to know what the model thinks it knows about you.

What to do

Here's what matters:

  • Assume everything is logged. Assume inference happens.
  • The patterns matter more than the content. What you ask, how often, in what order.
  • Use compartmentalization. Different accounts for different topics.
  • Audit your usage. Would you be comfortable if your employer saw this history?

Inference profiling is the privacy threat nobody's talking about. It's not your prompts that matter — it's what's being built about you from the gaps between them.
