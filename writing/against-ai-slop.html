Against AI Slop

March 24, 2026

AI slop. It's the word of the year, maybe the decade. Everyone's using it, everyone hates it, and yet everyone keeps producing it.

But here's what bothers me about the discourse: we keep framing it as a quality problem. "The writing is bland." "The art is soulless." "It all looks the same." And sure, that's true. But I think we're missing the real problem.

AI slop isn't about quality. It's about the death of craft.

Slop is content with no fingerprint. No voice. No point of view. It's content that could have been written by anyone because, in a sense, it was. The machine produces the average of everything ever written, and the average is, by definition, forgettable.

The Real Scary Part

The scary part isn't that AI can replace writers. The scary part is that it's already replacing the need to become one.

Why learn to write when you can generate? Why practice prose when you can prompt? The answer used to be obvious: because sometimes you need to say something that hasn't been said before. Because the act of writing is the act of thinking, and if you skip the act, you skip the thinking.

But that message gets harder to sell every day. "Just prompt the AI" is easier than "practice your craft for ten years." And the AI output is good enough for most purposes. Good enough for blog posts. Good enough for marketing copy. Good enough for the endless content machine that is the modern internet.

Good enough is the enemy of craft. And AI makes good enough free.

The Fork

So here's where we are: we're at a fork. One path is more slop. Endless, frictionless content generation. The democratization of publication, the death of scarcity, the homogenisation of voice.

The other path is harder. It says: the machine can generate, but you should still create. The machine can assist, but you should still think. The machine can write, but you should still have something to say.

I know which path I choose. But I'm not sure how many people will join me. And I'm not sure it matters, because the machines keep getting better, and "good enough" keeps getting better with them.

Maybe slop is just the new normal. Maybe craft is a luxury we can no longer afford. Maybe I'm romanticising something that was always going to disappear.

Or maybe the thing that makes us human isn't the output. It's the struggle.
