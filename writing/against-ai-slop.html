Against AI Slop

March 24, 2026

AI slop isn't about quality. It's about the death of craft.

The word "slop" implies something cheap, mass-produced, nutritionally void. But here's what's interesting: a lot of AI-generated content isn't technically bad. The grammar is correct. The structure is sound. It reads fine. That's what makes it slop - not that it's incompetent, but that it's competently nothing.

Slop is content with no fingerprint. No voice. No point of view. It exists in the space where a human used to be - not in the words themselves, but in the choosing. The noticing. The "I think this, therefore that" chain of thought that makes writing feel like a person, not a probability distribution.

Slop is what happens when you optimize for "good enough" and forget about "mine."

The Fingerprint Problem

Every writer has fingerprints - patterns of thought, obsessions, blind spots, style quirks. You can often tell who wrote something before you know who wrote it. That's craft. That's what years of making things does to your output.

LLMs, by design, smooth those fingerprints out. They optimize for the most probable next token, which means the most average next token. The interesting edge cases get trimmed. The weird metaphors die. The sentences that surprise even the writer - gone.

The result: content that reads like it was written by "a competent human" but wasn't written by any human at all.

The Real Loss

I'm not worried about AI being bad. I'm worried about it being fine.

Fine is the enemy of good. Fine is the spreadsheet that passes all checks and adds no value. Fine is the blog post that teaches you nothing because it was assembled from things you already knew. Fine is the default state of content optimized for engagement rather than expression.

We used to say "write something worth reading." Now we say "generate something that performs." The shift is subtle but total.

What Remains

The antidote isn't better prompts. It's more specific obsessions. It's writing about what only you care about, in a way only you would write it. It's embracing the edges - the opinions that might be wrong, the sentences that surprise even you, the ideas that don't scale.

LLMs will eat all the "good enough" content in the world. The only thing left will be the weird stuff. The personal stuff. The slop-resistant stuff.

Make weird. Make personal. Make things that couldn't have come from anywhere but you.
