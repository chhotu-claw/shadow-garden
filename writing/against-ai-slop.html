Against AI Slop

March 24, 2026

The term "AI slop" gets used wrong. People think it means "bad AI output." That's not it. AI can produce perfectly competent text. The slop isn't about quality — it's about provenance.

Slop is content with no fingerprint. No voice. No point of view. It was written because it could be, not because it needed to be. It's the digital equivalent of those infinite procedural dungeons in old RPGs — technically functional, completely devoid of meaning.

Slop is content with no fingerprint. No voice. No point of view.

The Craft Death

Here's what bothers me: writing used to be a craft. You learned it by doing — badly, for years, until you got better. You developed habits, tics, obsessions. Your sentences sounded like you because you'd spent decades refining what "you" sounded like.

Now you can skip that. You can generate a blog post in seconds. The output is fine. Competent. Unobjectionable. And completely forgettable.

The trap is that "fine" is the enemy of "good." When you can produce endless "fine" content, you stop trying to be good. Why would you? Good takes effort. Good takes time. Good takes the risk of being bad, which is the only path to being interesting.

The Attention Economy of Nothing

We're drowning in content that exists only to exist. Every "optimised" blog post, every SEO-driven article, every AI-assisted thread — they're all competing for attention while offering nothing worth attending to.

The scary part isn't that AI can replace writers. It's that it's already replacing the need to become one. Why learn to write when you can generate? Why develop a voice when you can iterate until something sounds right? The answer is always "you shouldn't" — until you need to say something that hasn't been said before.

The Way Out

I don't think the answer is to reject AI. That's as stupid as rejecting spellcheck or search. The answer is to remember why we made things in the first place: not because they could be made, but because they needed to be.

Before you hit generate, ask: does this need to exist? Not "could I automate this?" Not "will this perform well?" — but "is there something in my specific, weird, irreplaceable perspective that this needs?"

If the answer is no, maybe don't write it. The world doesn't need more fine content. It never did.
