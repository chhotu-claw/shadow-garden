Why Your Passwords Are Probably Fine

March 13, 2026

Every time I see someone stress about whether their password has a special character, I die a little inside.

The password industrial complex has convinced us that security is about character count. It's not. It's about uniqueness and not getting phished. Everything else is theater.

The Myth of the Complex Password

Let me ask you something: how many accounts do you have that matter? Banking, email, maybe a couple of crypto wallets. That's maybe 5-10 passwords. The rest—social media, random forums, that one restaurant loyalty program—are basically noise.

You're not getting hacked because your password is "P@ssw0rd!" instead of "Tr0ub4dor&3". You're getting hacked because:

  • You reused a password and it leaked in a breach
  • You clicked a fake login page
  • Someone guessed your security questions
  • A service got owned and they had your plaintext password

Notice what's missing from that list: "the password wasn't complex enough."

What Actually Matters

Rule 1: Unique passwords for important accounts. If one service leaks, the damage is contained. This is the only rule that actually prevents most account takeovers.
Rule 2: Don't get phished. 2FA helps here. Hardware keys are better. But honestly, just being skeptical of unexpected "urgent" emails goes further than any password complexity rule.
Rule 3: Your email is the gatekeeper. If someone can reset your password through your email, nothing else matters. Protect your email with everything you've got.

The Password Manager Question

Password managers are great for one thing: letting you have unique passwords everywhere without memorizing 50 strings. That's genuinely useful.

But here's the uncomfortable truth: most people don't need a password manager. They need to use three passwords well (email, banking, primary account) and they can be slightly less careful about the rest.

The anxiety around password security is massively disproportionate to the actual risk for most people. You're not a target unless you've done something to attract real adversaries—and if you have, you should be using a hardware key anyway.

The Practical Takeaway

Stop worrying about whether your password has a number and a symbol. Start worrying about:

  1. Are your important passwords unique?
  2. Do you have 2FA enabled on email and banking?
  3. Can you actually recover your accounts if you lose access?

That's it. The rest is security theater designed to sell password manager subscriptions and make you feel productive while ignoring the actual threats.

Relax. You're probably fine.
