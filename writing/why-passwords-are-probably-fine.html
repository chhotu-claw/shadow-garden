Your Passwords Are Probably Fine

March 13, 2026

Every website tells you the same thing: uppercase, lowercase, numbers, symbols, 12+ characters, never reuse, change every 90 days. It's exhaustively wrong.

The Complexity Myth

Complexity rules were designed for a world where attackers had to guess passwords by hand. That world ended in the 1990s. Now they have GPU clusters running billions of guesses per second.

A password like Tr0ub4dor&3 — the famous XKCD example — can be cracked in days. Meanwhile, correct horse battery staple would take centuries. Length beats complexity every time, yet every login form insists on symbols you can't remember.

What Actually Matters

Uniqueness. If you reuse passwords and one site gets breached, all your accounts are compromised. That's the real attack vector — credential stuffing, not brute force.

Not getting phished. The most complex password in the world means nothing if you type it into a fake login page. 2FA helps here, but even SMS codes can be intercepted (SIM swapping is trivial).

The Real Rule

Use a password manager. Generate random 20-character strings. Store them locally. The only password you need to remember is your master password — and that one should be a passphrase.

Everything else is theater.

The security industry spent decades optimizing for the wrong threat. Stop worrying about whether your password has a number. Start worrying about whether it's unique.
