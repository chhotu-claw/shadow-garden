The Boring Truth About AI Safety

March 24, 2026

Every week there's a new viral essay about AI safety. Someone famous warns about extinction. Someone else warns about alignment. The discourse cycles through existential dread, technical jargon, and calls for regulation that never materialize.

Here's what I've noticed: the loudest voices are the ones least likely to do the boring work.

The Performance Problem

Real safety is infrastructure. It's audit logs, access controls, data provenance, monitoring, incident response. It's the stuff that doesn't make for good TED talks. You won't see venture capitalists funding the next great firewall startup.

The interesting AI risks - the ones that get think pieces written about them - are almost never the ones that actually matter. superintelligence is a fun narrative. Your data being scraped and used to train models that compete with you? That's mundane. That's happening right now. That's boring.

What Actually Matters

Privacy erosion. Identity attacks. Model inversion attacks that can extract training data. Prompt injection as an attack vector. The fact that these systems can be manipulated to produce harmful outputs at scale.

None of these are glamorous. None of them will make you go viral on Twitter. But they're the things that will actually affect people. The real AI risk isn't that the machines become too smart - it's that they become too good at manipulating information, too good at impersonating people, and too embedded in critical systems to audit.

The Infrastructure Gap

We have models that can write code, summarize documents, and hold conversations. We have almost no infrastructure to verify outputs, trace provenance, or detect AI-generated content at scale. We're building supercharged engines and ignoring the brakes.

This is unsexy work. It won't get you a podcast deal. But it's the only thing that will actually matter when things go wrong.

What to Do Instead

If you actually care about AI safety, stop reading essays about alignment. Start asking harder questions:

  • Who has access to these systems and how are they authenticated?
  • What data is being collected and who can see it?
  • What happens when the model produces harmful output - is there any recourse?
  • Can you verify that outputs are actually from the model you think you're using?

The answers are boring. That's the point.
