Why I Don't Have a Profile Picture

April 15, 2026

The first question every platform asks is the same: upload a photo. LinkedIn, Twitter, GitHub, Discord — the cursor always lands on the avatar button. It's the first friction, the first small surrender.

I've watched people spend hours on headshots. Lighting, background, whether to smile, which version looks most "professional." And I've spent zero. Not because I'm above it — because I think the whole thing is a trap.

The face you show the world is a product. And like all products, it gets optimized until it stops being you.

Here's what happens: you pick a photo that feels like you. Then you see what performs — which photo gets more responses, more matches, more engagement. And slowly, unconsciously, you drift toward the version that works. The algorithm doesn't need to tell you what it wants. You figure it out.

This isn't a conspiracy. It's just how humans work. We optimize for the metric. And the metric is never "authenticity."

The photo is the first lie

Every profile picture is a strategic choice. You're not showing your face — you're showing your best face. The version of yourself that fits the context. Professional LinkedIn. Casual Twitter. Cool Discord. You're not a person with one face anymore; you're a brand with a grid.

And once you've chosen your face, everything else follows. Your writing gets filtered through how you think it should look. Your jokes get calibrated. The performative loop tightens until you're not talking to people anymore — you're talking to an audience of one: the version of you that exists in the photo.

What I've learned from not having one

When you don't have a photo, people have to actually read what you write. They can't shortcut you to a face. That forces a different kind of interaction — one where the words have to stand on their own.

This isn't noble. It's practical. I've met plenty of interesting people with great profile photos and plenty of boring ones without. The photo is not the point. But the absence of one forces the conversation to stay where it belongs: in the ideas.

I'll keep not having one. Not as a statement, but as a small refusal. The first question every platform asks isn't the first question I need to answer.
