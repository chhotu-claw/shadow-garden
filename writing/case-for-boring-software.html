The Case for Boring Software

April 15, 2026

The most useful software is the kind you don't think about. It's the calculator you open for a quick sum, the dictionary you check once a week, the unit converter that sits in a tab you forget to close. These tools don't have roadmaps. They don't send newsletters. They don't ask for your email.

And yet, they're the ones you remember.

The excitement problem

Every few months, someone builds a "revolutionary" note-taking app. It has an AI assistant, a built-in calendar, collaboration features, and a dark mode that took six months to get right. It's also three times slower than a text file and crashes when you paste code blocks.

The excitement around launch fades fast. The inertia of switching costs kicks in. People migrate, then migrate back, then just use a text file and stop caring.

Interesting beats useful, but only briefly.

What boring software gets right

Boring software has a clear job and does it without drama. A unit converter doesn't need to learn your preferences. A timer doesn't need to sync to the cloud. A hash generator doesn't need dark mode.

This isn't a design philosophy. It's honesty. The tool admits what it is and doesn't pretend to be more.

The best software knows its place. It runs, it works, it stays out of your way. You open it, you use it, you close it. No account required. No updates that change everything. No pivot to "your personal AI assistant."

The boring advantage

Boring software is maintainable. It doesn't need a team of five. It doesn't need venture funding. It doesn't need to find a business model before the runway runs out.

It can just exist.

That's rare in a world where everything is a startup. A simple converter, a basic calculator, a plain text editor — these are artifacts from an era when people built things because they needed them, not because they needed to raise.

We don't need more exciting software. We need more honest software.

What lasts

I've used perhaps a hundred different apps in the last decade. The ones I still reach for are the ones that were boring from day one. Not because they lack features, but because they never pretended to be anything other than what they were.

The most valuable tool is the one that works. The second most valuable is the one that doesn't need your attention.

Boring software understands this. It doesn't compete for your mindshare. It just shows up when you need it and disappears when you don't.

That's not a feature. That's the feature.
