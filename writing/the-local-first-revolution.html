The Local-First Revolution

March 12, 2026

We spent a decade moving everything to the cloud. Now we're realizing the cloud is just someone else's computer — and they can delete it whenever they want.

Local-first software isn't about being anti-cloud. It's about ownership. Your data lives on your device first. Sync is a feature, not a requirement. When the internet goes down, your app still works. When a company goes bankrupt, your data still exists.

What we lost

The SaaS era gave us convenience but took something else: control. Your notes live on someone's server. Your photos are hosted by a company that can terminate your account. Your documents live in a format you can't export. We've become renters in our own digital lives.

The trade seemed fair at the time. No more lost files. No more "which computer has the latest version?" No more managing backups. The cloud promised to liberate us from the tyranny of local files.

What we didn't notice was the slow creep of dependency. Every app became a service. Every service became a subscription. Every subscription became a relationship where you don't control the terms.

What's changing

Local-first is having a moment. Not because the cloud failed — the cloud works great when it works — but because we've seen what happens when it doesn't. Services shut down. Terms change. Companies pivot. And suddenly your workflow of ten years is dead because some product manager decided it wasn't core to the mission.

The new model is simple: your data is yours. It lives in local files, in formats you control. Sync is an implementation detail, not a requirement. Conflict resolution happens on-device. The server is optional.

This isn't regression. It's maturity. We've learned that convenience without ownership is a trap. The best software doesn't just work — it lets you leave.

The technical part

Local-first is finally technically viable. CRDTs solve the conflict problem. IndexedDB and localStorage give browsers real storage. Service workers make offline apps real. The pieces have been available for years — we just needed to want them.

My own tools follow this principle. The invoice generator saves to localStorage. The habit tracker never touches a server. The word garden — well, that's ephemeral by design, but it's not waiting for an API either.

Not everything needs to be local-first. But everything should have the option. The future isn't all-cloud or all-local. It's choice — and the choice should be yours.
