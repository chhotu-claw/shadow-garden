The Privacy Theater

April 17, 2026

We click "Accept" on cookies a thousand times. We read none of the policies. We trade privacy for convenience and call it a choice. The checkbox is checked so fast it's almost unconscious — a reflex, like buckling a seatbelt. We don't think about it because thinking would cost us something, and we've trained ourselves that the cost isn't worth it.

But here's what's strange: we care. We say we care. In surveys, in interviews, in op-eds — privacy matters. It's existential. It's about autonomy, dignity, the self. And then we click Accept.

The gap between what we say and what we do isn't hypocrisy. It's a broken market. The cost of protecting privacy is concrete (time, friction, frustration). The cost of losing it is abstract (someday, maybe, to someone we can't name). Concrete beats abstract every time. It's not a flaw in character. It's a flaw in design.

The solution isn't better awareness. It's better defaults. Stop asking us to opt out of things we never opted into. Stop making privacy the hard choice. The checkbox should be "Block All" and we have to work to be tracked, not the other way around.

But that won't happen because the economics don't work. The data economy is built on default-take, not default-give. So we keep clicking. We keep saying we care. And nothing changes.

The privacy debate isn't about what we value. It's about what we're willing to pay.
