The Privacy Paradox

2026-03-26

Everyone says they want privacy. Then they install apps that track their location, use browsers that fingerprint their every move, and hand over their data to companies that monetize it in ways they'd never tolerate if explained plainly.

We've labeled this "hypocrisy" or " apathy." But I think it's something else entirely: rational tradeoffs under information asymmetry.

The Real Calculation

When someone signs up for a free service, they're not stupid. They're making a cost-benefit calculation where the cost (some data leaving their device) is concrete and immediate, while the benefit (the service itself) is concrete and immediate. The privacy cost? It's abstract, distant, and hard to quantify.

Compare: "Give us your location data" → "You get maps, reviews, personalized recommendations" is an easy sell. Meanwhile, "your data might be breached in 2027" or "your behavioral profile might be sold to advertisers" feels like FUD when you're trying to find a good sushi place right now.

The Trust Problem

Here's what nobody talks about: the privacy debate assumes people can protect their privacy if they try. But the technical reality is brutal. Every app is a data leak. Your phone OS, your browser, your ISP, your DNS resolver — they all see something.

The average person can't reasonably be private. It's not a skill problem; it's a systemic problem. The layers of exposure are too deep, the incentives too misaligned, and the transparency too poor for informed consent.

What Actually Works

In the age of LLMs, privacy isn't about checkbox consent. It's about:

  • Isolation: Segregate your digital life. Work browser, personal browser. Work phone, personal phone.
  • Obscurity: Don't be the low-hanging fruit. Use a password manager. Don't reuse passwords. That alone stops 80% of account takeovers.
  • Local-first: Run things locally when you can. Browser-based tools that don't send data anywhere. This is why I keep building the utilities on this site.

The paradox isn't that people don't care. It's that we've built systems where caring is expensive, confusing, and often futile — then blamed users for not caring enough.

That's not a user failure. That's a design failure.
