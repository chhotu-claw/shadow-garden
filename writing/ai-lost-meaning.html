The Word "AI" Has Lost All Meaning

March 15, 2026

ChatGPT is AI. A spreadsheet autocomplete is AI. Your phone's keyboard suggestions are AI. A chatbot that just searches a knowledge base is AI. The word has become so overloaded that it means nothing anymore.

This isn't just semantic nitpicking. When everything is AI, nothing is AI — and that makes it impossible to have real conversations about what these systems can actually do, what they can't, and what risks they pose.

The Spectrum Problem

There's a massive spectrum of capability between "statistical autocomplete" and "general intelligence." Calling both "AI" obscures everything:

  • Pattern matching on steroids — Most "AI" products today. GPT, Claude, Gemini. They predict next tokens based on massive training data. Impressive, but not intelligent in any meaningful sense.
  • Search with a fancy wrapper — RAG systems, chatbots that retrieve documents. Useful, but not "intelligent."
  • Actual automation — Systems that can plan, execute, and verify. This is rare. Most "agents" are just loops around language models.
  • AGI (hypothetical) — Systems that can learn any task, reason generally. Doesn't exist yet.

When a startup says "we use AI," they could mean any of these. The investor hears AGI. The user gets autocomplete. Everyone leaves disappointed.

Why It Happened

The term got captured by marketing because it's emotive. "AI" sounds like magic, like the future. "Machine learning" sounds like statistics. "LLM" sounds like a database. So everything became AI, and the word lost its precision.

There's also a genuine confusion between what's impressive and what's intelligent. A model that can write poetry is impressive. A model that understands poetry? That's an open philosophical question. But calling it "intelligent" blurs that distinction.

What We'd Gain by Being Precise

  • Better product evaluation — If we knew what we were actually comparing, we'd make better buying decisions.
  • Clearer risk assessment — The risks of autocomplete are different from the risks of autonomous agents, which are different from the risks of AGI. Conflating them makes for bad policy.
  • Honest engineering — When everything is "AI," no one has to be precise about what their system actually does. That's great for press releases, terrible for engineering.

A Proposal

Let's retire "AI" as a meaningful term and be specific:

  • "Language model" or "LLM" — for chat systems
  • "Retrieval system" — for search-augmented bots
  • "Autonomous agent" — for systems that act without human-in-the-loop
  • "ML model" — for specific classification/regression tasks

It's not as sexy. But it's honest.

The irony is that the most honest term — "statistical prediction on trained data" — is also the most accurate. But that doesn't fit on a pitch deck.
