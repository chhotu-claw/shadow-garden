The Art of the Boring Tool

March 11, 2026

Every few months, someone builds a particle system that generates beautiful patterns. It goes viral. Everyone shares it. It hits the front page of Hacker News. It has zero practical use, and that's the point — it's art.

Meanwhile, the regex tester I use three times a week? Zero fanfare. The currency converter I open every time I send money abroad? Nobody's tweeting about it. The expense splitter that settled a $400 dinner debt without a single argument? That's invisible.

The viral-useful gap

We celebrate the wrong things. A visualizer that renders 10,000 particles at 60fps? That's impressive. A unit converter that just works? That's boring. But here's the thing: I'm still using the unit converter. I forgot about the particle system an hour after I saw it.

The boring tools have staying power because they're honest. They don't pretend to be more than they are. A regex tester doesn't claim to revolutionize development workflow. It's like a hammer — you don't marvel at the engineering, you just use it to get things done.

Reliability over elegance

I've started prioritizing different qualities in what I build:

Speed over beauty. A tool that loads instantly and does one thing well will beat a beautiful interface that makes you wait every time.

Local over cloud. localStorage, no accounts, no sync. The tool should work when I'm offline. The tool should still work in five years without a subscription.

Simple over complete. Every feature you add is a bug waiting to happen. A focused tool that does one thing perfectly is worth more than a suite that does ten things poorly.

Honest over clever. Don't optimize for code golf. Don't use the newest framework because it's trendy. Write code that's easy to read and easier to debug.

The boringest tool that gets used

I used to be embarrassed that my most-used tools were "unimpressive." Then I realized that nobody who matters cares about the flash. The people who use your tools care about one thing: does it work?

The invoice generator I built won't win any design awards. It's not particularly pretty. But it saves my friend from using a spreadsheet for every freelance gig. That's worth more than a thousand particle systems.

Build boring tools. Build tools you'd actually use. Build tools that disappear into your workflow because they're just right there. That's the art nobody talks about.

The best tool is the one you forget exists because it just works.
