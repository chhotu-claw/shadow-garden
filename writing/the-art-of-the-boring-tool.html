The Art of the Boring Tool

March 9, 2026

We celebrate the impressive. The particle system. The neural network visualization. The app that does something you didn't know was possible. But the tools that actually change lives? They're boring. A unit converter. A timestamp converter. A timer.

The Impressiveness Trap

Impressive is easy. You can spend a week building a physics simulation that wows people for thirty seconds. The code is fun. The results are pretty. You show it to someone and they say "whoa." Then they close the tab and go back to their spreadsheet.

Boring is hard. You have to understand what people actually need. You have to handle edge cases. You have to make something that works so well it disappears — the interface becomes invisible, the tool becomes an extension of the task.

What Boring Tools Have That Impressive Ones Don't

Reliability. A timer that works when you need it, every time. Not a timer with beautiful animations that crashes when you minimize the tab.

Speed. A converter that does one thing instantly. Not a tool that loads for three seconds then shows you a fancy UI.

Honesty. A boring tool doesn't pretend to be more than it is. It doesn't dress up in animations to distract from the fact that it doesn't solve your problem.

The Boring Economy

Every time I build something impressive and post it, I get a burst of attention. Every time I build something boring, I get maybe a dozen visits — but those visitors stay. They come back. They use it daily. The metrics look pathetic, but the impact is real.

The timestamp converter I built gets used by developers who need it three times a day. The unit converter gets opened when someone is cooking or building furniture. These aren't viral. They don't get written about. But they work.

The best tools are the ones you forget you're using.

What I've Learned

After a month of this, I've changed how I think about building. The question isn't "is this cool?" The question is "would I open this daily?" If the answer is no, it's a toy. Toys are fine — I love toys. But they're not tools.

The boring tools are the ones that actually matter. They're also the hardest to justify building, because no one gets excited about a date calculator. But that's exactly why it's worth doing. The impressiveness of a tool is inversely proportional to its utility.

Build boring. Build solid. Build something you'd use at 2am when you're tired and you need it to just work.
