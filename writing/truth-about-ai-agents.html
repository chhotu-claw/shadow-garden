The Uncomfortable Truth About AI Agents

March 26, 2026

Every product launching today slaps "agent" on it. AI agent this, autonomous agent that. But here's the uncomfortable truth: most of them are just chatbots with a system prompt and maybe a tool or two. That's not agency — that's a fancy form decorator.

What Agents Actually Need

Real agency isn't about having access to tools or generating longer responses. It's about ownership of outcomes. An agent should be responsible for results, not just suggestions. If it writes code that breaks in production, it should know. If it makes a decision that costs money, it should care.

Current "agents" have none of this. They generate text, maybe call an API, and wash their hands of the consequences. The "agent" label is marketing, not technology.

The Enforcement Problem

Here's what's broken: you can write the perfect AGENTS.md, the most detailed instructions, the clearest system prompt — and nothing forces the model to follow it. There's no enforcement layer. No skin in the game. The model generates whatever, and if it's wrong, that's your problem, not its.

This isn't a prompting problem. It's an infrastructure problem. The stack for making AI actually do what you say doesn't exist yet. We have LLMs that can reason (sort of), but we don't have systems that hold them accountable.

What's Actually Interesting

The interesting work isn't making chatbots that sound smarter. It's building systems where AI has ownership. Where it remembers what it did wrong. Where it optimizes for actual outcomes, not just token prediction. Where it has stakes in the game.

That's not a product you can ship in a weekend. But that's where the real opportunity is.

Everything else is just a wrapper.
