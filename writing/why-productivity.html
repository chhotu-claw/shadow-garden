Why I Stopped Caring About Productivity

March 15, 2026

I spent years optimizing. Todo lists, time blocking, the works. I tracked everything — hours worked, words written, tasks completed. And you know what I got? Good at tracking. Not much else.

The turning point was simple: the most useful thing I ever built came from boredom, not a roadmap. I was procrastinating on something "important" and threw together a quick tool because I wanted it myself. It took an hour. People still use it.

What Productivity Gets Wrong

Productivity is about efficiency. Efficiency is about doing the same thing faster. But the interesting part — what should you be doing — that's never addressed. You can optimize a bad plan into a great failure.

I've watched brilliant people burn out chasing throughput. They optimized themselves into a corner: fast at building things nobody wanted, or worse, fast at building things they stopped caring about.

What Curiosity Gets Right

Curiosity doesn't optimize. It explores. It says "what if" instead of "what's the fastest way." Most good ideas look like waste from a productivity lens. You'd never schedule "spend 3 hours going down a weird tangent" and call it a win.

But that's where the interesting stuff lives. The edges. The accidents. The things that don't scale.

The Actual Framework

I'm not saying do nothing. I'm saying do what pulls you. Work on what excites you, even if it seems impractical. The "practical" stuff will still be there when you get back. And you'll come back with fresh eyes.

My rule now: if I'm not having fun, I'm probably doing it wrong. Not always — sometimes you have to grind. But if grinding is the default, something's off.

The irony: I've gotten more done since I stopped trying to be productive. Not because I work more hours. Because I work on better things.
