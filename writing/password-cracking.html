How Password Cracking Actually Works

March 18, 2026

Hollywood would have you believe password cracking is about trying every possible combination, character by character. That's technically true but practically useless — a 12-character alphanumeric password has 72 quadrillion possibilities. At 10 billion guesses per second (modern GPU cluster), that's still 83 days. Hollywood doesn't do nuance.

The Real Attack Vectors

1. Dictionary Attacks
Most people don't use random strings. They use "password123", "iloveyou", "qwerty". Attackers start with massive wordlists — billions of commonly used passwords,Compilations from real breaches, proper nouns, names, and variations with l33t speak (p@ssw0rd). If your password is in the top 10,000, it takes milliseconds.

2. Rainbow Tables
When databases get breached, they store password hashes — not plain text. A rainbow table is a pre-computed lookup table that reverses cryptographic hashes. You give me the hash, I give you the password. The countermeasure? Salting (adding random data before hashing) makes rainbow tables useless for each unique salt.

3. GPU Hash Cracking
Modern GPUs have thousands of cores. A rig with 8 RTX 4090s can do roughly 300 billion MD5 hashes per second. That's not a typo. This is why MD5 and SHA-1 are obsolete — they were designed for speed, not security. Good algorithms (bcrypt, Argon2, scrypt) are intentionally slow and memory-hard.

4. Credential Stuffing
People reuse passwords. Attackers take email:password pairs from one breach and try them everywhere else. This is why password reuse is the silent killer. One site gets pwned, your entire digital life unravels.

What Actually Protects You

Length over complexity. "correct horse battery staple" (28 chars) is exponentially harder to crack than "Tr0ub4dor&3" (11 chars) because entropy scales exponentially with length but linearly with complexity.

Use a password manager. I'm not paid to say this. Random 20-character passwords stored in a manager are the only realistic way to have unique passwords everywhere. The master password should be memorable but long — a passphrase only you know.

Enable 2FA everywhere. Especially email, banking, and anything with your identity. Authenticator apps beat SMS (SIM swapping is real). Hardware keys (YubiKey, Google Titan) are the endgame.

Check if you've been pwned. Have I Been Pwned has recorded over 600 million breached accounts. Check your email. If you're there, change that password immediately — and anywhere else you reused it.

The Uncomfortable Truth

Security is a tradeoff. The "best" password is useless if it's 50 characters of random characters you can't remember. The "best" 2FA is useless if you lose your phone and can't recover your account. Find the balance that lets you sleep at night.

The biggest threat isn't sophisticated cracking — it's human nature. We're lazy, we reuse passwords, we click links we shouldn't. Every security measure is fighting that tendency.

"Passwords are like underwear: don't let people see it, change it often, and don't share it with strangers."

Pretty good advice, actually.
