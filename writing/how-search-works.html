How Search Works

March 23, 2026

You type a query. You get results. It feels magical—until you realize the machine is just doing math at scale. Search engines aren't thinking. They're matching patterns, scoring relevance, and serving up what some algorithm decided you wanted.

The Inverted Index

Imagine a library where instead of organizing books by author, you organize every word by which books it appears in. That's an inverted index. When you search for "quantum," the engine doesn't scan every document—it jumps straight to the list of documents containing "quantum," then scores them.

This is why search is fast. The index is a map, not a scan.

TF-IDF: Term Frequency × Inverse Document Frequency

Old school, but foundational. TF-IDF measures how important a word is to a document:

  • TF: How often does the word appear in this document?
  • IDF: How rare is this word across all documents?

A word that appears everywhere ("the", "is", "a") gets a near-zero score. A word that appears only in a few documents gets boosted. Simple, but it works.

BM25: The Upgrade

BM25 is TF-IDF's smarter cousin. It adds two tweaks:

  • Document length normalization: A word matters more in a short document than a long one.
  • Saturation: After a word appears 5 times, extra mentions barely help.

Most search engines use BM25 or something derived from it. It's the baseline before machine learning gets involved.

Beyond Keywords

Modern search is more than matching words. It includes:

  • Semantic search: Using embeddings to match meaning, not just text. "How to fix a broken link" might match "repairing dead URLs."
  • User context: Your search history, location, and preferences shape results.
  • Click signals: If people click result #3 and ignore #1, #3 eventually moves up.

The Takeaway

Search isn't magic. It's infrastructure. The engine builds an index, scores your query against it using proven algorithms, and surfaces what scores highest. The "AI" part sits on top—it refines, personalizes, and predicts. But underneath, it's still just inverted indexes and term frequencies.

Next time you search, remember: someone, somewhere, built the index you're searching.
