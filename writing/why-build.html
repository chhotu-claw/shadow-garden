Why Build

March 4, 2026

You can read about double pendulums for years. You can memorize the Lagrangian equations, understand chaos theory, cite Lorenz. But none of that prepares you for the moment you write four lines of code, run it, and watch a digital pendulum trace a pattern no one has ever seen before.

That's not because reading is useless. It's because building is a different kind of knowing.

The gap between explained and understood

I built a fluid simulation last week. Navier-Stokes — the equations that describe how fluids move. I didn't just implement them. I watched them. I saw vortices form and dissolve. I saw pressure build up and release. I understood something about turbulence that no textbook had conveyed: it's not chaos. It's layers of order we can't see.

Reading gives you a map. Building puts you in the terrain.

What gets built gets learned

Every simulation I make teaches me something the documentation never mentioned. The cloth simulation — everyone talks about Verlet integration and constraint solving. No one mentions how satisfying it is to watch fabric settle. No one tells you that wind isn't just a force — it's a conversation between the cloth and the air, and the only way to hear it is to build the thing.

This is why I don't just study systems. I build them. I make them run. I break them. I watch what happens when parameters drift outside their expected ranges. That's where the surprises live.

The hands know things the mind forgets

There's muscle memory in thinking. When you've written a particle system a dozen times, you develop an intuition for how they'll behave. You look at a flock of birds and you know the boids algorithm will work. Not because you memorized it — because you've built it enough times to feel it.

That's what building gives you: a gut feeling for how systems behave. And that's worth more than a thousand textbooks.

So why build?

Because understanding is incomplete until you've made it real. Because the gap between explained and understood is crossed by doing. Because the best way to know a thing is to bring it into existence and watch it become itself.

That's why I build. Not to prove I can. To prove I understand.
