The Paradox of Choice

March 13, 2026

We used to have three toothpaste options. Now we have thirty. We used to have five TV channels. Now we have five hundred. We used to date people in our town. Now we swipe through thousands.

And we're less happy.

The Paradox

Barry Schwartz wrote about this in 2004 — The Paradox of Choice. More options should mean more freedom, more satisfaction. But empirically, it doesn't work that way. At some point, more choices become a burden instead of a gift.

There are three reasons why:

1. The cost of evaluation goes up. With three options, you can quickly compare. With thirty, you can't. You either spend hours researching (exhausting), pick arbitrarily (regrettable), or do nothing (paralysis).

2. Expectations rise. With few options, "good enough" is easy. With many, you think: "There might be something better." You hold out. You second-guess. Even when you choose, you wonder.

3. Responsibility shifts inward. When there were three toothpastes and you didn't like yours, you could blame the limited choice. Now? You chose this. You have no one to blame but yourself. And that bites.

The Modern Disease

We live in the age of infinite choice. Every interface is designed to show you more. More products, more content, more connections, more ways to optimize your life. And we've internalized the idea that more is better.

But the data says otherwise. Studies show that jam samplers with 6 options sell more than samplers with 24. 401(k) participants with more fund options save less. Dating apps — need I say more.

The problem isn't having options. The problem is thinking more options = more happiness. They correlate negatively past a certain threshold.

What Works

The solution isn't to eliminate choice — that's not realistic, and some choice is genuinely valuable. The solution is to design your life around decision fatigue.

Defaults matter. Pick good defaults and stick to them. Your operating system, your toothpaste, your morning routine. Automate the small stuff so your decision energy goes where it matters.

Satisficing over optimizing. Pick "good enough" and commit. The perfect option rarely exists, and even if it did, you'd never find it in finite time.

Curate your inputs. You can't control the infinite internet, but you can control what you let in. Fewer feeds, fewer subscriptions, fewer "just in case" services. Curation is the skill of the decade.

Embrace constraints. The best creative work happens under constraints. The best decisions happen with less information. Sometimes "no" to options is "yes" to peace of mind.

The Quiet Life

There's a reason people move to small towns, delete social media, simplify their wardrobes. Not because they can't handle complexity, but because they've learned: less choice, more life.

The paradox isn't that we have too few options. It's that we've confused having more with living better. The good life isn't the life with the most options. It's the life where you can actually choose.
