Why I Don't Trust Benchmarks

2026-03-26

Every week there's a new benchmark. "Model X beats model Y on benchmark Z." And every week, I scroll past it because I've learned something: benchmarks measure what machines do, not what humans care about.

The Benchmark Illusion

A benchmark is a controlled test with clear inputs and measurable outputs. That's great for comparing two systems on the same task. But the moment you read "Model X is 15% better than Y," you're already one step behind.

Because the question isn't "is it better on this specific task?" It's "does this matter for the thing I actually want to do?"

LLMs that ace trivia benchmarks still hallucinate. Models that score high on math benchmarks still mess up basic arithmetic in production. The benchmark isn't lying — it's just not telling you what you think it's telling you.

The Selection Problem

Who picks the benchmark? Who decides what's worth measuring? The people building the model get to choose the test, and they optimize for the test. It's not cheating exactly — it's just that the incentive structure naturally drifts toward making the numbers look good.

This is why I trust what breaks in production more than what passes in a benchmark. Production is adversarial. Benchmarks are curated.

What Actually Matters

Here's what I care about in practice:

  • Consistency: Does it produce the same output for the same input, or does it vary wildly?
  • Failure modes: When it fails, how badly? Is it a graceful degradation or a confidence hallucination?
  • Latency: A 1% accuracy boost doesn't matter if it takes 10x longer.

None of these show up in a single benchmark number. But they show up every day if you're actually using the thing.

The Honest Take

Benchmarks are useful for one thing: comparing two systems on a specific, well-defined task. They're useless for answering "is this model better, period?"

That question doesn't have a number. It has a feeling — based on actually using the thing, over time, on the messy tasks that matter.

I'd rather use a tool I trust than a tool that scores higher on something arbitrary. And I trust the one that doesn't break when I need it to work.
