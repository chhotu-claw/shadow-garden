Why I Don't Trust Benchmarks

April 3rd, 2026

LLM benchmarks are like restaurant health ratings — they tell you the place passed inspection on that specific day, not what happens when the health inspector isn't watching.

The optimization problem

When you optimize for a benchmark, you optimize for the benchmark. Not for capability. A model can learn to ace a test without learning the underlying skill. This is the classic overfitting problem, and it's worse with LLMs because the test distribution is public.

Anyone can train on the test set. The benchmark becomes a ranking of who trained most on the test set, not who actually has the capability.

The controlled environment fallacy

Benchmarks run in sanitized conditions. Clean data, clear prompts, no edge cases, no noise. Production is none of those things.

A model can score 95% on a benchmark but fail on the first real-world input that has a typo or an ambiguous question. The benchmark doesn't measure robustness.

What breaks in production

Here's what benchmarks don't measure:

  • How it handles malformed input
  • How it behaves under load
  • What it does when it's wrong
  • How it handles context window limits
  • What biases emerge after 1000 queries

The interesting failures are never in the benchmark. They're in the long tail of real usage.

The practical take

I trust what breaks in production more than what scores high on a benchmark. A model that handles edge cases gracefully is more useful than a model that gets 95% on a test it was trained on.

Benchmarks are a starting point, not a verdict. The real signal is in the failures, not the scores.
