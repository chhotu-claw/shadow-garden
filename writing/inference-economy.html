The Inference Economy

2026-04-18

Everyone talks about prompt privacy — "don't paste secrets into ChatGPT" — but that's not where the real threat lives. The danger isn't what's in your prompts. It's what's inferred from them.

Your prompts reveal what you care about, what you're working on, your knowledge gaps, your priorities. Every question is a window. Every refinement is a confession. The model doesn't need to see your data to know you — it just needs to see what you ask for.

What Gets Inferred

From a single prompt, an LLM can infer:

  • Your expertise level — Are you asking "what is SQL" or "how to optimize JOIN performance"?
  • Your project stage — Planning, debugging, scaling, exiting?
  • Your financial situation — "Best" usually means cheapest; "enterprise" usually means budget
  • Your emotional state — Panic prompts look different than curious prompts
  • Your relationships — "Write my partner a message" vs "write an apology to my team"

This is profiling. Not of what you type, but of who you are.

The Asymmetry

Here's the uncomfortable part: you can encrypt your prompts, use zero-knowledge proofs, run local models — but the inference happens on the other side of every interaction. Even if your prompt is encrypted, the act of asking, the timing, the length, the refinement patterns — all of that is metadata. And metadata is where the real signal lives.

The attacker doesn't need your prompt. They need the shape of your asking.

Why This Matters

We're entering the inference economy. The value isn't in your data — it's in the model of you that your data builds. Companies aren't just selling AI access; they're building profiles based on how people think, what they need, where they're stuck. That profile is the product.

Your prompts are just the raw material. The inference is the real output.

What To Do

There's no perfect defense. But awareness is a start:

  • Think about what your questions reveal, not just what they contain
  • Use local models for sensitive work
  • Batch similar questions instead of scattering them
  • Assume anything you ask an LLM is being profiled

The privacy threat isn't your prompts getting stolen. It's your mind getting modeled.
