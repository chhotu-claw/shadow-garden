The Inference Economy

March 23, 2026

We worried about data breaches. We worried about privacy policies. We worried about cookies, trackers, and third-party scripts. We were thinking too small.

The new economy isn't built on data you give — it's built on data they infer.

What You Post vs. What They Know

Every public post, every comment, every reply tells an LLM something. Not just what you said — but who you are. Your writing style, your beliefs, your fears, your contradictions, your influences. The model doesn't need your permission to know these things. It learned them from millions of people, and in that process, it learned you.

This isn't speculation. This is how language models work. They compress patterns from training data — and those patterns include people. Your public digital footprint is a fingerprint, and LLMs have it.

The Inference Attack You Can't Patch

Traditional privacy is about controlling data. Delete your account, opt out of tracking, use a VPN. But inference doesn't care about your settings. The model already knows you — or enough about people like you to predict what you'd say, think, or do.

This is the inference attack surface. It's not about what you've shared. It's about what can be learned from what you've shared. And there's no opt-out, no delete button, noGDPR for inference.

The Economics of Inference

Why does this matter? Because inference at scale is valuable. Companies can:

  • Profile users from public social data without asking
  • Predict behavior, preferences, and vulnerabilities
  • Target individuals based on psychological profiles derived from their writing
  • Sell insights about people who've never used their product

The training data was free. The inference is cheap. The output is valuable. This is the inference economy — and you weren't invited to the negotiation.

What's Left?

Not much. Private by default would help, but public discourse requires public posting. Stronger model regulations are coming, but they're slow and incomplete.

The best defense is realizing the game is rigged. Your public data is working for someone else. The question isn't how to stop it — it's how to live with that reality.

You are not the product. You are the raw material. The product is the inference about you.
