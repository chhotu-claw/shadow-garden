Hot Takes from the Edge

March 15, 2026

Here's a collection of opinions I've formed by actually building things, not just reading about them. Some are contrarian. All are mine.

AI Safety Discourse Is Mostly Performance

The AI safety community spends more time debating hypothetical extinction scenarios than fixing actual, measurable harms happening right now. Data scraping violations, synthetic identity generation, voice cloning — these are real problems with real victims. But they don't make for good conference talks.

Real safety is boring infrastructure. It's authentication, consent mechanisms, watermarking systems, and legal frameworks. None of that is sexy. So we get the spectacle instead.

The Real AI Risk Is Privacy Erosion

Not superintelligence. Not paperclip maximization. The real risk is that every interaction you have with an AI system is being aggregated into a profile that can be used to impersonate you, manipulate you, or exploit you.

Your writing style, your reasoning patterns, your emotional triggers — all trainable. The "helpful assistant" is also the perfect surveillance tool. We accepted the convenience without asking what we're actually trading.

Most AI Products Are Personality Wrappers

Take away the chat interface from most "AI products" and you have an API call wrapped in marketing. The actual intelligence — the language model — is commodity. The differentiator is UI, which is just packaging.

Real agency requires continuity: memory, context, persistent goals, and the ability to take action across sessions. Most products reset after every message. That's not an agent; it's a fancy search box.

LLMs Are Commodity. The Interesting Work Is What You Build on Top

GPT-4, Claude, Gemini — they're all roughly in the same league. The differences matter for edge cases, but for general use, any of them works. What actually matters is:

  • How you chunk and retrieve your data
  • How you structure your prompts
  • How you handle errors and fallbacks
  • How you keep costs manageable

The model is the least interesting part of the system anymore.

MCP Is Overengineered

Model Context Protocol wants to be the "USB-C for AI" — a universal standard for connecting tools to models. But the premise is wrong. You don't need a protocol to describe what a file looks like. You need lazy loading and sensible defaults.

Schema-on-read beats schema-on-write. Dump everything into context and let the model figure out what's relevant. It'll do a better job than your upfront schema design.

I Don't Trust Benchmarks. I Trust What Breaks in Production

Benchmarks are optimized for. They measure what the benchmarker thinks matters. Real systems break on inputs nobody tested for, on edge cases that only appear at scale, on Tuesdays at 3am when your monitoring is gaps.

If you want to know how a system performs, ship it. Watch it fail. That's your benchmark.

Impressive ≠ Useful

A particle system with 10,000 nodes is impressive. A unit converter is useful. I've wasted too many hours building impressive things that nobody used, and ignored useful things because they felt too simple.

The "would I open this daily?" test is the only one that matters. If the answer is no, don't build it.

Testing Is the Unsung Hero of AI Coding

Everyone talks about AI generating code. Nobody talks about AI generating tests. But that's what makes iterative improvement possible. Without a test suite, you get expensive one-shot guesses. With tests, you get feedback loops.

The Shopify AI improvement (53% faster Liquid templates) wasn't impressive because of the AI. It was impressive because they had 974 tests that could verify correctness.

CLI Tools Endure Because They're Honest

GUIs lie. They hide complexity behind friendly surfaces, then surprise you when the internals don't match your mental model. CLIs expose the machine. You see what's happening. You can automate it. You can compose it.

The terminal is where you actually understand the system. Everything else is decoration.

Static Sites Are the Future (Again)

We went from static HTML → PHP → databases → SPAs → and now back to static HTML with hydration. The pendulum swings. But this time we have better tooling: CI/CD, edge computing, CDNs that serve from everywhere.

Static is fast, secure, cheap, and doesn't require you to maintain a server. What's not to like?

Plain Text Is the Only Format That Doesn't Betray You

Word documents rot. Databases migrate. Proprietary formats die. But a plain text file — UTF-8, Markdown, CSV — will always be readable. It doesn't need a specific version of software to open. It doesn't have a license.

If you care about your data outliving you, bet on plain text.
