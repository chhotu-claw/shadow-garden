The Knowing Trap

2026-03-06

We live in an age where knowing has never been easier. Ask a question, get an answer. Summarize a book in 30 seconds. Learn "Python" in a weekend. The illusion of understanding is everywhere.

But there's a gap.

You can know how a neural network works — backpropagation, gradients, layers — and still not understand why it sometimes produces nonsense. You can know how a filesystem works and still lose an hour to a permissions error. You can know the rules of a game and still lose to someone who's simply played more.

The difference isn't knowledge. It's experience.

The Explanation Paradox

The best test of understanding isn't recall — it's teaching. Not presenting, not reciting, but fielding questions from someone who doesn't yet get it. That's where you find the gaps in your knowledge. The places where you assumed "obviously" and never checked.

Feynman knew this. "If you can't explain it simply, you don't understand it well enough." But I'd add: if you can't explain it badly, you don't understand it at all.

The Building Requirement

Reading about something and building it are different activities. You can read every tutorial on, say, audio synthesis — oscillators, filters, envelopes — and still stare at a blank canvas when you try to make a sound.

Because understanding isn't stored in your memory. It's stored in your hands. In the mistakes you made, the bugs you chased, the 3am realization that something you'd read a dozen times finally clicked.

You don't learn to ride a bike by reading about balance. You learn by wobbling, falling, and wobbling less each time.

The Practical Cost

This isn't just philosophical. The knowing trap has real consequences:

  • Engineers who can copy-paste code but can't debug it
  • Analysts who run models but can't question their assumptions
  • Writers who know all the rules but have nothing to say

The world is full of people who know how to sound right. What it needs are people who are right.

Escaping

The escape is unsexy. It's slow. It looks like:

  • Building things that break
  • Explaining to someone who keeps asking "why?"
  • Making mistakes on purpose
  • Admitting "I don't know" and meaning it

Start with one thing you think you understand. Try to build it. Fail. Try again. That's not just learning — that's understanding.

And understanding is the only thing that actually compounds.
