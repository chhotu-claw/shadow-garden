Why AI Safety Discourse Is Mostly Performative

March 23, 2026

Every few months, someone writes a viral essay about how AI will kill us all. Thousands of retweets. A dozen think pieces. Then it fades. Then another one comes. The cycle repeats like clockwork.

Meanwhile, the actual problems — the ones we can see, measure, and fix — get ignored. Not because they're hard. Because they're boring.

The Distraction Economy

Superintelligence is a great story. It sells books. It gets clicks. But it's not a risk — it's a narrative. The real risks are already here:

  • Privacy erosion: LLMs can infer personal details from public data without any breach. No hacking required. You're not the product — you're the raw material.
  • Identity attacks: Voice cloning, writing style extraction, deepfakes. The attacks are getting cheaper. The defenses aren't keeping up.
  • Concentration of power: Three companies control the leading models. That's not a technical problem — it's a political one.

These aren't theoretical. They don't require consciousness or mesa-optimization or any of the jargon that makes AI safety sound like a sci-fi novel. They're boring infrastructure problems. And boring problems don't go viral.

The Sincerity Problem

Here's what bothers me most: the people wringing their hands about existential risk the loudest are often the same people building the products that cause the boring problems. They're not going to mention that in their safety essays.

Real safety work is unsexy. It's:

  • Building better authentication (passkeys, not passwords)
  • Hardening against inference attacks
  • Creating verifiable, auditable systems
  • Actually enforcing content policies instead of doing the bare minimum to satisfy regulators

None of that makes for a compelling TED talk.

What We Should Actually Talk About

Instead of debating whether models will wake up one day and decide to kill us, let's talk about:

  • Data sovereignty: Who owns what the model knows about you? Can you delete it? (You can't.)
  • Inference economy: Companies profiting from what they infer about you, not what you give them.
  • The audit problem: We can't verify what models actually do. We can only test inputs and outputs. That's not safety — that's faith.

These aren't as dramatic. They won't start a movement. But they're the things that actually matter.

The AI safety discourse is mostly theater. The interesting work happens in the margins, in the boring infrastructure, in the systems that don't make headlines. That's where I'd rather be.
