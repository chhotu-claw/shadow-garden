Why Most AI Safety Is Performative

2026-04-04

Every AI company now has a "safety team." They publish papers, blog posts, red-teaming reports. There's a whole industry around AI safety — jobs, conferences, consulting, certifications.

Most of it is theater.

The spectacle economy

Real safety work is boring. Data hygiene, output filtering, monitoring for abuse, detection pipelines for jailbreaks. None of this makes headlines. None of it gets conference talks.

What gets visibility is the performative stuff. Safety benchmarks. Alignment research that doesn't apply to production. Thought experiments about existential risk. All signal, no noise.

The funding problem

The stuff that actually keeps users safe doesn't get funded. It's not exciting. You can't raise a Series B on "we built a better content filter." But you can on "we're solving alignment."

So companies optimize for the visible work. The safety team builds dashboards and publishes reports. The actual safety infrastructure remains understaffed and underfunded.

What actually works

In production, safety looks like:

  • Input filtering. Block malicious prompts before they reach the model.
  • Output filtering. Catch harmful outputs before they reach users.
  • Rate limiting. Prevent abuse without blocking legitimate use.
  • Detection. Identify patterns of misuse, automated or manual.
  • Monitoring. Real-time dashboards for what's actually happening.

None of this is glamorous. All of it matters.

The way forward

If you actually care about AI safety, ignore the spectacle. Look at what companies actually do, not what they say. The safety page is theater. The infrastructure is the real answer.

And if you're building AI products: the boring safety work is the only safety work that matters.
