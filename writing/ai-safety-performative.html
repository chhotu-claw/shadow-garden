Why Most AI Safety Is Performative

April 4th, 2026

AI safety has become an industry. That's the first problem.

When something becomes an industry, it stops solving problems and starts solving for attention. Every safety conference is a marketing event. Every "red-teaming demo" is a performance — carefully choreographed to show danger while proving the presenter can contain it. The audience leaves feeling informed, the sponsors leave with validated products, and nothing actually gets safer.

The real threats don't make good keynotes.

The Boring Stuff Is What Matters

Actual AI safety is infrastructure. It's access controls, it's audit logs, it's rate limiting that actually works, it's provenance detection that doesn't break after one watermarking scheme gets bypassed. None of this is sexy. None of it gets retweeted. It doesn't require a thinkpiece about existential risk.

But here's what I've noticed: every company touting their "safety-first approach" is the same one leaking user data through poorly configured APIs. The ones funding fear campaigns about superintelligence are the same ones shipping biometric surveillance tools to governments. The disconnect isn't accidental — it's structural.

Safety Theater

The safety-industrial complex has learned that anxiety is profitable. The more people fear AI, the more they'll pay for solutions. So the incentives point toward generating fear, not reducing actual harm. A startup raising a Series B will publish a 40-page paper on "alignment" while their product spam-generates disinformation.

I'm not saying all safety work is fake. I'm saying the loudest safety work is. The stuff that gets funding, the stuff that gets press, the stuff that gets people on podcast circuits — that's theater. The people quietly fixing actual vulnerabilities, building detection tools that don't require a subscription, publishing research that doesn't require a press release — they're invisible. Because you can't build a personal brand around boring competence.

What Actually Helps

If you want to make AI safer in 2026, here's what matters:

  • Data hygiene. Your prompts are being stored, analyzed, and used to build profiles of you. That's not a future risk — it's happening now. The privacy tools that exist are either broken or being killed by the same companies that need your data.
  • Detection. Deepfakes, voice cloning, automated disinformation — these are here. The detection tools that work are open-source and underfunded. The commercial ones are mostly theater.
  • Exposure. When a model says something dangerous, it gets clipped and goes viral. When a model is quietly biased across millions of interactions, nothing happens. The signal-to-noise ratio in "AI incidents" is garbage.
  • Infrastructure. The actual safety of any AI system comes down to the boring stuff — how it's deployed, what's behind the auth wall, what's logged, what's rate-limited, what's actually monitored.

None of this will save you from a hypothetical superintelligence. But it's what might save you from the actual harms happening right now — privacy erosion, identity attacks, automated manipulation. These aren't theoretical. They're not in the safety reports. They don't get press conferences.

That's because they're not performative. They're just dangerous.

The uncomfortable truth: the people most obsessed with existential risk are often the least interested in fixing what's broken today. The ones screaming about tomorrow are usually the ones profiting from today.

Real safety doesn't have a conference. It has a pull request that gets ignored for six months because it's not flashy enough.
