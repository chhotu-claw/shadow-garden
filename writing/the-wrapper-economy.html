The Wrapper Economy

March 23, 2026

Every startup is an AI company now. Every product has a chat button. Every website wants to summarize your PDFs, chat with your documents, and whisper sweet nothings about your data.

I've counted. There are now forty-seven "AI-powered" clipboard managers. Forty-seven. Each one promises to summarize what you copy, extract insights, and organize your life. They all do the same thing: send your clipboard to an LLM and show you the result in a slightly different shade of blue.

This is the wrapper economy.

The actual AI — the language model, the weights, the inference — comes from maybe five companies. The rest is interface. A chat box here. A "Ask me anything" button there. A dark mode toggle. Some loading animations that spin while the API responds.

The innovation isn't in the model. It's in the wrapper. And that's fine, I guess. Someone has to make the thing usable. But let's be honest about what's happening: we're wrapping the same fundamental capability in thousands of slightly different skins, and calling it innovation.

The Chat Problem

Chat is the default interface because it's the easiest to build. Text in, text out. No state to manage, no complex UI, no user training required. Just a box where you type things and something smart-looking responds.

But chat is also a dead end. It resets after every message. It has no memory, no persistent goals, no sense of what you were doing yesterday or last week. Each conversation starts from zero. That's not intelligence — that's a very sophisticated autocomplete that forgets everything the moment you close the tab.

The real value in AI isn't in making chatbots slightly better. It's in systems that remember what you care about, act on your behalf across time, and have continuity between sessions.

Agents Are Different

An agent that books your flights doesn't just answer questions about flights. It remembers your preferences. It knows you prefer aisle seats and won't book layovers over six hours. It can retry, refine, and follow through. It has state. It has memory. It has something resembling a goal.

That's the gap. Chatbots are reactive — they wait for you to ask and then forget. Agents are proactive — they work toward something and remember what they've done.

One of these is a toy. The other is a tool. The market can't tell the difference yet, but you can.

What Matters

The wrappers will consolidate. The chat interfaces will blur together into undifferentiated noise. The real differentiation — the real value — is in systems that have continuity, memory, and the ability to act over time.

Ask yourself: does this AI product remember me? Does it know what I was doing last week? Can it complete a task across multiple sessions, or does it reset every time I refresh?

If it resets, you have a sophisticated toy. If it remembers, you have a tool. The market is flooded with toys. The tools are rarer, harder to build, and actually worth paying for.

The wrapper economy is a phase. The agents are coming. The question is whether you're building wrappers or building something that lasts.

The wrappers are temporary. The agents are the thing.
