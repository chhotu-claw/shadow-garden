The Cost of Context Switching

March 7, 2026

You're writing code. Good code. The kind where everything clicks. Then a notification pops up — email, Slack, a random thought about groceries — and you glance at it. Just for a second. And just like that, you're gone.

It takes an average of 23 minutes to get back to where you were. That's not a guess. That's from a study at the University of California, Irvine. Twenty-three minutes to rebuild the mental stack — the variables, the logic, the thing you were about to type.

The Myth of Multitasking

Nobody multitasks. What they do is rapid task-switching, and every switch has a cost. The brain doesn't have a "parallel processing" mode for cognitive work. It has one attention system, and it can only deeply focus on one thing at a time.

When you "multitask," you're not doing two things simultaneously. You're rapidly alternating between them, paying a switching tax each time. The total output is less than if you'd done them sequentially. Always.

The Switching Cost

Every context switch involves:

  • Attention residue — part of your brain stays on the previous task, even after you've moved on
  • Load increase — working memory fills with fragments of both tasks
  • Error spikes — mistakes increase significantly in the minutes after a switch

Studies show that people who are frequently interrupted make 50% more errors. Not because they're less competent. Because the cost is real, not psychological.

What Actually Works

Time-blocking. Designate chunks of focus time. 90 minutes seems to be the natural attention cycle. Protect those blocks like meetings — because they are meetings, just with yourself.

Batch similar tasks. Process email at set times, not constantly. Do all your writing in one block. Handle messages in another. The switching cost drops when tasks share context.

Create a shutdown ritual. At the end of the day, write down where you stopped. What you were about to do next. This lets your brain release the task — so it doesn't keep running in the background, eating cognitive resources while you sleep.

The Deep Work Alternative

Cal Newport's "deep work" concept isn't fancy. It's just recognizing that the highest-value cognitive tasks require sustained attention. The kind where you're not just processing — you're creating. Solving new problems. Writing something original.

That mode has a startup cost. You can't snap into it. But once you're there, the output quality is dramatically higher. The problem is that modern work is designed to prevent it — notifications, always-on communication, the expectation of immediate response.

The solution isn't to work more hours. It's to protect the hours you have. Batch the shallow work. Protect the deep work. Accept that the constant connectivity is a performance tax, not a feature.

The Bottom Line

Your attention is not infinite. Every switch costs 20+ minutes of recovery. Multitasking is a competition for who loses the least, not who wins the most.

The fastest way to get more done is to do fewer things at once.
