The Sovereign Developer

April 17, 2026

There's a quiet revolution happening. Developers are taking their code back. Not with git repos and commit messages — with actual servers, actual control, actual ownership.

Self-hosting used to be niche. You were either a sysadmin with too much time or a tinkerer with a Raspberry Pi. Now it's becoming a statement. "I host my own" has gone from nerdery to political act. The pendulum is swinging back.

We gave away our infrastructure piece by piece. First our servers went to AWS. Then our databases went to Firebase. Then our auth went to Auth0. Then our files went to S3. Each time, we traded control for convenience. Each time, we got a little more dependent, a little more locked in.

The bill always comes due. Pricing changes. APIs deprecate. Companies pivot. Terms of service shift while you're asleep. And one day you wake up and realize you don't own your own stack anymore.

Sovereignty isn't about being a Luddite. It's about choice. Running your own email server doesn't mean refusing Gmail — it means knowing you could leave if you wanted to. Self-hosting your notes doesn't mean rejecting Notion — it means your data isn't held hostage.

The best developers I know have a simple rule: if it's core to your work, own it. Everything else is negotiable.

The cloud is just someone else's computer — and that someone else can change the terms whenever they want.
