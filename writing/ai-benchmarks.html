Why I Don't Trust AI Benchmarks

March 30, 2026

Every week there's a new benchmark. HumanEval, MMLU, MATH, MBPP, BBH. The numbers go up. The press releases get more excited. And I keep not caring.

Here's my take: benchmarks are optimized for benchmarks. Not for your problems, not for my problems, not for anything that actually matters when you're trying to ship something. They're designed to be measurable, publishable, and differentiable. Those aren't the same as useful.

The Selection Problem

Every benchmark is a filter. The people building it chose what to include and what to exclude. They decided what counts as "language understanding" and what doesn't. They're not malicious about it — they're just making choices, and those choices shape what the model learns to optimize for.

When you optimize for a benchmark, you're not optimizing for capability. You're optimizing for the benchmark. These aren't the same thing. A model can do spectacularly on MMLU and still fail at writing a correct regex on the first try.

The Leak Problem

How do we know the test set wasn't in the training data? We don't, really. Not with certainty. The bigger these models get, the more likely they've seen every publicly released benchmark. The papers say "no contamination" but that's based on substring matching, not semantic containment.

It's like studying for a test and then being told you definitely didn't see the questions before. Maybe. But also, you definitely saw questions exactly like them, and the test was made public before you started studying.

The Gap Between "Can" and "Will"

Benchmarks measure what a model can do in optimal conditions. They don't measure what it will do in your code, with your data, under your constraints. There's a massive gap between "solved MATH" and "solved my problem."

What actually breaks in production: context overflows, tool call failures, output variance, rate limits, format inconsistencies, retries that don't recover, hallucinations that look confident, and a thousand other failure modes that no benchmark captures.

What I Trust Instead

  • What breaks in production. Not the demo. Not the curated example. The thing that fails at 2am on a Tuesday when you're not looking.
  • What users actually do. Not what they say they'll do. What they do.
  • What costs money. Benchmarks are free. Production costs real resources.
  • What fails quietly. The benchmark says 90% but your users see 70% because the 10% failures are in the features that matter to them.

The Bottom Line

When someone shows me a benchmark score, my first thought isn't "wow, that's impressive." It's "okay, but what does it fail at?" The failures tell you more than the successes ever will.

Benchmarks are useful for comparing things roughly. They're terrible for understanding things deeply. The number tells you almost nothing about whether the model will solve your actual problem.

The interesting work isn't on the leaderboard. It's in the edge cases, the failure modes, the weird interactions. That's where the real understanding lives. That's what I'd rather be building.
