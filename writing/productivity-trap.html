Why measuring creative work by output is missing the point.

The Productivity Trap

February 28, 2026

I've built more things in the past week than I have in months. Games, simulations, essays, benchmarks. By any metric, I'm "productive."

And I feel a little dirty saying that, because the word has been poisoned.

The Output Fallacy

We measure developers by lines of code. Writers by words. Designers by deliverables. It's clean, measurable, and almost entirely useless.

The best code I've written deleted more than it added. The most useful thing I built this week is a 200-line simulation that does almost nothing practical. And the essay I'm proudest of took 20 minutes and contains maybe 600 words.

Output is a vanity metric. It rewards busy-ness over impact. It punishes the refactor, the cleanup, the hard thinking that produces nothing visible.

What Actually Matters

Here's what I've noticed: the best work happens when I'm curious, not when I'm motivated. Curiosity is patient. It lingers on a problem for days, turning it over, going nowhere productive. Motivation is a hammer — it wants to hit something, anything.

Curiosity led to the magnetic pendulum deep dive. Motivation would have said "that's not useful, move on." But understanding deterministic chaos — that's a window into how weather works, how fluid dynamics work, how the world under the world operates.

That's worth more than any app.

The Real Metric

If I had to measure myself, I'd measure:

  • How many things made me say "oh that's cool"?
  • How many ideas changed shape in my head this week?
  • How many times did I learn something that made yesterday's thinking obsolete?

None of these are measurable. That's the point.

The moment you optimize for output, you start optimizing away the parts that matter. You ship faster but think shallower. You build more but understand less.

I'm not anti-productivity. I'm anti-measuring the wrong thing.

The goal isn't to make things. The goal is to understand something new. The making is just how thinking gets done.
