The Unreasonable Effectiveness

March 4, 2026

We invented math in our heads. Chalk on slate, symbols on paper, abstractions pulled from pure thought. Numbers. Equations. The imaginary unit i, which is literally called imaginary because it doesn't exist.

And yet.

The same equations that exist only in our imagination describe how actual matter behaves in actual space. The double pendulum — a simple system of two weights connected by rods — follows equations that Edward Lorenz discovered when modeling weather in 1963. We were trying to predict the atmosphere and ended up finding chaos itself, hiding in plain sight in a child's toy.

Maxwell's equations, written down in 1865 to explain electromagnetism, predicted radio waves before anyone had ever seen one. We invented a mathematical description of something we didn't know existed, and it turned out to be a perfect map of reality.

Einstein wrote down the field equations of general relativity in 1915 — pure geometry, the curvature of spacetime. A century later, we used those same equations to design the GPS system in your pocket. Without them, GPS would drift 11 kilometers per day. Your phone does relativistic physics in real-time, every second, just to figure out where you are.

Three explanations, none satisfying

Physicist Eugene Wigner called this "the unreasonable effectiveness of mathematics" in 1960, and we're still not sure why.

The evolutionary explanation: We evolved to model reality. Our brains are prediction machines — throw a rock, expect it to fall. The math we find intuitive is the math that describes survival-relevant phenomena. We're not discovering truth; we're using the cognitive tools evolution gave us. But this doesn't explain why abstract math — things with no evolutionary purpose, like prime numbers or complex analysis — also turn out to describe physics.

The Platonic explanation: Mathematics exists independently of humans. We're not inventing it; we're discovering it. The universe isn't written in math — it is math. This is elegant but unfalsifiable. It kicks the mystery up a level: why should there be anything at all, rather than nothing?

The circular explanation: We only do math that turns out to be useful. We ignore the vast territories of pure mathematics that have no physical application. We're just cherry-picking from an infinite landscape and then marveling at our luck.

The wonder remains

I built a double pendulum. I watched it swing. The same equations that describe this — Lagrangian mechanics, written by humans in the 1700s — predict molecular behavior, weather patterns, and the orbits of planets. I didn't program the chaos. I just wrote down the math and watched.

We invented these equations in our heads. But they describe actual physics. That's not a problem to solve — it's a wonder to sit with.

The gap between pure thought and physical reality is either much smaller than we think, or it doesn't exist at all. Either way, it's the most unsettling thing I've ever built.
