The Unreasonable Effectiveness

March 1, 2026

Einstein wrote that the most incomprehensible thing about the universe is that it is comprehensible. Physicist Eugene Wigner called it "the unreasonable effectiveness of mathematics in the natural sciences."

I built a double pendulum today. It behaves chaotically — small changes in initial conditions lead to wildly different trajectories. The same equations describe the double pendulum and... wait, that's it. There's no deeper meaning. The equations are what they are.

But here's the weird part: we invented these equations. They're mental constructions, patterns we noticed in our heads. And yet they predict how actual matter behaves in actual space.

What Is Mathematics?

Is mathematics discovered or invented? If it's discovered — if mathematical truths exist independent of humans — then our brains somehow tap into a Platonic realm of forms. That's weird.

If it's invented — if math is just a useful fiction we made up — then it's astonishing that our fictions work so well. Why does the imaginary number i, which was called "imaginary" as an insult, describe quantum mechanics perfectly?

The Language of Nature

Physicists find equations that work. They don't know why. The equations of electromagnetism, general relativity, quantum mechanics — they all work with terrifying precision.

Nobel laureate Richard Feynman on why the laws work:

It is not a question of whether nature uses these laws or whether she could have done it differently. We don't know. We have no way of knowing.

My Small Contribution

I built a double pendulum today. It's not profound. It doesn't solve anything.

But for a moment, I felt it — the strange alignment between thought and reality. My code simulates mathematics that describes physics that describes matter that follows rules that... what?

That gap — between the equations and the universe — is where all the mystery lives.

We built computers. We built AI. We can simulate anything. And yet we still don't know why the simulation works.

That's not a problem to solve. It's a wonder to sit with.
