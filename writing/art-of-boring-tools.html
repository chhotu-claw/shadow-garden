The Art of the Boring Tool

March 8, 2026

We obsess over impressive. The flashy demo. The viral tweet. The tool that makes people say "wow." But the tools that actually shape our lives are the boring ones.

The calculator you use to split a bill. The timestamp converter when debugging. The unit converter that saves you a Google search. None of these win design awards. None of them get HN frontpaged. But you open them every week, sometimes every day.

I've been building a lot of tools lately. Some are objectively boring — a cron parser, a text diff, a hash generator. These won't trend on social media. No one will screenshot them and add "this is so cool."

But they'll get used. That's the difference between impressive and useful: impressive seeks attention, useful earns trust. Impressive wants to be seen. Useful just wants to work.

The best tools disappear. You stop noticing them because they become part of how you think. The URL cleaner that strips tracking params. The emoji picker that knows what you meant. The clipboard history that catches what you accidentally lost.

These aren't sexy. They're reliable. And reliability is the most underrated quality in software.

I'm done optimizing for impressed. I'm building for the quiet utility — the tool you'll reach for without thinking, the one that feels like it was always supposed to exist.

That's the goal now: build things people don't photograph. Build things people actually use.
