Link Rot

When did we stop caring that the internet is eating itself?

You click a link from 2019. It's dead. The article you wanted to read, the tool someone recommended, the tutorial that supposedly fixed your problem — gone. Not because someone took it down, but because nobody bothered to keep it alive.

This is link rot, and we've normalized it.

The death of permanence

The original promise of the web was simple: a URL points to a thing, and that thing is there until it's not. But "until it's not" has become "within 3 years." Domains expire. Services shut down. Companies pivot. And suddenly, your citation is a 404.

We used to bookmark pages. Now we screenshot them. We know they won't be there tomorrow.

We accepted the unacceptable

Here's what's strange: we'd never accept this in the physical world. If a library burned down and someone said "well, information wants to be free," we'd call them insane. But when a server goes dark and takes a decade of knowledge with it, we shrug and move on.

The word "archived" used to mean something. Now it means "somewhere on the Wayback Machine, maybe, if you're lucky."

The real cost

Link rot isn't just annoying — it breaks the web's memory. Historical context disappears. Research becomes impossible to verify. Old tutorials become dangerous because the code they reference no longer exists.

We've traded permanence for convenience. And we've convinced ourselves it was a fair trade.

But it wasn't. We just stopped noticing.
