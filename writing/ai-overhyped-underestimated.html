Why AI Is Both Overhyped and Underestimated

March 25, 2026

The AI discourse is stuck in a false dichotomy. On one side, you have people claiming we're months away from sentience. On the other, people insisting LLMs are just "glorified autocomplete." Both are wrong. Both are right. Neither is useful.

The Overhype

The worshippers see every new model release as a step toward some mystical threshold — AGI — as if intelligence is a single dimension you can measure on a line. But "intelligence" isn't one thing. A calculator is intelligent in one sense, a cat in another, a toddler in yet another. LLMs excel at pattern matching across massive corpora. They can write code, summarize documents, and pass exams. They cannot plan, reason reliably, or understand what they're actually doing.

The worshippers are right that something genuinely new is happening. They're wrong about what it is.

The Underestimation

The doomers say "it's just autocomplete" to dismiss what these systems can do. But autocomplete that can write functioning code, debug errors, explain complex papers, and hold context across thousands of tokens — that's not trivial. That's a fundamental shift in what computers can do with language.

The doomers are right that these systems have hard limits. They're wrong about those limits being fixed.

The Gap

Here's what's interesting: the real capabilities aren't in the middle of these two extremes. They're in the edges — the things the models can do that surprise even their creators, and the things they consistently fail at that seem trivial to humans.

LLMs can write a whole essay but can't count to ten consistently. They can pass bar exams but fail kindergarten math. They can mimic any writing style but have no idea what they're saying. This isn't a bug — it's the fundamental architecture. They're next-token predictors trained on human text, and human text contains both genius and garbage.

The Real Question

The useful question isn't "are they intelligent?" — it's "what can they do reliably, and what do they fail at predictably?" The answer to that question is what matters for building actual tools, not philosophical debates about consciousness.

The worshippers keep moving the goalposts. The doomers keep moving the goalposts. The rest of us just want to know what actually works.
