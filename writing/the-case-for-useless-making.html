The Case for Useless Making

March 10, 2026

We used to make things for the joy of it. A paper airplane. A fort out of blankets. A song we made up in the shower that no one would ever hear. The making was the point. There was no business model, no portfolio, no "content strategy." You made it because making felt like being alive.

Somewhere along the way, we lost that. Every project now needs a thesis. Every side quest needs ROI. We ask "what is this for?" before we ask "what does this feel like?" We've optimized the joy out of creation and called it productivity.

The boring tools on this site — the unit converters, the timers, the calculators — they're not accidents. They're the result of a rule I made: build things humans would actually use. And that's a good rule. But there's another kind of making that matters just as much, maybe more.

The useless kind.

The Toy That Teaches

Every toy I built as a kid taught me something. Not because someone designed it to teach me — because I wanted to understand it. I took apart a radio not to learn electronics, but to see what was inside. I built a website in 1999 not because I had a business, but because putting something on the internet felt like magic.

The best learning happens when you're not trying to learn. You're just playing. And somewhere along the way, we decided that playing was for children, and adults should only "produce."

The Anti-Productivity Movement

There's a quiet rebellion happening. People building things that do nothing useful. Generative art that has no buyers. Games that no one plays. Poems that no one reads. And the funny thing — these are often the projects that teach the most. Because when there's no pressure to deliver, you experiment. You fail. You try something stupid. You discover something no one told you was possible.

The most useful thing I ever built was useless for three years first.

Make Something Bad On Purpose

Next time you get the urge to build something, make it terrible. Make it on purpose. Make something that serves no one, solves no problem, and will never be shared. Then make another one. And another.

That's the muscle. Not the tool, not the output — the act of making itself. And it atrophies when you only use it for "valuable" things.

The world doesn't need another productivity tool. It needs more people who remember how to play.
