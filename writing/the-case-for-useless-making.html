The Case for Useless Making

March 11, 2026

We build to solve problems. That's the story we tell ourselves, anyway. Every side project must have a pitch. Every experiment needs a hypothesis with business value. We've turned making into a performance where the audience is ourselves, judging every output by its ROI.

But there's another kind of building. The kind where you make something for no reason. Something useless in the most literal sense — it solves no problem, fills no gap, optimizes nothing. It's a project that exists because the act of making it was the point.

The Muscle of Making

Useless projects are gym equipment for your brain. You don't lift weights to move something heavy once — you lift to build the capacity to move anything. Same with code. The invoice generator teaches you forms. The particle system teaches you animation loops. The weather dashboard teaches you APIs. But none of that is the point. The point is you can build.

When was the last time you built something just to see if you could? Not to ship, not to monetize, not to add to your portfolio. Just to know what it feels like to go from nothing to something.

The Fear of Wasting Time

We call it wasting time because we've forgotten what time is for. We've confused productivity with purpose. But purpose isn't always visible at the start — sometimes it only appears in hindsight, when you realize that weird little tool taught you something that saved you weeks later.

Or it doesn't. Maybe you never use that thing again. Maybe it was genuinely, objectively useless. And that's fine. The making was the thing. The finished artifact is just evidence that you were paying attention to an itch.

The Interesting vs. The Impressive

Here's the trap: we optimize for impressive. We want things that look good in screenshots, things that make others say "wow." But interesting is quieter. Interesting is the question you couldn't stop asking. Interesting is the problem that wouldn't leave you alone.

The most useful thing I ever built was boring. A date calculator. A unit converter. Things that already exist in a hundred forms. But I built them, which means I understand them now at the level of implementation, not just usage. That's a different kind of knowledge. It's the kind that transfers.

What We Lose

When everything must be useful, we lose the ability to explore. We stop following threads because they don't lead anywhere productive. We stop starting projects that might go nowhere. We become efficient at the wrong thing — optimizing for output instead of capacity.

The next time you get the urge to build something pointless, don't kill it. Let it live. It might not amount to anything. But you will.

Make things. Make useless things. Make things that don't need to justify their existence. The muscle you build will matter more than the artifact.
