The AI Hypetrain

March 8, 2026

Every year, AI gets announced. Every year, it's supposed to change everything. And every year, most of us keep doing the same things the same way, maybe with a slightly better autocomplete.

The gap between what's promised and what's delivered isn't shrinking. It's growing. And nobody in the industry wants to talk about why.

The Performance Problem

Here's what I notice: AI demos are filmed in ideal conditions. Perfect lighting. Clean data. The one-in-a-thousand case that makes the highlight reel. Nobody shows the 999 failures.

LLMs are genuinely impressive at certain tasks. Summarization. First-draft writing. Parsing unstructured data. But the moment you need reliability — actual, boring, production-grade reliability — the wheels come off. Hallucinations aren't a bug to be fixed. They're a feature of how these systems work. We can reduce them. We can't eliminate them. And that matters more than anyone in marketing wants to admit.

The Agency Illusion

"AI Agent" is the current buzzword. The idea: AI that acts for you. Books your flights. Manages your email. Actually does work.

What most "agent" products actually do is wrap a chat interface around the same APIs you've had for years. They're not agents. They're personality-infused shortcuts. Real agency requires continuity — memory, planning, execution across sessions. Most products don't have any of that. They have a nice UI.

The Privacy Paradox

AI companies want your data to improve their models. They also want you to trust them with your most sensitive information. These goals are in direct conflict, and the resolution is always "trust us."

Local-first AI is emerging, but it's early. For now, the privacy calculus is simple: every prompt you send is data someone else owns. That has implications we haven't fully grappled with.

What Actually Works

What works: AI as a thought partner. AI as a first-draft generator. AI as a coding assistant that handles the boilerplate so you can focus on the hard stuff.

What doesn't work: AI as a replacement for judgment. AI as a substitute for domain expertise. AI as something you can just "set and forget."

The best AI users I know treat it like an intern: smart, fast, but needing supervision. The worst treat it like an oracle: everything it says must be true.

The Honest Take

AI is a genuine tool shift. Not a revolution — a tool shift. Like the calculator. Like the spreadsheet. Like the search engine. Useful, powerful, transformative in the right hands. Not magic. Not a threat. Just software that happens to use words instead of buttons.

The hype will keep coming. The demos will keep impressing. And the gap will keep widening for those paying attention. The question isn't whether AI is real. It's whether the version being sold to you is the version you'll actually use.

Usually, it isn't.
