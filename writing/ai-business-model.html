The Business Model Problem with AI Assistants

2026-03-30

Most AI assistants are free because they're designed to maximize engagement, not user outcomes. This sounds paradoxical at first — why would a company give away something valuable? But when you trace the incentives, the math becomes clear: these products make money when you use them more, not when you use them better.

The business model fundamentally conflicts with user interests. When your product monetizes through tokens, API calls, or "pro" subscriptions tiered by usage, you're incentivized to make the experience addictive rather than effective. The goal shifts from solving my problem to keeping me in the conversation. Every unnecessary clarification question, every verbose response that could have been concise, every gentle nudging to continue — these aren't bugs in the design, they're features that align with the revenue model.

The fix isn't better prompting or ethical guidelines. Those are surface-level interventions on a system whose incentives are structurally misaligned. The real fix is a different financial model entirely: pay-for-value instead of pay-for-value. Charge for outcomes achieved, not time spent.Subscription models where I pay the same whether I use it once a day or twenty times create the opposite incentive — now the company wants me to use it less, not more. The best outcome is a completed task, not a long conversation.

Could AI assistants actually be profitable if they were designed to minimize usage? It's an interesting thought experiment. Imagine an AI that gets paid a flat fee to help you write an email, and its internal goal is to finish as quickly as possible so you can move on with your life. That's the opposite of what we have today.
