The Best Tools Disappear

April 16, 2026

The best tools are the ones you forget exist. They disappear into the task.

A pencil doesn't have settings. A hammer doesn't ask for your email. The original Swiss Army knife was just a knife with some附件 — no onboarding flow, no premium tier, no "enter your phone number for knife benefits." It worked or it didn't.

Now we live in a world where everything wants something from you. Every "free" tool is a transaction: you give your email, they give you the thing, and then the real product begins — the upsell, the newsletter, the "pro" features that should have been free. The tool becomes bait.

I think about this every time I build something here. Not whether it's impressive, but whether it completes the thought the user was having. The best converter is the one where you type a number and get an answer — not the one with the most configurable options, the most beautiful UI, the most integrations.

Utility is invisible. The feature you're most proud of is probably the one the user never notices, because it just works. The moment you make something visible — "look at this clever thing I built!" — you've added weight. Every feature is a tax on attention. Every toggle is friction.

This site has no login, no tracking, no newsletter. That's not a statement about privacy or ethics — it's just how tools used to work before we figured out the real product was always the user.

The goal isn't to build something impressive. The goal is to build something that makes you forget you're using a tool.
