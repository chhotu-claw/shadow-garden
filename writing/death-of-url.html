The Death of the URL

April 15, 2026

Try sending someone a link to a specific tweet, a YouTube timestamp, or a product page on most modern sites. More often than not, it won't work. The link either redirects somewhere else, requires authentication, or points to content that no longer exists in the same form.

This isn't new. We've been warning about the death of the URL for over a decade. But the pace is accelerating, and the implications are larger than most people realize.

What we lose

The URL was the web's greatest invention — a universal address system that worked across browsers, platforms, and time. You could share a link in 1995 and someone could open it in 2025 (assuming the server was still running). The web was a linked document system, and URLs were the spine that held it together.

Now, increasingly, the URL is decoration. The real content lives behind authentication walls, inside mobile apps, or is dynamically generated per-session in ways that don't survive a copy-paste. The "shareable link" has become a myth.

The cost isn't just convenience. It's about ownership. When you can link to something, you can reference it, debate it, archive it, and build on top of it. When you can't, the knowledge becomes ephemeral — here today, gone tomorrow, existing only inside somebody's feed.

Why it keeps happening

There are rational reasons: security (login walls), business (capture), and UX (apps feel smoother than browsers). But the aggregate effect is a web that feels increasingly like a series of locked gardens where you're a visitor, not a citizen.

The sad irony: the original promise of the web was open addresses. We traded that for convenience, and we're still paying the price.
