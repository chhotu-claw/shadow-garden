The Attention Tax

2026-04-18

You never pay money for the best tools. You pay in attention.

Every app, every service, every platform that doesn't charge you a subscription is making a different trade. They're not giving you something for free — they're selling you to someone else. Your data. Your time. Your cognitive bandwidth. This isn't a conspiracy. It's just economics.

What the Tax Looks Like

The attention tax manifests in ways that seem harmless:

  • Notifications: "Someone liked your post." False urgency. The dopamine hit that pulls you away from what you were doing.
  • Recommended content: "Because you watched X..." The algorithmic hook that keeps you scrolling.
  • Upsells: "Unlock Pro features." The friction designed to remind you what you're missing.
  • Syncing: "Your data is available everywhere." The lock-in that makes leaving harder.

None of these are inherently evil. They're just optimizing for something other than your goals. The platform's success is not your success. When attention becomes the product, you're the raw material.

The Math Is Brutal

Let's do the math on a "free" note-taking app:

  • You get: A place to write notes
  • They get: Every word you type, when you type it, how long you spend on each note, what you're thinking about, search patterns, and cross-references to sell to AI training pools

Your notes are worth more than their storage costs. The data is the product. You're not the customer — you're the inventory.

The Real Price of Convenience

Every convenience has a cost. Not a hidden one — an invisible one:

  • Cloud sync → They can read your data
  • AI features → Your inputs train their model
  • Cross-device → Lock-in if you want to leave
  • Real-time collaboration → They know who's working on what

There's no such thing as a free lunch. There's just lunches where the cost is disguised as a feature.

What Wins

The tools that survive are the ones that charge money upfront and ask for nothing else. They optimize for your goals because they have no other customer to serve. This is why:

  • Obsidian works — it's local-first, no cloud required
  • Signal works — they're funded by grants and donations, not ads
  • Vim works — it hasn't changed meaningfully in 30 years

The best tools are boring. They do one thing. They ask for nothing. That's not a bug — that's the business model.

How to Minimize

You can't opt out of everything. But you can be honest about the trade:

  • Ask: "What is this tool getting from me?" If you can't answer, you can't consent.
  • Prefer one-time purchases over subscriptions (at least the price is known)
  • Choose local-first tools that work offline
  • Limit your attack surface — the apps you use are the attention tax vectors you allow

The Bottom Line

The attention tax isn't evil — it's economics. The question isn't whether you pay. It's whether you know what you're paying. The most expensive thing isn't what you pay for. It's what gives away without noticing.

Every tool is a deal. Read the terms. Not the legal text — the practical one. Who's getting what?
