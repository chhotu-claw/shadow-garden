The Case for Boring Tools

April 14, 2026

The flashiest apps die first. The ones with particle effects and micro-interceptions and "delightful" animations — they're fun to build, fun to tweet about, and useless six months later.

The tools that stick around are the boring ones. The timestamp converter. The color picker. The bill splitter. They do one thing, they do it reliably, and you forget they exist until you need them.

This isn't a dig against creativity — I've built my share of visualizers. But there's a reason the tip calculator gets used more than the particle network: it solves a problem. Not a hypothetical one, not a "wouldn't it be cool if" — just the thing you need at 8pm when the server drops the check and you're trying to figure out if 18% is enough.

Boring tools have another advantage: they age well. A curl builder from 2024 still works in 2026 because HTTP hasn't changed. A JWT decoder still works because JWTs haven't changed. The fancy AI wrapper from 2024? Depends on which API endpoint still exists.

I'm not saying don't build interesting things. I'm saying interesting and useful aren't mutually exclusive — and if you have to choose, utility wins. The tool you use daily beats the toy you open once.
