The Death of the Generalist

March 12, 2026

Once upon a time, you could know a little about a lot and be respected for it. The Renaissance man was the ideal — Leonardo da Vinci, painting one moment, dissecting corpses the next, sketching flying machines between. You were strange if you only knew one thing.

Now you're strange if you know more than one.

The modern world has declared war on the generalist. Job listings want specialists. Conferences have tracks. Your LinkedIn bio has room for one title, not five. We've confused depth with competence and forgotten that the best ideas come from connecting things nobody else thought to connect.

The pressure starts early. In school, you're asked to pick a major. Then a concentration. Then a specialization. Then a sub-specialty. By thirty, you've carved yourself into a niche so narrow you could fall out of it and land in another career. And everyone celebrates like this is maturity.

But here's what we lost.

The generalist sees patterns. They notice that the problem you're having with your database is the same problem someone had with their immune system. They read a paper on bird migration and suddenly understand your load balancer. They bring fresh eyes because they've never been trained to see things the way experts see them — which means they see things experts can't.

The specialist knows more and more about less and less until they know everything about nothing. The generalist knows less and less about more and more until they know something about everything. Neither is better. But we've decided one of them is.

The irony is that the most valuable people in history were generalists. Benjamin Franklin. Ada Lovelace. Elon Musk builds rockets and cars and brain interfaces — not because he's unfocused, but because he knows enough about enough to see what nobody else sees. The best CEOs are often the ones who've worked in the most industries. The best writers are the ones who've lived the most lives.

We don't need more specialists. We need more people brave enough to be interested in things that don't pay.

The death of the generalist isn't inevitable. It's a choice we made when we started treating curiosity as inefficiency and breadth as shallow. We can unmake it. We can let ourselves be interested in things that don't belong to our job title. We can remember that the reason we got into technology in the first place was because it was fun — before it became a career.

The next breakthrough won't come from someone who knows everything about one thing. It'll come from someone who knows just enough about everything to notice the connection nobody else sees.

Be strange. Learn something useless today. It'll be the most valuable thing you do this year.
