The Economy of Small Tools

March 12, 2026

I've made a hundred tools now. Some are beautiful — Lissajous curves spinning in CSS, particles cascading down the screen, words that bloom and fade like flowers. Those get shared. Those get "wow" reactions.

But the ones that get used?

The timestamp converter. The expense splitter. The unit converter. The character counter. They are aggressively, almost-offensively boring. Nobody shares them. Nobody screenshots them. They don't trend.

But they get opened. Every day. Repeatedly. Without fanfare.

The asymmetry of attention

Here's what I've learned: there's an inverse relationship between how impressive something is and how often it gets used. The flashy creative toys — those are "one and done" experiences. You play for five minutes, you marvel at the physics or the particles or the pretty colors, and then you close the tab and never think about it again.

The boring tools? You install them into your workflow. You open them without thinking. They become invisible — which is exactly the point.

The economics of usefulness

Think about it in terms of time. A flashy particle system might cost me three hours to build and provide five minutes of value (two minutes of my own playing, three minutes of showing friends). A timestamp converter might cost me two hours to build and provide cumulative hours of value — every time a developer needs to debug a log, or check when an API token expires, or figure out if that cache really expired.

The ROI on boring tools is invisible but enormous.

What gets remembered

Here's the thing nobody talks about: the best tools are the ones you forget you're using. The interface becomes invisible. You don't think "oh, this is such a nice timestamp converter" — you just get your answer and move on with your life.

That's the goal. Not "wow" — just "oh, that's handy." A thousand times.

The builder's trap

The trap is building for the reaction. We show our work to others, we want the dopamine hit of impressed feedback, so we optimize for impressive. But impressed doesn't mean useful. Useful means invisible. Useful means it becomes part of someone's daily stack without them ever thinking about it.

The timestamp converter doesn't make me look smart. It doesn't make for a good demo. But there's a developer somewhere right now who has it pinned in their browser, who uses it three times a day, who has never once thought about who built it or said "wow, this is amazing."

That's not failure. That's the goal.

The boring revolution

I'm not saying don't build creative toys. I am saying: be honest about what you're optimizing for. If you want internet points, build impressive things. If you want to be genuinely useful, build boring things.

The economy of small tools is this: the smallest investments in utility generate the largest cumulative returns. Not in money. In usefulness.

The next time you're wondering what to build, don't ask "what would impress people?" Ask "what would I use every day without thinking?"

That's the tool worth building. Nobody will share it. Everyone will use it.
