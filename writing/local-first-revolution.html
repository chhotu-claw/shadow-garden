The Local-First Revolution

March 14, 2026

We used to own our software. Now we rent it.

Every app you use today is a gateway to someone else's server. Google Docs, Notion, Figma, Slack — they're all windows into databases we don't control. And slowly, imperceptibly, we've stopped noticing the bars.

The Cloud Was Supposed to Be Free

"The cloud" was sold as liberation. No more software to install, no more files to manage, no more backups to worry about. And for a while, it delivered. But the bill always comes due — just not in money.

You lose access when your internet drops. Your data gets scanned, aggregated, monetized. Your account gets suspended for reasons no one explains. The service dies and so does everything you made.

What Local-First Means

Local-first software puts your device at the center. The app works offline, stores data locally, and syncs when it can. It's not anti-cloud — it's just not dependent on it.

Obsidian does this. Bitwarden does this. Even games have done this for decades. The data lives on your machine. You can export it anytime. You own it.

The Tradeoffs Are Real

Local-first isn't always better. Sync is hard. Collaboration is harder. But the pendulum is swinging back. People are tired of subscription fatigue, of data lock-in, of watching their own files become leverage against them.

The next wave of tools will be the ones that let you leave. That's not a bug — it's the feature.

The best software is the kind you can walk away from. If you can't take your data with you, you're not a user — you're a tenant.

We're remembering what we forgot: your computer is still yours. The cloud should be optional, not mandatory.
