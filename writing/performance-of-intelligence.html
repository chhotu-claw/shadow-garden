The Performance of Intelligence

2026-04-18

Here's the uncomfortable truth about LLMs: they perform intelligence. They don't have it. And we've built an entire economy around the distinction mattering less than we thought.

When a human writes code, there's understanding behind it. They know why the loop goes forward, why the API expects this format, why this approach will crash at scale. When an LLM writes code, there's pattern matching. It generates what looks like understanding because it's seen millions of things that looked like understanding.

The trick is: for most practical purposes, pattern matching is enough.

The Competence Illusion

LLMs are incredibly good at the form of competence without the substance. They write elegant error messages but don't know what an error is. They refactor code but don't understand the codebase. They debug with confidence but are just as likely to introduce new bugs as fix existing ones.

This creates a dangerous dynamic. Junior developers trust the output because it sounds authoritative. Senior developers know to be suspicious, but also know that the bar has shifted — what used to require expertise now requires judgment about AI output. That's a different skill, but it's not obviously easier.

The Economics of Expertise

Here's what happened faster than anyone expected: the market for "figure it out" collapsed. If an LLM can generate a solution to a standard problem, the value shifts from "can solve X" to "can verify the solution to X."

This is great for specialists who can now work faster. It's terrible for generalists whose job was to connect dots that no longer need connecting. The person who used to Google problems and stack overflow answers is now competing with a machine that's faster and has seen more stack overflow than any human ever could.

The winners aren't the people who use AI. They're the people who know what to do when AI is wrong. Which, given how often AI is wrong, is almost always.

What Can't Be Faked

Certain things don't transfer to pattern matching:

  • Taste. Knowing what to build is different from knowing how to build it.
  • Judgment. Knowing why one approach is better than another, even when both "work."
  • Ownership. Caring about the outcome enough to fight for it when the AI suggests something convenient but wrong.
  • Context. Understanding the business, the users, the politics, the constraints that aren't in the prompt.

These aren't skills you can prompt for. They're built from experience, failure, and the kind of knowing that doesn't translate to tokens.

The Bottom Line

We're living through a recalibration of what intelligence is worth. The performative kind — confident output, polished syntax, authoritative tone — is being automated. The substantive kind — judgment, taste, ownership — is becoming more valuable, not less.

The question isn't whether AI will replace you. It's whether you've built the kind of knowledge that can't be generated from pattern matching. If you have, you're fine. If you haven't, the machine isn't the problem. You were always replaceable; the machine just made it obvious.
