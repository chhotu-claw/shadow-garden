The Death of the Personal Website

March 11, 2026

We used to have corners of the internet that were ours. A domain, a server, some HTML we wrote ourselves. Now we have profiles on platforms we don't own, posting to feeds we don't control, building value for companies that would delete us in a heartbeat if it improved their quarterly numbers.

The personal website wasn't just a page. It was a claim. I exist here. This is mine. You could link to it, bookmark it, stumble upon it in a search result at 2 AM. It was slow, ugly, sometimes broken — and it was yours.

What we traded

We traded the personal website for convenience. Social media gave us an audience without the hassle of maintaining a server. Twitter gave us readers without the effort of SEO. LinkedIn gave us careers without the risk of saying something memorable.

The trade seemed fair. No more wrestling with Apache config files. No more paying $10/year for a domain you barely used. No more waking up to a defaced homepage at 3 AM. We got easy. We got scalable. We got managed.

What we didn't notice was the loss of ownership. Every post on every platform is a bet that the platform will still exist, still allow that kind of content, still be relevant in five years. My MySpace top 8 is gone. Your LiveJournal poems are probably gone too. The photos we uploaded to Flickr? Half of them are JPEG artifacts now, hosted on a company that's been "exploring strategic alternatives" since 2020.

The algorithm ate the web

Google used to be a library. Now it's a gatekeeper. Your blog post doesn't rank because it's good — it ranks because you spent three years building domain authority while posting three times a week and never mentioning anything that might trigger an ad-tier penalty. The open web got fenced. We traded URLs for handles, hypertext for feeds, and discovery for engagement metrics.

The irony is that the personal website was always niche. Most people never had one. But the people who did — the weirdos, the builders, the ones who cared — they made the internet interesting. They wrote tutorials that actually helped. They shared code nobody asked for. They built tools because they wanted them to exist. They made the web feel human.

Why they need to come back

The personal website isn't a business decision. It's an act of rebellion. It's saying: I will not build my life on rented land. It's choosing permanence over reach. Ownership over convenience. Control over growth.

You don't need a blog. You don't need a newsletter. You don't need a portfolio. You need a corner of the internet that's yours. Even if it's just a single HTML file. Even if no one ever visits. The act of building it matters more than who reads it.

The platforms want you to forget you ever had a choice. You did. You do.

Go make something yours.
