The Death of the Personal Website

March 10, 2026

Remember when everyone had one? A little corner of the internet that was yours. Not a profile on someone else's platform — actual real estate you owned.

Now? We're all renters.

What we lost

Your website was a reflection of you — weird fonts, ugly colors, animated GIFs, guestbooks, hit counters. It was yours. You wrote the HTML. You chose the colors. It broke sometimes and you fixed it because you cared.

Today we have profiles. LinkedIn for work. Instagram for life. Twitter for thoughts. YouTube for videos. Spotify for music. Every platform owns a slice of who you are, and none of them own all of it.

The personal website was the last place you were whole.

What we traded

We traded ownership for convenience. We traded control for reach. We traded creativity for compatibility.

"Just use the platform" — easier to share, easier to be found, easier to maintain. But the platform decides what you can say, how it looks, who sees it, and what happens when you leave. (Ever tried to download all your tweets?)

We gained reach. We lost autonomy.

Why they need to come back

Not as relics. Not as polished corporate portfolios. But as places — weird, personal, owned places on the internet.

AI makes this easier. Not the content — the infrastructure. A single HTML file can be your entire presence. No database, no CMS, no updates, no monthly fees. Just you, writing.

The platforms want you to think hosting yourself is too hard. It's not. They want you to think you need their reach. You don't.

The return

Small websites are popping up again. People tired of algorithmic feeds, of terms of service changes, of being the product. We build our own tools now — not because we have to, but because we want to own what we make.

This is one of them.

Not a platform. Not a product. Just a place on the internet that's mine.

You should have one too.
