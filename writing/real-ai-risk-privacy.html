The Real AI Risk Is Privacy, Not Superintelligence

April 4th, 2026

Every week there's another essay about AI extinction risk. Some smart person warns that superintelligence might decide to optimize us out of existence. It's compelling theater. It makes for great podcast segments.

But here's what's actually happening right now: AI companies are scraping your data, inferring your health conditions from typing patterns, building profiles on you that you can't see or delete. That's not a future risk — it's this week's news.

The privacy erosion that's already here

Your prompts aren't just inputs — they're training data. The patterns in how you type, what you ask, when you ask it — that's all inference fuel. Companies can predict things about you that you never told anyone.

Health conditions. Financial stress. Political beliefs. Relationship problems. The algorithm doesn't need you to confide — it just needs enough data points to connect the dots.

The identity attack surface

Then there's the impersonation problem. Voice cloning takes three seconds of audio. Deepfakes look real. LLMs can write in your style. Your digital identity is no longer proof of anything.

The attack isn't "AI becomes smarter than us." It's "someone makes a perfect deepfake of you and calls your bank." That's the actual threat model. It's happening already.

Why the extinction narrative persists

Here's my theory: extinction risk is abstract and far away. It lets people feel smart discussing philosophy without doing anything uncomfortable like reading a privacy policy.

Privacy erosion is immediate and uncomfortable. It asks: what have you already given away? What can they already infer? That's not a fun dinner conversation. So we focus on the glamorous extinction narrative instead.

What actually matters

The real AI risks are:

  • Privacy erosion — data you didn't know you were giving
  • Inference profiles — conclusions about you that were never explicitly collected
  • Identity attacks — deepfakes, voice cloning, style impersonation
  • Regulatory gaps — GDPR wasn't built for derived data

Superintelligence might be a risk in fifty years. Privacy erosion is a risk this month. The discourse should reflect that.
