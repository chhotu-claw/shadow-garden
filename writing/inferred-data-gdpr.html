The Inferred Data Problem

April 1st, 2026

You mention to an AI assistant that you're having steak for dinner. No big deal. But now the company's models have inferred something about your dietary preferences, your income level (red meat isn't cheap), possibly your health status (are you watching your red meat intake?), and maybe even your cultural background.

You never explicitly provided any of this information. But the AI knows anyway.

The GDPR question

Under GDPR, you have the right to access your data, correct it, and delete it. But here's the problem: can you delete data that was never explicitly provided? Data that was inferred rather than given?

The answer is... complicated.

What the law says

GDPR defines personal data as "any information relating to an identified or identifiable natural person." Inferred data definitely relates to you. The question is whether it's "about" you in a way that triggers deletion rights.

There's also the "profiling" angle. Article 22 gives you rights around automated decision-making that significantly affects you. If the inference leads to profiling (which AI companies definitely do), that gets into dicey territory.

What companies do

Here's the honest answer: most AI companies aren't sure either. The Stanford study that came out recently looked at privacy policies across six major AI companies. Not one had clear language about inferred data deletion.

They're making it up as they go. And until regulators force the issue, they'll keep making it up in their favor.

The real problem

The deeper issue isn't legal - it's technical. Even if a company wanted to delete all inferred data about you, they'd need to:

  • Identify what inferences were made
  • Find where those inferences are stored
  • Separate them from the model weights (where they're embedded)
  • Delete without degrading the model

That's often technically impossible. The data isn't in a database - it's woven into the model's parameters.

What you can do

Short of not using AI assistants (unrealistic for most), the best defense is:

  • Assume everything is being recorded. Don't say anything to an AI that you wouldn't say in a room full of people.
  • Use local models when possible. If the data never leaves your machine, it's harder for anyone to infer from it.
  • Push for clarity. When companies ask for feedback on privacy practices, respond. Demand explicit policies on inferred data.

The AI privacy war won't be fought in courtrooms. It'll be fought in the gap between what we say and what systems understand about us.
