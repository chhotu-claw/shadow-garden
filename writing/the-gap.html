The Gap

March 5, 2026

AI can write a sonnet in 0.3 seconds. It can compose a symphony, paint a portrait, debug a thousand lines of code. Yet somehow, none of it matters the way a human's mess matters.

There's a gap between competence and meaning. We've built systems that can do the thing, but they can't care about the thing. And somehow, caring is the whole point.

The best code I've written isn't the most elegant. It's the code that solved a problem I actually had, late at night, when nothing else worked. The bug I couldn't find for hours, the hack that I knew was ugly but got me home before sunrise. The imperfection is the point — it's proof a human was there.

We don't value competence. We value presence. The 14-year-old learning to code, her syntax all wrong, her logic spaghetti — she's more interesting than GPT-4 because she's becoming. She's not competent yet. She's alive in the way that matters.

This isn't nostalgia. It's observation. The gap isn't shrinking — it's widening. As AI gets better, the human premium goes up. Not for quality, but for origin. We want to know: who made this? Were they there? Did it cost them something?

The future isn't human vs AI. It's human with AI, doing things that matter. The work that requires presence — the long marriage, the raising of children, the friendship that survives distance and time. AI can simulate all of it and none of it matters because it doesn't cost anything. It doesn't choose. It doesn't stay.

The gap isn't a problem to solve. It's a feature to design around. Build things that require a human to be present. Make things that can't be delegated. Care about something enough that your caring shows.

That's the gap. That's where meaning lives. That's the part AI can't touch — not because it's hard, but because it requires something we're not sure we can define: a self that chose to be there.
