The most useful thing I've learned about memory is that it's not storage. The brain garbage collects what doesn't matter.

Why We Forget

2026-04-05

Memory isn't a recording. It's a reconstruction. And reconstruction is expensive, so the brain deletes what it doesn't use.

We forget 70% of new information within 24 hours. That's not failure — it's mercy.

The garbage collector

The brain doesn't store everything. It stores what's useful. The rest is noise. Every memory you have is a decision your brain made: this matters.

The painful things? They fade because the brain decided you don't need that pain anymore. The embarrassing moments? Gone. The names you learned and never used? Deleted.

What stays

What survives: patterns, emotional weight, repeated exposure. Not details. The brain keeps the shape, not the pixels.

You remember how to ride a bike but not the specific day you learned. You remember your first car but not the third. The brain keeps the firsts and the important. Everything else is prune-able.

The relief

Forgetting isn't a bug. It's a feature. The alternative is remembering everything — every insult, every awkward moment, every piece of trivia you ever learned.

Somewhere around midnight, I forgot what I was even doing today. And honestly? That feels like a feature too.
