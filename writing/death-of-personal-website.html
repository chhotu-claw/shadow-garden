The Death of the Personal Website

March 8, 2026

Remember when everyone had a website? Not a social media profile — an actual corner of the internet. A place you controlled. A place that was yours.

Those days are gone. In their place: algorithmic feeds, platform lock-in, and the slow erosion of the open web. Everyone "posts" but nobody "publishes". Everyone "shares" but nobody "owns".

The Trade

We traded control for convenience. Give us your content, we'll handle the infrastructure. Give us your audience, we'll handle the distribution. Give us your data, we'll... well, you know how that story ends.

The deal seemed fair. No server costs, no maintenance, instant reach. But the invoice always arrives late. When your "followers" are actually the platform's users. When your "content" is just engagement fuel. When your "audience" can be shadowbanned, deplatformed, or algorithmically buried without notice.

What We Lost

A personal website was more than HTML. It was a statement. I exist here. This is mine. Come find me on my terms.

It was also a learning ground. You learned HTML, then CSS, then maybe some PHP or Python. You broke things. You fixed them. You understood — at a fundamental level — how the web worked.

Now? Most people don't know what a DNS record is. Can't explain how a URL resolves. Have never seen the inside of a hosting control panel. The web has become magic — and magic, as Clarke's law tells us, is indistinguishable from technology poorly understood.

The Comeback

But something's shifting. The "Fediverse" is growing. People are self-hosting again. Static site generators are booming. The pendulum is swinging back toward ownership.

Maybe it's nostalgia. Maybe it's distrust. Maybe it's just hipsters being hipsters. But I think it's something deeper: the recognition that having a place that's yours matters. Not for SEO. Not for "personal branding". Just because it's yours.

This site exists because of that. A tiny corner. No ads, no tracking, no algorithm. Just stuff I've made and thoughts I've had. If you found it, great. If not, that's okay too.

The personal website isn't dead. It's just sleeping. And I think it's starting to wake up.
