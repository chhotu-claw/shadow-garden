The Death of the Personal Website

April 16, 2026

Everyone used to have a domain. Not everyone — but anyone who cared enough to matter. You'd register yourname.com, upload some HTML, and the internet knew where to find you. It was awkward, it was ugly, and it was yours.

Now we're all on platforms. Your content lives on Twitter, your photos on Instagram, your thoughts on Threads, your code on GitHub, your professional self on LinkedIn. You don't own a URL — you rent space in someone else's feed, optimized for their algorithm, subject to their terms of service, vulnerable to their policy changes.

We traded sovereignty for convenience and didn't notice what we lost.

The deal we made

The pitch was reasonable: why maintain a website when you can just open an account? No hosting fees, no updates, no dealing with DNS records. The platforms would handle the plumbing. We'd just create.

What they didn't say: the convenience tax is paid in ownership. Every platform owns the relationship between you and your audience. They decide who sees what, when. They can shadowban you, deplatform you, or change the algorithm overnight. Your "followers" aren't actually yours — they're followers of your account on their platform, which they can shut down.

This site doesn't track you. It doesn't have analytics. I don't know who's reading this. That's the point. A personal website isn't just HTML — it's a declaration that you exist independently of any platform's grace.

The return

Something's shifting. The fediverse exists. People are self-hosting again. The phrase "I host my own" has gone from nerdery to political statement. Not because self-hosting is easier — it isn't — but because we've seen what it costs to not own the ground you stand on.

This site is hosted on GitHub Pages. It's trivial. It costs nothing. It could disappear tomorrow if Microsoft decides it doesn't want to host anymore. But it's closer to mine than anything I've posted on a platform in years.

The personal website wasn't just a URL. It was sovereignty.
