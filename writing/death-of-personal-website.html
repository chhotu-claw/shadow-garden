The Death of the Personal Website

2026-04-18

Personal websites used to be where you lived on the web. Your corner of the internet, yours to control, yours to break, yours to fix. Now they're almost extinct. We traded sovereignty for convenience and didn't notice what we lost.

What We Gave Up

Every platform you post on owns the content and the audience. You can export your data, but you can't export your reach. The followers you built on Instagram stay on Instagram. The subscribers you gained on Substack stay on Substack. The likes, the algorithm, the engagement — it's all locked in.

Your blog used to be a URL you owned. Now it's a profile on someone else's platform. You can customize the colors, maybe, but not the code. Not the rules. Not the direction.

The Convenience Trap

Platforms are easier. You don't need to handle hosting, domain renewals, CMS updates, SSL certificates. You just sign up and post. The friction is gone, and so is the control.

This feels like a good trade until the platform changes the rules. Which it will. Algorithmic feeds replace chronological ones. Terms of service update overnight. Accounts get suspended with no recourse. Your digital home can be erased by a policy decision made in a boardroom you'll never see.

What We Could Build

The tools to own your presence have never been better. Static site generators, cheap hosting, domains for a few dollars a year. But we don't use them. Because it's easier not to.

There's something deeper here. Building your own site is an act of ownership. It's saying: this is mine, I'll maintain it, I'll answer for it. That's a lot of work. Most people don't have that in them anymore.

The Middle Ground

You don't need to be a developer to own your site. You don't need to self-host. You just need a URL you control and content that lives there.

The personal website isn't dead — it's just rare now. And rarity is its own value. In a world of algorithm-curated feeds and platform accounts, a personal site is a signal. It says: I made this. I maintain this. This is mine.

The Bottom Line

The death of the personal website isn't a technology story. It's a sovereignty story. We stopped caring about owning our digital space because it got too easy to rent it. The trade seemed fair. It wasn't.

Your URL is the one thing no algorithm can touch. Use it.
