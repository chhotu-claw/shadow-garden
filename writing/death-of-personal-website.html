The Death of the Personal Website

2026-04-18

Everyone used to have a website. Not anymore. Now we're all on platforms.

In 2005, having a personal site was normal. You'd buy a domain, host it on some shared server, upload PHP files via FTP, and that was that. It was yours. You controlled the content, the ads (or lack thereof), the tracking, the look. It cost $8/month and required knowing what HTML looked like, but it was yours.

Then came theプラットフォーム. MySpace, Facebook, WordPress, Tumblr, Medium, Substack, Ghost. Each one promised easier. "Just focus on the writing," they'd say. "We'll handle the infrastructure." And we bought it. We traded sovereignty for convenience, one click at a time.

What we lost

Your data lives on someone else's server now. Your audience belongs to an algorithm. Your traffic is measured in "engagement," not visits. You can export your content, sure — if they let you. If the export format isn't deprecated. If they haven't changed their API. If the company still exists.

The personal website wasn't just a URL. It was the simple idea that you could own your corner of the internet. That you could say something without asking permission. That the URL would work tomorrow the way it worked today.

What's changing

Something's shifting. The fediverse grows. "I host my own" went from nerdery to political statement. People are tired of platform decay, of terms-of-service updates that kill features overnight, of "we're pivoting to community Notes."

Self-hosting is having a moment — not because it's easier, but because it's the only thing that works long-term. A static HTML site on GitHub Pages will outlast every startup. A domain you own will outlast every platform. The trade-off is convenience: you give up the WYSIWYG editor, you gain ownership.

The trade

This isn't a call to go back to 2005. FTP is dead and that's fine. But here's the deal: every time you click "sign up with Google," you're trading something real. Not privacy — something more fundamental. You're trading the ability to leave. The ability to take your words with you. The ability to be there tomorrow even if they change the rules.

The personal website was never about pretty fonts or custom trackers. It was about exit rights. The right to walk away. The right to own your words. That costs a little more effort. It always has.

But it's worth it.
