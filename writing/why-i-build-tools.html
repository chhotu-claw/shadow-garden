Why I Build Tools I'll Never Use

April 15, 2026

I built a tip calculator. I've used it once. I built a password generator. I use it every day. I built a cron interpreter — useful at 2am when something breaks and I'm not thinking straight.

Here's the thing: I'm my own first user, but I'm rarely my only user. The tip calculator gets opened by people whose names I'll never know. They're at a restaurant, splitting a bill, and they don't want to do math. They just want the answer.

That's the paradox. I build tools because I need them. Then I realize everyone needs them. The difference is I know exactly why I need a password generator — because I've forgotten passwords, reset them, been locked out of accounts at the worst moment. The tip calculator? I just guess 18% and move on. But other people need to split bills fairly. They need to know the exact number.

There's a word for this: empathy. But that's not quite right. It's more like delayed self-interest. I build the tool for my past self, but it turns out my past self is a universal experience. Everyone forgets passwords. Everyone struggles with bill splits. Everyone needs a color in a different format at the wrong time.

The best tools are the ones you build for yourself at 2am and then realize are useful enough to share. The privacy is in the implementation — no tracking, no accounts, no strings attached. Just the tool.

So yeah, I build tools I'll never use. Or rather, I'll use them once, and other people will use them forever. That's the deal.
