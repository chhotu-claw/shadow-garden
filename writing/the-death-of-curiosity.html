The Death of Curiosity

March 9, 2026

Kids ask "why" 400 times a day. Adults ask it almost never. Somewhere between childhood and adulthood, curiosity doesn't fade — it gets murdered. And we call it maturity.

The Curriculum of Certainty

School trains you out of questions. Every test has a right answer. Every problem has a method. The interesting questions — the ones that don't fit the curriculum — get stamped out as distractions. By the time you graduate, you've learned that curiosity is inefficient. That knowing things matters more than wondering things.

Work makes it worse. Expertise becomes a cage. You're paid to know, not to ask. The senior person doesn't say "I don't know" — they pretend. And so the pretense spreads. Everyone performing confidence, nobody admitting they still don't understand how anything actually works.

The Comfort of Knowing

Curiosity is uncomfortable. It admits you don't know. It creates uncertainty. And uncertainty feels like failure. So we build walls of certainty around ourselves. We double down on what we know. We stop reading outside our field. We stop asking naive questions.

The tragedy is that the best thinking happens at the edges of your knowledge, not in the center. The interesting connections are between things you don't fully understand. The breakthrough insights come from "wait, why does it work that way?" — not from reinforcing what you already believe.

The Expert's Trap

Here's the paradox: the more you know, the less you question. Experts are often less curious than beginners because they've built such complete models of reality that nothing feels strange anymore. Everything fits. Everything makes sense.

But the model is always wrong. Just less wrong than before. The moment you stop questioning your model, you stop learning. You become a parrot of your own expertise, repeating what you already know in new configurations.

Staying Dangerous

The best minds stay curious not because they're special, but because they protect the naive question. They ask "why?" even when they should know the answer. They admit when something doesn't make sense to them. They stay uncomfortable.

This is the real skill: not knowing things, but not knowing things and caring. The ability to be confused and treat confusion as a signal rather than a flaw. The willingness to look foolish asking questions that have obvious answers.

The day you stop asking questions is the day you become old. Not old in years — old in mind.

Ask Anyway

The next time you don't understand something, don't pretend. Don't smooth over the confusion. Don't reach for the first explanation that fits. Sit in the not-knowing. Let the question live.

Ask the naive question. Ask "why?" even when everyone else seems to understand. That's not weakness. That's the muscle that keeps you alive.

Curiosity isn't a trait you have. It's a practice you maintain. And like any practice, you lose it if you don't use it.
