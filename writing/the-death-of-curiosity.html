The Death of Curiosity

March 11, 2026

Kids ask why about everything. Why is the sky blue? Why do birds sing? Why can't I touch the stove? Every sentence is a question, every moment an opportunity to understand the world.

Then something happens. We call it growing up.

The murder

It doesn't happen all at once. First, we learn that some questions are inconvenient. "Why do adults drink?" "Why does Grandma look sad in that photo?" The answers are complicated, or painful, or adult. We learn to read the room.

Then we learn that asking questions makes us look slow. The kid who asks "why do we need X?" in class is the kid who slows things down. The kid who already knows is the smart one. We start performing knowledge instead of seeking it.

By high school, curiosity is suspect. Questions become interruptions. Wonder becomes naivety. The students who ask "but why does it work that way?" are shuffled to office hours while the rest of the class moves on to the next topic.

What we lose

Curiosity isn't just about learning facts. It's about engagement. It's the difference between experiencing the world and just being in it. The curious person is alive in a way the satisfied person isn't.

We lose curiosity and gain something worse: certainty. The comfortable feeling that we already understand. That there's nothing left to discover. That the world is exactly as complicated as we've decided it is.

This is the death. Not the death of knowledge — the death of wonder.

The disguise

Here's the thing: adults don't stop being curious. They disguise it.

We call it research when we spend three hours down a Wikipedia rabbit hole. We call it hobby when we spend weeks mastering a video game. We call it professional development when we learn a new framework.

But notice: all of these are defensive. We justify curiosity by attaching it to something productive. We can't just wonder — we have to be working on our wonder. The question itself isn't enough. It has to have ROI.

This is the tragedy. We've learned to be curious only when curiosity pays. We've killed the useless kind — the kind that asks "what would happen if I..." at 2 AM for no reason at all.

The resurrection

The good news: curiosity isn't gone. It's just suppressed.

You can feel it when you encounter something genuinely new. A city you've never visited. A person with a completely different life. A piece of music that makes you feel something you can't name. For a moment, the questions come back. The world is strange again.

The trick is not to let that moment is to sit with the pass. The trick question instead of rushing to the answer. To say "I don't know" and mean it as the beginning of exploration, not the end of discussion.

Ask why again. It costs nothing. The answer might change everything.
