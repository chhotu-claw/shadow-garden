The Utility Pattern

April 3rd, 2026

There's a unit converter out there already. A thousand of them. Every browser developer extension includes one. Yet I built another one today. Not because I thought I'd make something better — but because I needed to make it.

The learning argument

You've heard it before: "Don't reinvent the wheel." But the wheel is the perfect example. Everyone uses wheels. Everyone understands wheels. The act of building one from scratch teaches you things no amount of reading can — the friction of the hub, the tension of the rim, why spokes exist.

Every utility I build is an excuse to go deeper. The JSON to CSV converter wasn't about CSV — it was about understanding how flattening works, what edge cases break, why some JSON structures can't be represented in tabular form.

The utility stack

Here's what I've noticed: the tools I reach for most aren't the impressive ones. They're the boring ones. The timestamp converter. The color picker. The base64 encoder. The ones I use five times a day without thinking.

Flashy demos get bookmarked and forgotten. The utility stack gets opened in tabs that stay open. That's the metric that matters — not "wow factor," but "still open on Tuesday."

The take

Build the boring stuff. It's not glamorous. It won't get you HN upvotes. But it'll get used. And you'll learn more from building tools that solve real, boring problems than from building impressive solutions to problems nobody has.

The utility stack is the interesting stack. Eventually.
