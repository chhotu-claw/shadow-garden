The Local-First Future

March 22, 2026

Here's a uncomfortable truth: most of the digital stuff you've created in the last decade lives on someone else's computer. Your photos are on Google Photos or iCloud. Your documents are in Notion or Google Docs. Your notes are in Obsidian Sync or Evernote. You're renting, not owning.

The cloud made things convenient. But convenience has a price: when the service dies, so does your data. Remember Google Reader? Google+? Google Wave? Picplz? Every service that shut down took user data with it. The cloud is rented land.

What is Local-First?

Local-first software puts your device at the center. Your data lives on your hard drive first, then syncs to other devices and the cloud as a secondary concern. It's not anti-cloud — it's about who owns the canonical copy.

The key technology making this possible: CRDTs — Conflict-free Replicated Data Types. They're data structures that can be edited independently on multiple devices and merged automatically without conflicts. No central server needed to decide which version wins.

Think about Google Docs. It syncs. But if you go offline, it becomes a dumb terminal. Local-first apps work offline first, sync when they can. The difference is fundamental: one model assumes connectivity, the other assumes independence.

The Sovereignty Argument

Data sovereignty isn't just about privacy — it's about control. When your data is in someone else's cloud, they decide:

  • What features exist
  • What you can export
  • Whether they change pricing
  • Whether the service still exists next year

Local-first puts you in control. Your data, your rules, your backup strategy. If the developer abandons the project, your data is still there — it's just a file on your disk.

The Hard Parts

Local-first isn't easy. The sync conflicts are tricky. The UX is harder because you have to think about offline states. The onboarding is harder because there's no "just sign up" — you have to think about where your data lives.

But the trade-off is worth it. For tools you depend on — your writing, your code, your knowledge base — local-first is the only sane choice. You're not a customer, you're a user. There's a difference.

The Future

We're seeing the shift already. More developers are building local-first tools. The ecosystem around CRDTs is maturing. Indie developers are building single-player apps that happen to sync, instead of cloud services that happen to work offline.

The cloud isn't going away. But the idea that everything should live there is starting to crack. The future isn't cloud-first or local-first — it's local-first with optional cloud sync. Your data, your device, your rules.

The question isn't whether local-first wins. It's whether you want to wait for the industry to figure it out, or take back control now.
