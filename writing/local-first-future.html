Why Local-First Software Is the Future

March 20, 2026

We spent a decade moving everything to the cloud. Documents in Google Docs, notes in Notion, photos in iCloud, passwords in 1Password's cloud vault. The pitch was compelling: access from anywhere, never lose data, collaborative by default.

It was wrong.

What We Lost

We traded ownership for convenience. Every piece of data we moved to the cloud became something we couldn't fully control. Terms of service change. Companies die. Accounts get locked. Features get removed. Your data becomes a license, not a property right.

But the real cost wasn't legal — it was architectural. Building everything around the network meant the software stopped working the moment you went offline. You couldn't work on a plane. You couldn't work in a cafe with spotty wifi. You couldn't work in a country with restricted internet access.

We built a generation of software that forgot how to run on the device it was running on.

The Pendulum Swings

Local-first is having a moment. Not because cloud is broken — it's genuinely useful — but because we've learned what we gave up.

The tools are emerging: Obsidian, Logseq, and RemNote for notes. Authenticated Origin Pulls and encrypted blobs for data. CRDTs solve the collaboration problem without a central server. WebContainers let browsers run Node.js locally. Tauri and Electron let you ship native apps that feel like web apps.

The technical problems that made cloud necessary — sync, conflict resolution, offline support — are being solved. Poorly at first, but getting better.

What Changes

This isn't about being anti-cloud. It's about defaulting to local, treating the cloud as an optimization, not a requirement.

Your data should live on your device first. Sync should be a feature you opt into, not a condition of using the software. If the server goes down, the app should keep working.

This is the model we had in 1999. We're just getting back to it — with better sync.

The Privacy Angle

There's a simpler reason this matters: privacy. Local-first software doesn't need to send your data to a server. It doesn't need to know what you're working on. It doesn't need to train models on your documents.

When your data lives locally, you can encrypt it yourself. You can back it up to a drive you own. You can delete it without filling out a GDPR request form.

That's not paranoia. That's sovereignty.

The cloud was a detour. Local-first is the return.
