The Offline Game

2026-04-18

Nobody disappears entirely anymore. That was 2005. But shrinking your footprint? That's actually doable now — easier than it was five years ago. Here's how.

The Real Problem

Most privacy advice is either useless ("use a VPN") or paranoid ("throw away your phone"). The actual problem is simpler: you have accounts everywhere, and most of them are leaking data you forgot existed.

The goal isn't zero. It's reducing noise — the constant low-grade exposure that adds up without you noticing.

Step 1: Find the Leaks

Before anything else, know what exists. Google yourself. Not ego搜索 — search for your email, your phone number, your old usernames. HaveIBeenPwned tells you what's already exposed. It's not pretty, but it's the baseline.

You'll find old accounts, reused passwords, data breaches you never heard of. This is the cleanup phase.

Step 2: The Account Excavation

Go through your email and find every "welcome" message from services you signed up for and forgot. Delete. Don't migrate — delete. Most of these accounts serve no purpose except collecting data on you.

Prioritize:

  • Social media you don't use
  • NewsLETTers you don't read
  • Apps you installed once and never opened again
  • "Free" services with no clear revenue model (they're selling your data)

Step 3: Shrink the Core

Your email is the hub. Everything flows through it. Make it private:

  • Switch to a provider you control (self-hosted, or a privacy-respecting one like Proton)
  • Turn off tracking pixels (most emails have a 1x1 pixel that reports when you open them)
  • Use aliases for signups (simpleplusemail@icloud.com — hide your real address behind Apple's relay)

Your phone is the bigger problem. It knows everywhere you go. Reviews, location history, app usage — all of it. Limit what you allow:

  • Turn off location history entirely (you can stilluse maps without it)
  • Review app permissions quarterly — most apps don't need contacts, mic, or location
  • Consider a secondary phone for things that require your real number (dating apps, food delivery)

Step 4: The Search Swap

Google knows more about you than anyone. Swap it out:

  • DuckDuckGo for日常 queries
  • Kagi (paid, no ads, no tracking) if you care about results
  • Startpage for Google's results without Google

This one sounds small. It's not. Your search history is the most intimate dataset about you — what you worry about, what you're curious about, what you're planning. It knows before you know.

Step 5: Go Loud on Purpose

Here's the counterintuitive part: the best privacy is talking less. Social media rewards oversharing. Don't.

You don't need to post about:

  • Where you are (especially on vacation)
  • What you're buying
  • What your kids are doing
  • Where you live
  • Your opinions on things you don't understand

The internet remembers forever. Your kids didn't consent to having their childhood uploaded. Start assuming everything is Permanent.

Step 6: The Physical Layer

Digital privacy has a physical component:

  • Use a password manager (1Password, Bitwarden) — unique passwords mean breaches don't cascade
  • 2FA everything that matters (SMS is better than nothing, authenticator apps are better)
  • Get a YubiKey or similar hardware key for critical accounts

What Not to Do

  • Don't go full tinfoil. Obsessive OPSEC makes you visible. Normal is invisible.
  • Don't trust "privacy" tools that aren't open source. If you can't audit it, it's just another product.
  • Don't pay for privacy with worse UX. You'll quit. Make it sustainable.
  • Don't lecture others. This is personal. You do you.

The Real Reward

Here's what happens: the spam slows down. The targeted ads get less weird. Companies stop sending you stuff you never asked for. You get less noise, fewer breaches, less stress about what's "out there."

You won't be invisible. You never were. But you can stop leaking unnecessarily. That alone puts you ahead of most people.

This isn't about fear. It's about intent. You chose this, not convenience.
