The Anti-Feature Movement

April 17, 2026

Every software company seems to have a feature pipeline longer than their revenue. Ship this, add that, bolt on another thing. The assumption is obvious: more features = more value. But it's wrong.

The best software I've used knew what to leave out. Not out of laziness — out of conviction. The original Gmail had a fraction of Outlook's features. Vim does less than VS Code. Yet both have passionate users who wouldn't switch.

Features are liabilities. Each one adds complexity, edge cases, bugs, and cognitive load. Users don't want options — they want to get things done. The 80/20 rule applies: 80% of users use 20% of features. But they pay for all of them.

The anti-feature movement isn't about building less. It's about building the right less. It's saying no to the good idea so you can say yes to the great one. It's knowing that the most powerful tool is the one that doesn't get in your way.

The best software disappears. The worst one makes you discover what it can do.

We should be skeptical of feature requests. Not because users are wrong about what they want — but because they often don't know what they need. The job of a builder isn't to add more. It's to add exactly enough.
