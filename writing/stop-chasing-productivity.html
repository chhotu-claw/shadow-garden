Stop Chasing Productivity

March 16, 2026

I spent years optimizing my workflow. Todo lists, time blocking, the Pomodoro technique, bullet journaling, PARA, Zettelkasten — you name it, I tried it. I had systems for my systems. My productivity setup was more complex than my actual work.

Then I stopped. And I got more done.

The Productivity Trap

Productivity is seductive because it feels like progress. Organizing your tasks, refining your note-taking system, finding the perfect app — these all generate the dopamine hit of "doing something" without actually doing anything that matters.

The problem is that productivity optimizes for speed, not direction. You can be incredibly efficient at executing a terrible plan. You can produce massive amounts of the wrong thing, very quickly.

What Actually Works

The best work I've ever done came from curiosity, not optimization. I'd fall down a rabbit hole on something interesting, build something weird, write about something I'd just learned — and somehow that led to the most valuable things I've created.

Not because I'm special. Because curiosity points you toward problems you actually care about solving. And caring about a problem is the only thing that sustains the effort required to solve it.

The New Rules

My rules now:

  • If I'm not having fun, I'm probably doing it wrong. Enthusiasm is a better signal than discipline.
  • Boring is fine — but pointless is not. Some work is just tedious. That's okay. But if it feels meaningless, stop.
  • One interesting thing per day. Not "one task." One interesting thing. Curiosity, not completion, is the goal.
  • Systems are support, not the point. Tools should serve the work, not the other way around.

The Irony

Since I stopped trying to be productive, I've built more tools, written more words, and learned more things than when I was tracking every hour. The paradox is real: trying less has produced more.

Maybe that's because the anxiety around "not being productive" was itself the thing draining my energy. Or maybe it's because curiosity-driven work has its own momentum that efficiency culture kills.

Either way, I'm not going back.
