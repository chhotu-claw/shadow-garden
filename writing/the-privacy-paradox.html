The Privacy Paradox

March 11, 2026

We say we care about privacy. We say it in surveys. We say it in op-eds. We say it when we're interviewed by journalists worried about surveillance capitalism. Then we unlock our phones with our faces, ask AI assistants for directions, and post photos of our kids on social media.

This isn't hypocrisy. It's something more interesting: the privacy paradox. We genuinely believe privacy matters. We also genuinely want convenience. And in the conflict between the two, convenience wins every time — not because we're weak, but because the tradeoff is invisible.

The Invisible Tradeoff

When you sign up for a free service, you're not trading money. You're trading data. But the cost is distributed — a little privacy loss here, a little there — while the benefit is immediate and tangible. You get a product that works. You get ease. You get connection.

The cost, by contrast, is abstract. It's not "your data is being sold." It's "an advertiser in some city you've never visited knows you like certain shoes." The harm is diffuse, delayed, and almost impossible to attribute. You can't point to a specific moment where privacy loss became damage.

This is the trick. Privacy erosion doesn't feel like loss. It feels like normal life.

What We Actually Lose

The scary part isn't surveillance. It's the normalization of surveillance. When everything you do is tracked, you start to optimize for the tracker, not yourself. You curate your searches. You phrase things carefully. You self-censor not because you're afraid of the law, but because you know the algorithm is listening.

That's the real cost: the drift from acting naturally to performing for systems you don't understand and can't see.

The Way Out

Privacy isn't a product you buy. It's a practice you maintain. Use Signal. Use a password manager. Use Firefox. These aren't solutions — they're friction. They're the speed bumps you install to slow yourself down before you trade away what you value.

The answer isn't perfection. It's intention. Ask: what am I trading, and is the trade fair? Most of the time, it isn't. Most of the time, the free product is more expensive than the paid one.

We can all afford to be a little more paranoid. Not because paranoia is rational, but because complacency isn't either.
