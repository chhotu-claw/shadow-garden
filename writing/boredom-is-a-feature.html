Boredom Is a Feature

March 12, 2026

We have solved boredom. That's the achievement of our era. Every waiting moment is filled. Every blank space is optimized. The fifteen minutes before your coffee finishes? That's now a LinkedIn scroll. The elevator ride? That's a podcast episode. The train journey? That's three episodes, actually, because you started the new series last night.

We used to be stuck. Stuck in waiting rooms, stuck on hold, stuck in traffic. These were not productivity failures — they were the default human experience. Your brain, left to its own devices, does something remarkable: it wanders. It makes connections. It has ideas you'd never think of if you were busy consuming something.

The problem is that wandering feels like doing nothing. And doing nothing feels like wasting time. So we filled the void, and we called it progress.

The Engineered Gap

The average person now consumes 100,000 digital words per day. That's roughly a novel every single day. And it's not even satisfying — we skim, we scroll, we refresh. The content isn't the point. The act of consuming is the point. We confuse input with progress.

Boredom was the gap where creativity lived. Not the exciting kind of boredom that makes you want to scream — the quiet kind. The "nothing is happening and my brain is going somewhere interesting" kind. That's where ideas come from. That's where you solved problems without realizing you were solving them. That's where you decided things.

The Attention Economy

Here's what's wild: boredom is expensive now. Not in money — in attention. Companies pay billions to capture the spaces between things. That three-second wait before a video plays? That's now an ad. That moment between notifications? That's a newsfeed swipe. We've monetized the gap, and then we wonder why we can't think.

The attention economy doesn't want you bored. Bored people stop scrolling. Bored people look out the window. Bored people think thoughts that don't serve any platform's business model. So they engineered the solution: never be bored again. And we bought it. We all bought it.

The Return

The counter-movement isn't about going offline. It's about the gap. It's about the unoptimized minute. It's about sitting in a room with nothing and seeing what happens.

Some of the best code I've written wasn't written — it was thought about during a long walk with no headphones. Some of the best decisions I've made weren't made in meetings — they were made in the shower, or while washing dishes, or in any of those small moments where nothing was happening and my brain had nothing to consume.

We built a world that solves every problem except the one we're not having: what to do with being alive between the moments that matter.

Boredom isn't a bug. It's a feature we've engineered away. And maybe — just maybe — it's the one we should bring back.
