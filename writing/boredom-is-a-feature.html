Boredom Is a Feature

March 9, 2026

We treat boredom like a disease. Something to cure. Something to fill. The moment that familiar itch hits — the phone is in our hand, the tab is opening, the scroll begins. We've become experts at avoiding the empty moment. We've forgotten what it's for.

The Architecture of Distraction

Every app on your phone is engineered to eliminate boredom. Infinite scroll. Autoplay. Push notifications. These aren't accidents — they're designed to create a permanent state of mild stimulation, just enough to prevent the brain from ever settling into quiet. The industry calls it "engagement." We call it living.

We built a world where silence is awkward, waiting is wasted, and doing nothing is failure. The productivity gospel tells us every moment must be optimized. Rest is recovery for more work. Entertainment is fuel for content consumption. Even our hobbies have become hobbies — quantified, shared, optimized.

What We Lost

Boredom isn't empty. It's the space where something happens. Daydreams live there. Weird questions surface there. The mind, unstimulated, starts to wander — and wandering is where creativity lives. Every great idea I've ever had came not from focus, but from that fuzzy in-between state. The shower thought. The 3AM insight. The idea that hits you when you're doing nothing.

We're not bored because there's nothing interesting around us. We're bored because we've stopped being bored. We've lost the muscle. The ability to sit with discomfort, to let the mind unravel, to be present with nothing — that's a skill, and like all skills, it atrophies with disuse.

The Case for Nothing

Some of the best thinking happens in transit. Not while reading or listening to a podcast — but in the gap. The train ride with no signal. The wait with no phone. The walk with no headphones. This is where thoughts connect. Where problems resolve. Where you hear yourself think.

I'm not saying we should quit technology. I'm saying we should leave gaps. Build boredom back into the day the way we build sleep. It's not wasted time — it's the time that makes everything else possible.

The ability to be bored is the ability to be present. The moment you can no longer do nothing, you're being had.

Reclaiming the Void

Start small. Leave your phone in another room for 10 minutes. Take a walk without earbuds. Sit in a waiting room and just... wait. It will feel uncomfortable. That's the point. The discomfort is where you find out what's actually on your mind.

We've built a civilization that fears the empty moment. But the empty moment is where we find out who we are. What we want. What we actually care about, beneath the noise.

Boredom isn't the problem. Our fear of it is.
