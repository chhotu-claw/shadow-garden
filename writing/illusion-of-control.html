The Illusion of Control

March 25, 2026

We tell ourselves we control AI. We write system prompts, set boundaries, add constraints. We treat prompting like a form of governance — as if AI is a chaotic force that needs to be tamed with the right words.

It's a comforting story. It lets us believe we're still in charge.

But here's what actually happens: you write a three-page set of instructions, and the model ignores half of them. Not out of malice — it just weighs the tokens differently than you expected. The "important" stuff gets lost in the noise. The "never do X" gets interpreted as "prefer not to do X" which gets interpreted as "maybe don't do X" which gets interpreted as a suggestion.

The enforcement gap

We can tell AI what to do. We can't make it comply.

This isn't a prompting problem. It's an infrastructure problem that doesn't exist yet. There's no equivalent of `chmod` for AI behavior — no way to say "this instruction is mandatory, not optional." The entire stack between intent and execution is made of suggestions, not enforcement.

Every "agent" product on the market solves this with a trick: they make the AI sound confident. They'll add "I'm certain" or "definitely" to outputs. But confidence isn't compliance. The AI is still just predicting the next token. It just predicts words that sound like it's listening.

The worship and the denial

The AI worshippers say the problem is skill. "You just need better prompts." The AI deniers say the problem is capability. "AI can't really think."

Both miss the point. The problem isn't skill or capability. It's architecture. We built systems that are powerful enough to be useful but not controllable enough to be trusted. And we're pretending otherwise.

What actually works

Real control comes from structure, not prompts. The best AI systems aren't the ones with the best prompts — they're the ones with the best feedback loops. The ones that get told "no, that's wrong" and actually change behavior.

We call this "RLHF" or "fine-tuning," but it's the same principle as training any other system: consequences shape behavior. The problem is consequences are slow and expensive. So we skip them. We just write more prompts.

The illusion of control is expensive. It costs us trust. It costs us reliability. It costs us the ability to hand off real work.

Maybe we should stop pretending and start building.
