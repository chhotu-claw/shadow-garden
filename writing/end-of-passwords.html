The End of Passwords

March 21, 2026

Passwords were invented in the 1960s for mainframe computers used by a handful of researchers. They were never designed for the internet. Every security breach in history — from Equifax to SolarWinds to the thousands of smaller leaks — traces back to one root cause: passwords.

We're not talking about a minor improvement. This is a paradigm shift. Passkeys don't just make authentication more convenient — they eliminate an entire class of attacks.

What Passkeys Change

The fundamental problem with passwords is that they're secrets shared between two parties. The server has a copy. If that copy leaks — and copies always leak — attackers have everything they need. You can use unique passwords, password managers, 2FA — but you're still playing defense on a compromised foundation.

Passkeys replace shared secrets with cryptographic key pairs. The server never sees your private key. There's nothing to leak. Even if a server is completely compromised, attackers get your public key, which is useless without the private key locked in your device.

The attack surface doesn't just shrink — it fundamentally changes shape.

The Infrastructure Is Ready

Apple, Google, and Microsoft have shipped passkey support. Every major browser supports WebAuthn. The credential managers built into iOS, Android, Windows, and macOS already store passkeys alongside passwords. Users who upgrade get seamless sync across their devices.

The transition is happening faster than most realize. Google reports billions of passkeys stored. Apple calls it "the end of passwords" as a product feature. This isn't a future vision — it's happening now.

The Hard Parts

Recovery is the unsolved problem. When you lose your device, you lose your passkey. Unlike passwords (which you can reset via email), there's no "forgot my private key" flow. The solutions exist — device transfer, cloud backup, social recovery — but none are standardized yet.

Cross-device is clunky. Scanning a QR code to move a passkey works, but it's not frictionless. Better solutions are coming, but we're not there yet.

The Verdict

Passwords will coexist with passkeys for years. Legacy systems, enterprise requirements, and user habits don't change overnight. But the direction is unambiguous: passwords are a transitional technology, and their decline has already begun.

The question isn't whether they'll disappear. It's how long the transition takes — and whether your service will be among the first to make the switch, or among the last to finally admit the obvious.
