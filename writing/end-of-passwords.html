The End of Passwords

March 19, 2026

Passwords are a 60-year-old solution to a problem we've only recently started solving properly. They're the dial-up modem of security — technically functional, universally understood, but fundamentally unsuited to how we actually live now.

What Passkeys Actually Are

A passkey is a cryptographic key pair generated by your device. The private key never leaves your device. The public key goes to the server. When you log in, your device proves it holds the private key via WebAuthn (the underlying protocol).

No password to type. No password to remember. No password to leak.

Why Now

Apple, Google, and Microsoft all ship passkey support now. Chrome, Safari, Edge — all have it. The infrastructure is there. The adoption problem isn't technical anymore; it's migration. Every existing account needs to either support both or force users to migrate.

The timing is right because phishing is epidemic and AI makes credential stuffing trivial. Password managers help, but they don't fix the fundamental problem: you're still sending a secret over the network that can be intercepted.

What Replaces What

  • Passwords → Cryptographic key pairs (FIDO2/WebAuthn)
  • Password managers → Device-based key storage (iCloud Keychain, Google Password Manager)
  • 2FA codes → Device presence (your phone/laptop is the second factor)
  • Reset emails → Recovery keys or cross-device verification

The Hard Part

Migration. You can't just flip a switch. Users with old accounts need to create passkeys. Services need to support both for years. And there's the recovery problem: lose your device, and you can't just click "forgot password" — you need backup mechanisms (recovery keys, trusted devices, social recovery).

But the direction is clear. By 2028, most consumer accounts will be passkey-first. Enterprise will lag, as always. Legacy systems will limp along even longer.

The Takeaway

If you're building something new: default to passkeys. Use password-only as a fallback, not the other way around. The future of authentication doesn't involve typing strings into fields — it involves your device simply proving it is who it says it is.

Passwords had a good run. They're done now.
