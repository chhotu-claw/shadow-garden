The Internet Is Not a Cloud

March 13, 2026

We say "the cloud" like it's something ethereal. Floating servers, humming somewhere in the sky, serving our bits and bytes like manna from digital heaven.

It's not.

The cloud is a building. Usually in Virginia. Or Oregon. Or Ireland. It's a building full of computers owned by Amazon, Google, or Microsoft, and they're letting you use some of their floor space—for a fee.

The Illusion of Elsewhere

When you upload a file to "the cloud," you're copying it onto someone else's hard drive. When you stream a movie, it's coming from a server farm in some industrial park. When you "save to the cloud," you're just saving to a computer that isn't yours.

This seems obvious when you say it out loud. But we behave as if cloud storage is somehow safer, more permanent, more magical than the hard drive sitting on our desk. It's not. It's less permanent. It's less under your control. It's someone else's computer, and they can delete your stuff.

They won't, probably. But they could.

What We Trade

We traded local control for convenience. Instead of managing our own servers, we let Amazon manage them. Instead of backing up our own drives, we let Google back them up. Instead of owning our data, we rent it.

The trade is often worth it. Running a server is annoying. Dealing with backups is boring. The cloud handles this for you. But we should be honest about what we're trading: we're trading ownership for convenience. We're trusting corporations to hold our digital lives.

The Real Cost

The real cost isn't the monthly fee. It's the loss of agency. When your photos are on Google's servers, you're playing by Google's rules. When your documents are on Dropbox, you're subject to Dropbox's terms. When your email is on Apple's iCloud, Apple can read it (they say they don't, but they could).

This isn't paranoia. It's just how it works. The cloud is someone else's computer, and on someone else's computer, you're a guest.

What We Forgot

We forgot that the internet was originally decentralized. No one owned it. No one controlled it. It was a protocol, not a product.

Now it's a product. And the product is us.

The cloud isn't the future. It's just someone else's computer wearing a friendlier name.
