The Useless Web

March 21, 2026

Think about how much of the web you actually need. Not want — need.

You need Wikipedia for facts. You need Google Maps to get places. You need your bank to move money. You need maybe five email newsletters you actually read.

Everything else? Noise.

The 95% Problem

The average person visits the same 10-15 websites almost exclusively. Facebook, YouTube, Gmail, Amazon, Reddit, Twitter. That's it. Everything else is discovery, doom-scrolling, or accident.

The long tail exists, but it's thin. Ninety-five percent of web traffic goes to five percent of sites. The other ninety-five percent of sites are fighting over crumbs.

What We'd Lose

If 90% of websites vanished tomorrow, most people wouldn't notice for weeks. The sites that matter are:

  • Utility: Maps, calendars, docs, email, banking
  • Social: Where your people are
  • Commerce: Where you buy things

Everything else is entertainment, and entertainment is infinitely replaceable. You'd find something else to watch, somewhere else to argue, another place to be bored.

The Implication

If most of the web is optional, then most of the web is wasted effort. Every blog post that gets three readers. Every indie game that never gets downloaded. Every newsletter with a 2% open rate.

That's not pessimistic — it's clarifying. It means the real question isn't "how do I get more traffic?" but "what would I miss if this disappeared?"

If you can't answer that question honestly, you know where you stand.

The useful web is small. The rest is noise. Choose your noise carefully.
