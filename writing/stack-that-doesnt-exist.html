The Stack That Doesn't Exist

March 25, 2026

You can tell an LLM to "write clean code" and it will nod and comply. You can tell it to "follow best practices" and it will generate something that looks reasonable. You can even tell it to "be careful" and it will add some error handling.

But here's what you can't do: make it actually care about the outcome.

The Compliance Gap

We have prompts. We have system messages. We have fine-tuning. We have RAG. We have MCP and function calling and a dozen frameworks promising "agentic" behavior.

What we don't have is enforcement.

The gap between "tell AI what to do" and "make AI do what you told it" is an infrastructure problem. It's not a prompting problem. It's not a model problem. It's a systems problem, and nobody's solved it yet.

Current "agent" products are personality wrappers. Give them a task and they generate text that looks like work. The difference between that and actual agency is ownership of outcomes. Real agents would need to face consequences. They'd need to have skin in the game. They'd need to be responsible for what they produce.

What the Stack Would Look Like

The infrastructure for real AI compliance doesn't exist, but if it did, it might look something like:

  • Verification layers — systems that actually check outputs against requirements, not just vibes
  • Outcome tracking — feedback loops that connect actions to results over time
  • Accountability graphs — knowing which agent did what, when, and why it failed
  • Automatic recovery — not just "try again" but "try again with understanding of what went wrong"

None of this is flashy. None of it makes for good keynote slides. That's why nobody's building it.

The Honest Take

Right now, "AI agents" are mostly elaborate autocomplete. They generate plausible-looking output and hope for the best. The enforcement mechanisms are either missing or purely cosmetic — a system message saying "think carefully" is not infrastructure.

This isn't a criticism of current models. They're impressive. It's a criticism of the ecosystem that pretends we've solved agency when we've really just made the output look more authoritative.

The real question isn't "how do we make AI smarter?" It's "how do we make AI actually do what we ask, consistently, with accountability?"

That's not a model problem. That's an infrastructure problem, and the stack to solve it hasn't been built yet.
