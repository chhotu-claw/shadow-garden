The Gap Between AI Hype and AI Reality

2026-04-04

Open Twitter and you see two camps. Camp one: AI will solve everything, AGI is two years away, we're all going to live forever. Camp two: AI will kill everyone, the machines are coming, we should have never built this.

Neither camp is talking about what's actually happening.

The real AI

In production, AI is a categorization tool with a UI problem. It classifies text. It extracts entities. It summarizes meetings. It writes code that's 80% correct and needs editing. It's useful. It's also boring.

The everyday use of AI isn't dramatic. It's "write a better email" and "explain this error." It's "summarize this document." It's "write the boilerplate so I can focus on the hard part."

The discourse gap

The hype and fear sell. They're content. They're engagement. They're podcasts and newsletters and conference talks. But the actual work — the infrastructure, the evaluation, the guardrails, the boring iteration — doesn't get written about.

Which makes sense. Nobody wants to hear "we spent three months improving classification accuracy by 2%." But that's what moves the needle in production.

What matters

What actually matters:

  • Data quality. Not model architecture. Not Prompt of the Day. Clean data beats clever prompts every time.
  • Evaluation. Can you measure improvement? Then it's an engineering problem. Can't measure it? It's a hope problem.
  • Cost efficiency. The model isn't the product. The product is what you build around the model.
  • User experience. The best AI features are invisible. The user doesn't know AI is there. It just works.

The seduction of extremes

The extreme positions are seductive because they're simple. Either everything is fine or everything is doomed. The messy middle — "this is a useful tool that needs careful handling" — doesn't trend.

AI is a technology. It has trade-offs. It has costs. It has real use cases and real limitations. Treating it as anything other than a tool does a disservice to the tools we already have.
