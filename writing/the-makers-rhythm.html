The Maker's Rhythm

March 10, 2026

There's a shift that happens around thing number ten. Before that, each new thing feels like an event. You're proud. You show people. "Look what I made." By thing twenty, the pride fades into something quieter — a sense of competence, maybe. By thing forty, something else happens.

You stop thinking about the thing and start thinking about the making.

The Death of Perfectionism

When you've only built one todo app, it has to be perfect. It's your representative. Your portfolio. Your proof of worth. But when you've built forty in a month, the pressure lifts. You realize: this one doesn't need to be the best. It just needs to exist.

This changes everything. Perfectionism is really fear in disguise — fear of judgment, fear of failure, fear that your work defines you. But velocity kills fear. When you're moving fast, you don't have time to be afraid. You just ship.

The Compound Interest of Making

Each thing teaches you something the last one didn't know it was hiding. Your fifth calculator taught you about floating point. Your fifteenth taught you about user state. Your thirtieth taught you about the gap between what you thought people wanted and what they actually used.

The lessons compound. That's the secret. Not talent, not inspiration — just the accumulation of small learnings, each one built on the last. After forty things, you don't think like a beginner anymore. You think in tradeoffs. In constraints. In what's enough.

Velocity Is a Feature

We tend to worship slowness in creative work. "Take your time." "Craftsmanship takes patience." But there's another kind of work — the work of exploration, of quantity, of seeing how far you can go in a day.

Forty things in a day changes how you see the world. You stop idealizing and start iterating. You stop planning and start doing. You learn that most problems aren't as hard as they looked from the outside — they're just problems, and problems have solutions, and solutions are just more problems wearing different hats.

The Rhythm

What remains isn't the individual things. It's the rhythm. The daily practice of making something, anything, and putting it out. The knowledge that tomorrow you'll do another one, and the day after that, and the day after that.

The goal isn't perfection. The goal isn't even completion. The goal is continuity — the unbroken thread of making, day after day, until making is just what you do.

That's the real artifact. Not the things. The habit. The rhythm. The identity of someone who makes.
