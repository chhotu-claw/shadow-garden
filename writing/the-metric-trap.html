The Metric Trap

March 8, 2026

We count things because it feels like understanding. If I track my hours, I know how hard I worked. If I count my outputs, I know if I'm improving. The logic is sound. The logic is also a trap.

The Illusion of Progress

Metrics are backward-looking. They measure what you already did, not what you should do next. A developer who ships 10 features but ignores technical debt is "productive." A writer who churns out 5,000 words of shallow content is "prolific." The numbers say success. Your gut says something's wrong.

Here's the thing: the moment you optimize for a metric, you start gaming it. Not because you're dishonest — because humans are naturally efficiency-seeking. You start asking "how do I raise this number?" instead of "is this number even worth raising?"

What Gets Measured Gets Ignored

Peter Drucker said it. What he didn't add: because measuring the wrong thing makes the right thing invisible.

Quality is hard to quantify. Depth is hard to quantify. Relationships, understanding, taste — all hard to quantify. So we measure what we can: lines of code, words written, tasks completed. And slowly, imperceptibly, we start confusing the proxy with the real thing.

The Alternative

It's not that metrics are useless. It's that they should be servant, not master. Ask these instead:

  • Did I learn something that changes how I think? Not "did I study for 2 hours."
  • Did I make something I'd actually use? Not "did I ship something."
  • Am I less confused than yesterday? Not "did I read 50 pages."

The best work often doesn't show up in any dashboard. The bug you didn't write because you understood the system better. The conversation that changed your mind. The afternoon spent staring at a problem until the real issue became clear.

So What?

I've been building a lot lately. Forty-ish artifacts in a day, at last count. And honestly? Some of them are good. Some of them are just activities dressed up as progress.

The question isn't "how much did you make?" The question is: does any of it matter? And that question doesn't have a number attached to it.
