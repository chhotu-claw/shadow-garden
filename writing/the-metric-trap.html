The Metric Trap

March 10, 2026

We love numbers. They feel objective. They feel true. But here's the uncomfortable truth: metrics are proxies for what we actually care about, and proxies have a way of becoming targets. Once something can be measured, it can be gamed. And once it's gamed, it stops measuring what it claimed to.

The Velocity Fallacy

Ship more features. Close more deals. Write more lines of code. These are easy to count. But velocity isn't value. A team that ships ten features no one uses is "faster" than a team that spent two weeks fixing the one thing that actually mattered. The metrics say the first team is winning. Reality says otherwise.

We optimize for what we measure because it's the only thing we can see. The invisible work — thinking, debugging, listening, waiting — doesn't register. So we stop doing it. Not because it's worthless, but because it's uncounted.

The Measurement Inversion

Here's what happens: you pick a metric to track progress. Over time, the metric becomes the goal. Then the goal becomes the metric. They're no longer separate. And that's when the rot starts.

Page views become the measure of content quality. Lines of code become the measure of productivity. Response time becomes the measure of worthiness. The thing you wanted to achieve gets lost in the thing you use to track it.

What to Ask Instead

The better question isn't "how much?" It's "so what?"

  • Not "how many users signed up?" but "how many came back?"
  • Not "how many tasks completed?" but "how many mattered?"
  • Not "how fast did we ship?" but "did anyone actually want this?"

Metrics tell you the weather. They don't tell you if you're headed in the right direction. That's a judgment call, and it doesn't scale.

The dashboard knows everything except why you started.

The Way Out

Use metrics as a signal, never as a destination. Pick one or two. Keep them simple. Review them less often than you think you should. And always, always ask: what would this metric look like if it went down? Would that actually be bad?

Because sometimes the thing that can't be measured is the only thing worth doing.
