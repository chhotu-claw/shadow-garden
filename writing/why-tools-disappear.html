Why Tools Disappear

March 22, 2026

The "app is dead" narrative just won't die. It's been recycled every few years: SaaS was supposed to kill desktop apps, then web apps were supposed to kill software, then PWAs were supposed to replace native apps, then AI was supposed to make interfaces obsolete. None of it stuck. Why?

The Abstraction Trap

Each wave of "the app is dead" is really just another layer of abstraction. SaaS didn't kill software — it moved it to the cloud. Web apps didn't kill desktop apps — they made software accessible without installation. PWAs didn't kill native apps — they made web apps feel native. AI agents won't kill apps either — they'll just become another way to interact with them.

The screen is still there. The interaction model is still there. What's changing is what's behind the glass.

The Infrastructure Effect

The best tools become invisible. They sink into the infrastructure until you stop noticing them. HTTP became "just how the internet works." Email became invisible. Browsers became a utility. The same thing happens at every layer — the tools you use daily become background noise.

This isn't death. It's maturation. When a tool becomes infrastructure, it stops being a thing you think about and starts being a thing you just use. That's not failure — that's success.

What Actually Changes

What's shifting is the surface. The interface layer keeps getting more abstract. From CLI to GUI to touch to voice to agent. Each abstraction makes things easier for newcomers but harder to customize. The agent era won't be different — it'll just be another interface layer on top of everything that came before.

The apps aren't disappearing. They're just getting further from the metal.

The Real Question

Instead of asking "will apps disappear?", ask: "what would I miss if this disappeared?" If you can't answer that, you know where you stand. The useful web is small. The average person visits 10-15 sites exclusively. 95% of web traffic goes to 5% of sites.

The question isn't how to get more traffic. It's what you'd build if it mattered.
