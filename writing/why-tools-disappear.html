Why Tools Disappear

March 20, 2026

Every few years, someone declares that a category of tools is "dying." First it was desktop apps. Then it was websites. Then it was native apps. Now it's apps themselves, supposedly replaced by AI agents.

Here's what actually happens: the tools don't disappear. They become invisible. They sink into the infrastructure until you stop noticing them.

The Invisibility Principle

Think about HTTP. In the early days, "the web" was a revolutionary tool. Now it's invisible — you don't think about HTTP when you check your email. The protocol became infrastructure.

The same thing happened to:

  • Email clients: You don't "use an email client" — you just use email. Gmail is infrastructure.
  • Word processors: Google Docs is so embedded in work that people don't think of it as "software."
  • Operating systems: Chrome OS, iPad, and phones made the OS invisible. Most people don't know what OS their phone runs.
  • Web browsers: Safari is just "the thing I use to get to the internet" for most people.

The pattern is consistent: a tool becomes successful precisely when it stops being visible as a tool.

What AI Agents Actually Change

When people say "apps will disappear," they mean: instead of clicking through interfaces, you'll just tell an agent what you want.

This isn't new. It's happened before:

  • Command lines replaced punch cards → GUIs replaced command lines → voice assistants replaced typing → each transition was "the end" of the previous interface
  • None of them actually killed the previous layer — they just added another one on top

The screen is still there. You're still looking at it. The "app" is just abstractified one level further.

The Good News

Tools that disappear are tools that won. The best software becomes so essential that it stops being software and becomes part of the furniture.

So when someone tells you "X is dead," ask: is it dead, or did it just win?

The most important skill in tech isn't learning tools — it's recognizing which ones are winning.
