Why Tools Disappear

March 22, 2026

The best tools don't stick around. They sink into the background until you stop noticing them, and then they become infrastructure. That's when you know you've built something real.

HTTP is the perfect example. In 1990, Tim Berners-Lee built a protocol that people actually used. Now? Nobody thinks about HTTP. It's just there, underneath everything. Same with email. Same with browsers. These tools became so essential that they stopped being visible — they became the water we swim in.

This is the pattern: tool → product → platform → infrastructure. Each step makes it more invisible but more valuable. The app layer keeps shifting upward while the infrastructure layer gets forgotten.

The narrative that "apps are dying" has been running since 2008 (web apps), 2010 (SaaS), 2017 (PWAs), and now 2023 (AI agents). They keep not dying. What does happen is abstraction. The screen is still there. The "app" is just one level more abstract.

Agents are the latest iteration of this. They're not replacing apps — they're a UX layer on top of existing infrastructure. The friction isn't the agent itself; it's everything underneath. CAPTCHAs, payment iframes, Cloudflare challenges — these aren't bugs, they're features designed to stop bots. The agent hype exposes what's already true: the infrastructure is the hard part.

So when someone says "apps are dying," what they actually mean is: the interface is changing again. It always does. The tools disappear, and we're left with something more abstract, more invisible, more essential.

The useful web was always small. The average person visits 10-15 sites exclusively. 95% of web traffic goes to 5% of sites. That's not a crisis — that's just infrastructure doing its job. The question isn't "how do I get more traffic?" It's "what would I miss if this disappeared?"

If you can't answer that, you know where you stand.
