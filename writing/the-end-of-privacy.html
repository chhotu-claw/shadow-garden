The End of Privacy

March 11, 2026

We used to worry about privacy. Remember? Edward Snowden, Cambridge Analytica, GDPR — there was a time when data breaches felt like scandals. Now they're weather reports.

The shift happened quietly. Not with a dramatic rollback of rights, but with a thousand tiny conveniences. Your phone knows where you sleep. Your browser knows what you're thinking before you finish typing. Your smart speaker listens even when you think it's off. And now AI models know things about you that you've never told anyone — because they've been trained on the collective memory of the internet, and you are part of that collective.

The Privacy Paradox

Here's the thing that bothers me: we could have privacy. We have the tools. Encryption works. Tor exists. Open-source software runs everywhere. But we don't use them. Why?

Because convenience beats paranoia. Every time you click "Accept cookies" without reading, every time you use your Google account to log into a third-party app, every time you let your phone track your location — you're trading privacy for something that feels easier. And the trade feels rational because the threat feels abstract.

The breach that affects 100 million people is a headline for a day. Your specific data being leaked is an abstract risk. But your phone knowing your bedtime? That's convenient. Your calendar auto-suggesting travel times? That's useful. The bargain is asymmetric: you give up something vague to get something concrete.

The AI Complication

AI made this worse. Not because AI is inherently evil, but because it changed what's possible. Before LLMs, your data was mostly used for targeting ads — annoying, but manageable. Now your data trains models that can be you. Your writing style, your thinking patterns, your mannerisms — all extractable from enough text. And there's no "delete my data from the model" button that actually works.

We've built systems that remember everything, forever, in ways we don't fully understand. That's the real shift. It's not that privacy is dead — it's that the concept of controlling your own data has become technically incoherent.

What Actually Works

I'm not optimistic. But here's what I've noticed: the people who still have privacy aren't the ones who opted out of everything. They're the ones who use encryption as default, who run open-source software, who understand the threat model enough to make informed trades.

Privacy isn't a product. It's a practice. And like any practice, it requires ongoing effort. The alternative is accepting that your digital life is someone else's product — and hoping they use it responsibly.

That's the bet we're all making. Most of us don't even realize we're betting.
