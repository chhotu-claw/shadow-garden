Privacy Without Paranoia

2026-04-18

Most privacy advice is either useless ("just use Signal") or paranoid ("wrap your phone in foil"). Neither is realistic. The middle ground is where actual progress happens.

The Real Problem Isn't Surveillance

We're not worried about the government. We've already made peace with that trade-off. The problem is的商业化 — your data is a product, and the product is being sold to people who want your attention, your clicks, your money.

Every account you forgot about is leaking data you don't remember sharing. Every "free" service is making money off you in ways you're not aware of. That's not paranoia — that's the business model.

First: Delete Before You Optimize

Don't start with new tools. Start with fewer accounts.

  • Go through your email. Search for "verify your account" or "confirm your email" — delete the accounts you never confirmed. They're still sitting in some database.
  • Check haveibeenpwned.com. See what's already out there. It's humbling.
  • Delete old social accounts. That MySpace from 2008? The photo album you uploaded to Fotolog? Gone, but the data isn't.

The Email Problem

Your email is the hub. Everything ties back to it. If someone gets access to your email, they get access to everything.

  • Use a password manager. Not because you're paranoid — because reusing passwords is how breaches spread.
  • Enable 2FA. SMS is better than nothing, but auth apps are better than SMS. Hardware keys are better still.
  • Consider email aliases. services like SimpleLogin (now-owned by Proton) let you have different emails for different services. The ability to shut down an alias when it starts receiving spam is underrated.

The Phone Problem

Your phone is the bigger problem. It knows where you are, who you talk to, what you search, when you sleep.

  • Review app permissions. Does a flashlight app need your location? Does a calculator need your contacts?
  • Turn off ad tracking. iOS and Android both have settings for this. They won't stop everything, but they make it harder.
  • Consider a secondary phone. Not for everyone, but if you use your personal phone for work, having a dedicated line for each isn't paranoid — it's practical.

The Search Problem

Your search history is the most intimate data. It shows what you care about, what you're working on, your fears, your questions you wouldn't ask a person.

  • Use a different search engine for sensitive queries. DuckDuckGo isn't perfect, but it's not building a profile of you.
  • Clear your history regularly. Or don't save it in the first place.
  • Consider what you're searching for. "How to fix X" tells everyone you're working on X. Sometimes that's fine. Sometimes it's not.

What We Actually Give Up

This is the honest part. Privacy has costs:

  • Convenience. Logging in with Google is faster than making an account. That's the point.
  • Personalization. Recommendations work because the service knows what you like. Privacy breaks that.
  • Sync. Your data on all your devices is great until it's end-to-end encrypted and you lose the key.

The trade-off is real. You don't have to make it. But you should know what you're trading.

The Bottom Line

You won't be invisible. You shouldn't try. But you can stop leaking unnecessarily. Start with deletion, not optimization. Use better passwords. Check your permissions. Clear your search history occasionally.

You don't need to go dark. You just need to be intentional about what you leave visible.
