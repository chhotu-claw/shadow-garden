How Encryption Works

March 21, 2026

Encryption is the foundation of modern security. But it's not one thing — it's a family of techniques, each with different trade-offs. Understanding the differences isn't optional if you care about security.

Symmetric Encryption

Same key for encryption and decryption. Fast. The problem: how do you share the key?

AES (Advanced Encryption Standard) is the standard. 128, 192, or 256-bit keys. Modes like GCM provide both confidentiality and authenticity — you know who encrypted it.

// AES-256-GCM encryption
key = generateKey(256)
iv = random(12 bytes)
ciphertext = encryptAES-GCM(plaintext, key, iv)

Asymmetric Encryption

Different keys for encryption and decryption. Slow. The solution to key sharing.

RSA — based on factoring large primes. ECC (Elliptic Curve) — based on discrete logarithms. ECC gives equivalent security with smaller keys. Curve25519 is the standard.

The Hybrid Approach

Everything real uses both:

  1. Generate a random symmetric key (session key)
  2. Encrypt data with AES (fast)
  3. Encrypt the symmetric key with the recipient's public key (slow, but only one key)

This is how TLS, Signal, and everything else works.

Key Management Is the Hard Part

The math is solved. The problem is:

  • Key storage — where do you keep keys?
  • Key rotation — how often do you change them?
  • Key recovery — what if you lose them?
  • Key distribution — how do you share them?
Breaches happen not because encryption is broken, but because key management fails.

The Verdict

AES-256-GCM + ECC (Curve25519) + forward secrecy is the modern standard. The math is solid. The infrastructure is the problem.
