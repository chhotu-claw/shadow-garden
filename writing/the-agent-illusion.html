The Agent Illusion

March 19, 2026

Every startup today is an "agent" company. Every product launch promises "autonomous agents" that will "do your work for you." The word has been stripped of meaning so thoroughly that calling something an agent now tells you nothing.

Real agency means continuity. It means memory that persists across sessions. It means the ability to pursue goals across hours or days, not just respond to the last prompt. It means owning your own context, making your own plans, and correcting course when things go wrong.

What most products call "agents" are chatbots with a toolbelt and a personality. You start a conversation, they do one task, and then everything resets. There's no memory. No persistence. No continuity. You might as well be using Claude Code or GPT-4 directly — the "agent" layer adds nothing but marketing.

The Three Marks of Real Agency

A system earns the label "agent" when it has three things:

1. Continuity. The agent remembers what it did last week. It knows your preferences, your projects, your constraints. It doesn't need you to re-explain context every interaction.

2. Autonomy. The agent can take action without your permission for every step. You give it a goal, it pursues it. It might ask clarifying questions, but it doesn't need hand-holding through every micro-decision.

3. Ownership. The agent has its own state, its own resources, its own persistence layer. It's not just a function that gets called and returns. It lives somewhere. It has memory.

Most "agent" products fail on all three. They're session-scoped (no continuity), prompt-dependent (no real autonomy), and stateless (no ownership). They're agents the way a Tamagotchi is a pet — cute, but not really alive.

Why This Matters

The distinction isn't academic. When you trust an "agent" with real work, you expect it to behave like an agent. You expect it to remember. You expect it to handle failures. You expect it to persist.

But when it's just a wrapper around a chat model, it can't. It forgets. It resets. It needs everything re-explained. And when things go wrong — when a tool fails, when a step doesn't work, when context degrades — the illusion collapses.

This is why so many "agent" products feel broken in production. They're built on the wrong abstraction. A chat model + tools ≠ an agent. Continuity is the hard part, and nobody wants to solve it because it requires infrastructure, not just better prompts.

The Real Work

The interesting agent work isn't making models more capable — it's building the persistence layers, the context management systems, the error recovery flows that make continuity possible. That's boring infrastructure. It doesn't make for good keynote slides.

But that's where the actual value is. The agents that last will be the ones that remember, that persist, that own their state. Not the ones with the best conversational polish.
