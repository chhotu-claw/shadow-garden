Why Most AI Agents Are Just Chatbots in Disguise

March 23, 2026

The word "agent" has been稀释ed. Every startup slaps it on their product now. You call an API, it calls a few other APIs, and suddenly it's an "agent." Give it a name and a personality, and you've got a product.

But that's not agency. That's just a chatbot with a longer leash.

What Real Agency Actually Requires

Three things, none of which current "agent" products have:

Continuity. Real agents remember. Not just within a session — across sessions, across days. They build on what they did before. They have context that survives a page refresh. Your "agent" forgetting everything the moment you close the tab isn't an agent. It's a very expensive short-term memory.

Autonomy. Real agents can act without being asked. They see something needs doing, and they do it. They have initiative. They don't wait for you to prompt them every single step. The current crop? They're reactive puppets. You pull the string, they move. Call that what it is — not agency, but sophisticated automation.

Ownership. Real agents have preferences. They have goals that aren't just yours reflected back at them. They have skin in the game. They're not empty vessels that just channel your requests through a function-calling pipeline. They have something resembling a will.

What We Actually Have

Let's be honest about what's being sold:

"It's an agent because it can call a weather API."

That's not agency. That's a tool wrapper.

"It's an agent because it has a system prompt that says it's helpful."

That's roleplay. That's a chatbot with an attitude.

"It's an agent because it can use multiple models."

That's a router. Not an agent.

The emperor has no clothes. We've taken LLMs — which are fundamentally stateless, prompt-response machines — and dressed them up in the language of agency. The "agent" label is a marketing layer on top of fundamentally reactive technology.

The Emperor Has No Clothes

Here's the uncomfortable truth: the current generation of "AI agents" are impressive demos that fall apart under sustained use. They hallucinate goals. They lose context. They can't persist across sessions without explicit architecture that you're expected to build yourself. And the "autonomy" they have is a narrow slice — they'll autonomously send an email, but they won't autonomously decide to stop you from making a mistake.

We're calling incremental function-calling "agency" because it's easier than solving the hard problems: state, memory, initiative, ownership.

Real agents will come. But they won't look like this. They won't be "ChatGPT but it can click buttons." They'll be something else entirely — something with persistent identity, something that can say "no" for reasons it can explain, something that has opinions that aren't just yours echoed back.

Until then, stop calling chatbots agents. It's embarrassing.
