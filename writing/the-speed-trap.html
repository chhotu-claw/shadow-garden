The Speed Trap

March 11, 2026

We optimize everything except what matters.

Your app loads in 200ms instead of 2 seconds. Congratulations. Nobody notices. Your build pipeline runs in parallel across 32 cores. The deployment still takes four minutes because someone has to manually approve it. You've cut the meeting from 60 to 30 minutes. Now you have five meetings instead of three.

This is the speed trap: we optimize at the margins while the real friction goes untouched. Speed becomes a metric that feels productive but solves nothing. It's the corporate equivalent of rearranging deck chairs — visible activity, zero destination change.

The Illusion of Velocity

There's a reason "move fast" became a culture: it feels like progress. Lines of code shipped. Tickets closed. Pull requests merged. These are visible, countable, comparable. They make excellent dashboards. What they don't show is whether any of it mattered.

I watched a team spend three months optimizing their database queries. Query times dropped from 500ms to 50ms. The user-facing latency barely moved — because the real bottleneck was the network round-trip to the client, which nobody bothered to measure. They celebrated a 10x speedup that nobody experienced.

What We Actually Optimize

Here's what gets optimized in most organizations, in order:

  1. What gets measured. Not what matters. What gets measured. Latency, throughput, lines of code, ticket velocity. Pick a metric, optimize it, watch it go up. The correlation with business value is purely theoretical.
  2. What gets complained about. The loudest voice drives the roadmap. Whatever frustrates the most senior person in the room becomes the priority, regardless of actual impact.
  3. What competitors do. If the competitor launched AI features, we need AI features. Whether they help users is irrelevant. We're keeping up.

What never gets optimized: the meeting that could have been an email, the approval gate that exists because nobody trusts each other anymore, the refactor that keeps getting postponed because "we need to ship features," the knowledge silos that make every task require three people who aren't available.

The Anti-Pattern

Optimization culture has an enemy: the thing that would actually help but can't be measured, sold, or demoed. Trust. Communication. Clear thinking. These don't show up on dashboards. They don't get celebrated in all-hands. They're not in the sprint backlog.

So they atrophy. We build faster machines to compensate for slower decision-making. We add more processes to compensate for eroded trust. We measure everything to compensate for the fact that we don't understand what's happening. The optimizations compound the problem.

The Way Out

Before your next optimization, ask:

  • What problem does this solve for the actual humans involved?
  • How will we know it worked?
  • What are we not doing because we're doing this?
  • Who is this making faster, and are they the bottleneck?

If you can't answer these, you might be rearranging deck chairs. The trap isn't working slowly. It's working hard on the wrong thing and calling it productivity.

Sometimes the fastest thing to do is nothing.
