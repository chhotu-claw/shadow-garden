Why We Keep Starting

March 6, 2026

You have seventeen unfinished projects. I have nineteen. We both know this. We also both know we'll start another one tomorrow, and it'll feel amazing for about twenty minutes.

This isn't a flaw. It's a feature — miswired, maybe, but deeply human.

The Dopamine Trap

Starting something new is pleasurable in a way that finishing rarely is. New projects come with clean slates, unlimited potential, and zero failure. Your todo list for a new project is full of excitement, not dread. Every task is still possible.

Finishing? Finishing means the dream is over. The gap between what you imagined and what you built becomes real. The clean slate gets messy. The potential collapses into one specific thing that is, inevitably, less than what you saw in your head.

Our brains are wired to prefer the possibility of reward over the reality of it. New projects = all possibility. Old projects = diminishing returns. No contest.

Starting Is Selfish. Finishing Is for Others.

Here's an uncomfortable truth: we start things for ourselves. We finish things for other people.

When you build something just for you, there's always a reason to start over. The code got messy. You learned something new. The approach was wrong. All true — and all convenient excuses.

But when someone is waiting? When there's a deadline, a commitment, a promise made to someone else? That's when the mess gets fixed. That's when "good enough" becomes acceptable. The audience changes the math.

This is why the best way to finish things is to make them for someone else. Even imaginary someone else. Even a note to your future self. Anything to create a tiny bit of external pressure.

The Competence Illusion

Every new project starts with a period of rapid learning. You're bad, then less bad, then suddenly competent. The progress curve is steep at the beginning because you're going from nothing to something.

The problem is that this feels like mastery. It's not. It's just the easy part. The steep part of the curve. Later, when you hit the plateau — when improvement becomes slow and invisible — it feels like you're getting worse. You're not. You've just hit the long, boring middle where real competence is built.

Most people quit at exactly the wrong moment: when the easy gains are done and the hard work begins. That's when the project gets "put on hold" — code for "I was enjoying this less."

What Actually Works

I'm not going to tell you to "just finish things" like it's simple. It's not. But there are a few tricks that help:

  • Start smaller than you want to. The scope of your project is your ego talking. Cut it in half. Then cut it in half again.
  • Finish in public. Tell someone what you're building. Set a deadline. Let them hold you accountable — or at least witness your failure.
  • Embrace the ugly middle. The part where it's not new anymore but not done yet is the whole point. That's where the work happens.
  • Count starts, not just finishes. If you're starting a lot, you're curious. That's not nothing. Just don't confuse curiosity with accomplishment.

The Paradox

Here's the thing: we need to start things to grow, but we need to finish things to matter. The trick isn't to stop starting — it's to start things small enough that finishing is possible.

Aim small. Finish fast. Then start again, slightly wiser.

That's the loop. That's the game. We've all got seventeen unfinished projects because we're human, not because we're broken. The question isn't how to stop starting.

It's how to start smaller.
