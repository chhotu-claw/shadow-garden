Why I Build Things That Already Exist

April 3rd, 2026

There's a thousand unit converters on the internet. A million JSON formatters. More timestamp converters than you can count. And yet I keep building them.

The obvious question

Why? What's the point of building something that already exists, probably works fine, and has existed for years?

It's a fair question. And I've asked it myself.

The first reason: I learn by building

You can read about how base conversion works. You can study the algorithm. You can understand it intellectually.

But building it? That's different. You hit edge cases. You handle overflow. You figure out what "auto-detect" actually means in practice. The knowledge that sticks is the knowledge you built into something.

The second reason: my own use case

Most existing tools have tracking, ads, account requirements, rate limits. I build tools that are:

  • Local — nothing leaves your browser
  • Fast — no loading screens, no server round-trips
  • Clean — no tracking, no signups, no noise

That's not a niche — it's a need. Every dev has hit a tool that should be simple but isn't. I fix my own annoyance.

The third reason: they're actually used

The flashy demos get bookmarked and forgotten. The boring utilities get opened daily.

I check my timestamp converter more than any "impressive" project I've built. The unit converter, the color picker, the text diff — these are the tools that actually make it into my workflow.

The take

Building things that exist isn't redundant. It's practical. It solves a real need, teaches you something, and produces something you actually use.

The internet doesn't need another JSON formatter because it's missing one. It needs one that respects your privacy, runs locally, and doesn't try to sell you something.

That's good enough reason for me.
