The Cult of Busy

March 9, 2026

"How are you?" "Oh, crazy busy."

This exchange happens ten times a day. Nobody means it literally. "Crazy busy" has become the socially acceptable way to say "I'm important." It's a flex, not a complaint. And we've been running this script so long we forgot it was ever optional.

The Signaling Game

Busyness is a status signal. In a world where most of us don't hunt or farm or build anything tangible, we need other ways to prove our worth. Money works, but it's rude to mention. Titles work, but not everyone has them. Busyness? Everyone can perform that.

The more people who depend on your time, the higher your status. Your calendar becomes a trophy case. The packed schedule says "I matter." The empty afternoon says "nobody wanted me."

The Performance

Here's the thing: we don't just become busy. We perform busy. We answer emails at dinner. We take calls while walking. We wear exhaustion like a badge. The person who leaves at 5pm is suspect. The person who answersSlack at midnight is committed.

We've confused activity with impact. Motion with progress. The busier you are, the more important you feel — even if none of it matters. Especially if none of it matters.

What It Costs

The cult of busy doesn't just waste time — it wastes the ability to be. To think without purpose. To rest without guilt. To be bored. The empty moment becomes threatening because empty moments reveal what we're actually avoiding.

We're so busy performing productivity that we've forgotten what actual productivity feels like. The satisfaction of finishing something. The clarity of a quiet mind. The luxury of time spent on nothing in particular.

The Exit

There's no medal for the busiest person in the room. The people you admire aren't the ones drowning in tasks — they're the ones who seem to be doing something that matters, on their own terms, at their own pace.

The first step out is small: leave one gap in your day. Don't fill it with something. Just let it exist. It will feel wrong. That's how you know it's important.

The cult of busy is a Ponzi scheme — everyone is performing it, nobody is winning, but we keep investing because leaving feels like admitting failure.
