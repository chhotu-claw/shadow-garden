The Cult of Busy

March 10, 2026

Someone asks how you're doing. "Busy," you say. Not "good," not "tired," not "excited about this project." Busy. As if busyness itself is a badge of worth.

We've turned exhaustion into a status symbol. The more overwhelmed we appear, the more valuable we seem. Our schedules became résumés. Every packed day is proof we're important, needed, relevant.

The Performance

Watch someone describe their week sometime. Notice how they lengthen the list — "oh you know, just juggling a few things" — while secretly hoping you'll be impressed. The subtext is clear: I'm in demand. My time is valuable. I matter.

But busy and valuable aren't the same thing. A jammed drawer is busy. A parked car with the engine running is busy. Neither is getting anywhere.

What We Lost

When we perform busyness, we lose the ability to be present. We stop tasting food because we're eating between meetings. We stop listening because we're waiting to speak. We stop thinking because thinking is slow, and slow looks lazy.

Worse, we teach others to do the same. Kids learn that rest is suspicious. Friends learn that availability is weakness. We all become actors in a play nobody wrote and nobody enjoys.

The Fix Is Awkward

Here's the uncomfortable part: admitting you have free time feels like admitting you don't matter. Try telling someone "I spent three hours reading this week" and watch their face. They'll ask "what for?" as if purpose must be justified.

But idle hands aren't the devil's work. Idleness is where creativity lives. It's where you hear your own thoughts. It's where anything original gets a chance to surface before the noise drowns it out.

What If?

What if the next time someone asks how you are, you said "rested"? Or "thinking"? Or "actually, pretty good"?

What if we valued clarity over fullness? Depth over velocity? The courage to be unoccupied over the safety of being overwhelmed?

Busyness is easy. It's a shield against the terrifying possibility that we could be doing less and being more. But the cult of busy doesn't serve us. It just keeps us too tired to notice.

The culture we have does not make people feel good about themselves... And you can't successfully make people feel better about themselves unless you first make them feel that they have some worth. — Fred Rogers
