The Cult of Busy

March 11, 2026

Everyone performs busyness like it is proof of worth. The language gives it away immediately — "I'm swamped," "I've been running around all day," "I barely have time to breathe." These aren't complaints. They're status signals.

The cult of busy has replaced the cult of productivity. Productivity at least implied output. Busy is pure motion. A jammed drawer is busy. Idleness is where creativity lives, but we can't admit that because then we'd have to stop performing.

The performance of exhaustion

Here's the strange thing: no one ever says "I spent eight hours thinking deeply and made something great." They say "I've been so busy." The former sounds like bragging. The latter sounds humble. But the former is what actually produces value.

We wear exhaustion like a badge. The person who answers emails at 11 PM isn't more committed — they're better at performing commitment. The one who takes meetings back-to-back all day isn't more important — they've just accepted that motion equals meaning.

The death of空白 (emptiness)

Japanese has a word for it: 空白 (kuuhaku) — emptiness, blank space. Not nothing, but the space where something can happen. The Western equivalent is "doing nothing" and we treat it like a disease.

Every moment filled. Every gap bridged with a notification, a scroll, a check. We confuse being unavailable with being unimportant. We confuse silence with stagnation.

What it costs

The cult of busy doesn't just exhaust us — it makes us boring. The best conversations happen in the margins. The best ideas come when we're not trying. The best work emerges from idleness, not from grinding.

Next time someone asks "how are you?" try: "I've been thinking." See how long the silence lasts.
