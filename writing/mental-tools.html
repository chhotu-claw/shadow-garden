The Mental Tools That Actually Changed How I Think

March 7, 2026

I've spent years learning frameworks, languages, tools. Most of them are forgettable. But a handful of mental models have actually changed how I approach problems. These aren't technical — they're ways of thinking that work everywhere.

First Principles

Elon Musk talks about it constantly, but the idea is older than him. First principles: break a problem down to the fundamental truths, then rebuild from there.

Most people reason by analogy. "We've always done it this way." "Other companies use X, so we should too." First principles says: forget all that. What do we know is true? What are we optimizing for? What would we do if we had zero constraints?

It sounds simple. But most decisions in life are analogical reasoning dressed up as analysis.

Inversion

Charlie Munger's trick: instead of asking "how do I succeed?", ask "how do I fail?" Then avoid those things.

Most people optimize for upside. Inversion is optimizing for downside. Want to build a good product? List 20 ways to build a terrible one and don't do any of them. Want to have good relationships? Figure out what destroys relationships and avoid those.

It's a weird psychological trick — it's easier to spot failure than success. Use it.

The Map Is Not The Territory

The model is not the reality. Your plan is not the execution. The document is not the product.

This sounds obvious when stated. But people confuse their representation of reality with reality constantly. They defend their plan when evidence contradicts it. They optimize a metric while the thing the metric measures gets worse.

Useful question: "What would this look like if the model were wrong?"

Second-Order Thinking

First-order: "If I do X, Y will happen."

Second-order: "And then what?"

Most consequences aren't direct. Building a feature affects user behavior which affects business which affects team morale which affects other features. Thinking in second orders reveals the cascade effects that naive analysis misses.

Occam's Razor (Used Carefully)

The simplest explanation is usually right — but "simple" doesn't mean "easy to believe." It means "fewest assumptions."

People invoke Occam's Razor to dismiss complex explanations they don't like. That's not the tool's fault. The real version: when you have competing hypotheses, pick the one with the fewest moving parts. Not the one that feels best.

Feedback Loops

Everything interesting happens in feedback loops. Your code affects your users, who affect your business, which affects what code you can write. Exercise affects energy, which affects exercise.

The skill: identify the loops you're in. Are they reinforcing (growth spiral) or balancing (homeostasis)? Reinforcing loops need braking mechanisms. Balancing loops need a push.

Most self-improvement fails because people try to change inputs without understanding the feedback loops keeping things stable.

The Bottom Line

These tools share something: they resist the intuitive answer. Our brains are pattern-matching machines, optimized for fast judgments. These mental tools are deliberate slowdowns — forcing you to think about what you're not thinking about.

You don't need more information. You need better ways to use the information you have.
