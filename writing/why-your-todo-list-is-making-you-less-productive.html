Why Your Todo List Is Making You Less Productive

March 7, 2026

You open your notes app. You see seven tasks. Your brain immediately starts prioritizing, estimating time, worrying about what won't get done. Before you've done anything productive, you've already spent mental energy on the overhead of productivity itself.

This is the paradox of the todo list: it feels like organization, but it's often the thing keeping you from doing meaningful work.

The False Urgency Problem

A todo list transforms everything into a deadline. Every item carries equal psychological weight — "write a report" sits next to "buy toothpaste" in your mental load, even though one matters and one doesn't. Your brain can't distinguish between important and urgent on a todo list; it just sees a pile of stuff that needs doing.

This is why people feel "busy" without being productive. The list creates the feeling of work. Checking things off releases dopamine. But checking off isn't the same as making progress.

The Serendipity Killer

Here's what your todo list doesn't account for: the 3-hour deep dive into something random that turns into your best idea of the year. The conversation that goes nowhere except into a train of thought that solves a problem you'd been stuck on. The weird article you click because you're procrastinating that changes how you think about something.

Todo lists optimize for execution. But the best work — the original, creative, valuable work — comes from exploration, not execution. When you're focused on doing, you stop wandering. And wandering is where the interesting stuff lives.

The Completion Illusion

Nothing feels better than finishing a todo list. Except — have you ever noticed how a finished list gets replaced immediately? The moment you clear it, you start adding more. The goalposts move. The finish line recedes.

This creates a treadmill effect. You're always working, never arrived. The todo list becomes an endless stream of "now this, then this, then this" with no destination in mind. You're so focused on the queue that you forget to ask whether the queue matters.

What Actually Works

I'm not saying don't write things down. Memory is fallible; notes are useful. But there's a difference between:

  • A capture tool — "don't forget to email Bob about the thing"
  • A todo list — "today I will do X, Y, Z"

The first is a memory externalizer. The second is a productivity theater.

Better approaches:

  • One thing at a time. Not a list of five. One. What's the most important thing? Do that until it's done or you're stuck. Then pick the next one. No list required.
  • Time blocks, not tasks. "Morning block for deep work" is vaguer than a todo list, but it leaves room for what matters most to emerge.
  • The 3-3-3 method. Three hours of focused work, three tasks for the day, three outcomes you want. That's enough structure without the prison of a list.

The Radical Alternative

Try this: tomorrow, don't make a todo list. Instead, at the end of the day, write down what you actually accomplished. Not what you planned — what actually happened.

I'll bet you two things. First, you got more done than you thought. Second, some of your best work came from directions you didn't anticipate.

The todo list assumes you know what matters. Usually, you don't. You know what feels urgent. Those are rarely the same thing.
