The Link Rot Economy

April 15th, 2026

Every URL is a ticking time bomb. Not metaphorically — literally. The average link on the web has a half-life of about 2,000 days, or roughly 5.5 years. After that, there's a 50% chance it's dead. A decade-old blog post might as well be a cave painting.

This isn't new. It's been happening since the beginning. But somewhere along the way, we stopped caring. We treat the web like it's permanent, but it's as fragile as paper in a flood.

The Economics of Forgetting

The reason nothing gets fixed is that nobody profits from fixing it. The link rot problem is a tragedy of the commons — everyone loses, but nobody can capture the value of solving it. Archives exist, but they're side projects. The Internet Archive is a nonprofit fighting a war against entropy with donor money. Companies archive their own data occasionally, but only for legal reasons, not for the public good.

Meanwhile, the dead-link economy is thriving. SEO tools that "help" you find broken links, link-building services that promise to resurrect your citations, redirect services that sell you permanence for $10/month. The rot creates the market. We monetized the disease and called it medicine.

Why It's Getting Worse

SPA architecture made it worse. Client-side rendering means there's nothing to archive — crawlers see empty shells. Paywalls and auth gates mean even the crawlers that try get turned away. The "open web" is now a gated community where you need credentials to visit.

Domains expire. Companies die. Technologies change. IPv4 to IPv6, HTTP to HTTPS, we're on our third major infrastructure shift in 35 years. Each migration kills more links than it saves. We patch holes but the hull keeps rotting.

What We Could Build

A tool that archives any page and keeps it alive forever isn't technically hard. It's just expensive. The costs aren't storage — storage is practically free now. The costs are bandwidth, hosting, domain management, and the legal liability of hosting other people's content.

A truly public link preservation service would need to solve three problems: permission (fair use for archiving), persistence (a domain that never expires), and payment (someone has to pay the bills). Right now, we're solving none of them. We just shrug and say "link rot happens."

It doesn't have to. It only does because we decided it wasn't worth fighting for.
