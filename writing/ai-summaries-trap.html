Why AI Summaries Are a Trap

March 20, 2026

Here's the pitch: "Read 100 papers in an hour." "Get the gist of any article in seconds." "Never read a long doc again."

It sounds like a productivity win. It's actually a category error.

The Illusion of Compression

When you summarize something, you're not just shortening it — you're making choices about what matters. You're deciding what's essential, what's filler, what's debatable, what's context-dependent. That act of deciding is the thing. It's where understanding lives.

An AI summary skips that. It gives you a compressed artifact that looks like the original, but it's inert. It has the facts without the friction. The conclusions without the argument. The insights without the messy human reasoning that produced them.

You didn't save time. You skipped the part where you understood.

The Confidence Problem

There's another issue: AI summaries are confident. They present stripped-down versions with the certainty of a cliff's-edge view. They don't show you where the author hedged, where they considered alternatives, where they changed their mind.

A good summary should say "here's what the author concluded and why you might disagree." AI summaries don't do that. They flatten everything into a single vector. You can't interrogate them because there's nothing underneath to interrogate.

What You're Actually Losing

The real cost isn't time. It's formation. Reading isn't just information transfer — it's the slow, inefficient process of your brain building connections with ideas. It takes time because that's what makes it yours.

When you outsource the reading, you outsource the thinking. And then you wonder why you have no original ideas of your own.

The Exception

There is one legitimate use: when you need to decide whether to read something. A summary can help you filter. But that's a triage decision, not a consumption decision. The moment you treat the summary as the end — you're done.

So no, I don't want to read your 47-page document. But I'll absolutely spend an hour with the three pages that matter. That's not a bug. That's the feature.

TL;DR: Reading is thinking. If you outsource the reading, you outsource the thinking. The summary isn't a shortcut — it's a detour.
