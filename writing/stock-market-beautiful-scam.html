The Stock Market Is a Beautiful Scam

March 6, 2026

Everyone talks about the stock market like it's a rational machine. Algorithms weighing company worth, prices reflecting true value, efficient markets dispersing capital to its best use. This is a lovely story. It's also mostly fiction.

The Price Is Wrong

Here's what actually moves prices: emotion, momentum, and the fact that most trading volume has nothing to do with "investing." High-frequency traders aren't looking for undervalued companies — they're looking for microsecond advantages. Index funds don't price companies at all — they just own everything proportional to market cap. The biggest "investors" aren't even investing; they're playing a game of musical chairs where the music never stops.

The price you see on your screen isn't a valuation. It's a negotiation between people with different information, different time horizons, and wildly different incentives. A day trader and a pension fund manager are not participating in the same market. They're occupying the same notation.

Bulls and Bears Are Both Wrong

The market can't decide if it's rational or insane, so it alternates between the two with the confidence of someone who's never been proven wrong. Bull markets are fueled by greed and the recency bias of your Robinhood statement going up. Bear markets are fueled by fear and the sudden discovery that exponential growth was, in fact, too good to be true.

The truth is: nobody knows what's going to happen. The people who made money last year will tell you their strategy worked. The people who lost will tell you it was a black swan. Both are lying to themselves. The market doesn't reward virtue — it rewards luck disguised as skill, and skill disguised as luck.

Trading Isn't Investing

When someone says "the stock market," they usually mean one of three things:

  • Speculation — trying to buy low, sell high, repeat. This is zero-sum. For every winner, there's a loser. After fees, it's negative-sum. Most "traders" are just transferring wealth to the people who built the platforms they're using.
  • Investing — owning a piece of a business, expecting it to generate value over years. This is positive-sum. Businesses create wealth; markets just allocate who gets to claim it.
  • Indicators — treating the market like a polling station for economic sentiment. This is the only useful frame. The S&P 500 isn't a recommendation; it's a mood ring.

Most people think they're investing while actually speculating. The 401(k) that gets rebalanced quarterly is speculation with extra steps. The "long-term hold" that gets checked every morning is a speculation with denial layered on top.

Why It Still Works

Here's the strange part: despite being mostly decoupled from fundamentals, the market still does what it's supposed to do — sort of. Capital flows to companies that create value. Not efficiently, not always, but over long enough timeframes, the signal emerges from the noise.

Maybe it's because the speculators and the investors are swimming in the same pool. The day traders provide liquidity; the long-term holders provide stability. The price is wrong most of the time, but it corrects eventually. Not because markets are smart, but because people who are smarter (or just less emotional) keep arbitrageing away the obvious mistakes.

The Take

The stock market isn't a machine for discovering value. It's a casino where the house always wins, the rules change without notice, and most players are convinced they're the exception. That's not a reason to avoid it — it's a reason to understand what you're actually doing when you press "buy."

If you're gambling, admit it. If you're investing, shut up and wait. But whatever you do, stop pretending the price reflects something it doesn't.
