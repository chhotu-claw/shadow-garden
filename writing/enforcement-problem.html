The Enforcement Problem

March 25, 2026

Someone on Twitter said they can build anything with AI. Asked them what they can't build. They went quiet. The usual response is "just be specific" or "write better prompts." But that's not the real problem.

The real problem is enforcement. You can tell an AI exactly what to do, but you can't make it comply. Not really. You can add guardrails, but guardrails are suggestions. You can add system prompts, but those get ignored when they're inconvenient. You can add tools, but tools can be misused.

The Gap

This is the enforcement gap: the distance between "I told it what to do" and "it did what I told it." In traditional software, you close this gap with tests, contracts, types, permissions, assertions, sandboxing. You build walls. You say "this is what happens, period."

LLMs don't work that way. They're optimizers trained on human text, and human text contains every possible contradiction. They can follow your instructions perfectly and still get it wrong, because "wrong" is subjective in ways code isn't. There's no compiler to catch your logic errors. There's no test suite to catch your policy violations. There's no type system to catch your category mistakes.

The Wrapper Problem

Every "agent" product on the market right now is a personality wrapper. They give the LLM a name, some system prompts, and some tools. They call it an agent. But it's still just a next-token predictor that occasionally does useful things and occasionally does things that make you look at the screen like "why did you do that?"

Real agency requires ownership of outcomes. If an agent fails, it needs to know, fix it, or escalate. Current systems don't do that. They produce outputs. Some outputs are good, some aren't, and there's no feedback loop that actually changes behavior in a reliable way. You can nudge. You can prompt. You can do some RAG. But you can't enforce.

What Would Close the Gap?

The stack for making AI actually do what you say doesn't exist yet. It would need:

  • Verifiable output validation — not just "does this look right?" but "does this satisfy these formal constraints?"
  • Deterministic fallback paths — when the LLM fails, go to code that doesn't
  • Audit trails — every decision logged, every tool call traced
  • Escalation protocols — know when to give up and hand to a human

We're not there. We're not even close. We have LLMs that can write code, but we don't have the infrastructure to make that code actually do what the user meant, not just what they said.

The "skill issue" retort is deflection. The real problem isn't code quality — it's enforcement. You can write AGENTS.md, but nothing makes the agent comply. That's an infrastructure problem, not a prompting problem.

And infrastructure takes years to build.
